Meat and offal usage

From a financial point of view meat and offal forms the major food expenditure in most catering businesses. It is imperative that the chef is aware of yields, portion control and waste minimisation.

A blend of first and second class cuts should be used to minimise the amount of wastage and maximise the amount of profit from a animal carcass.

Meat and offal dishes – key recipe points

  • Ensure that you follow the instructions of the recipe; weigh off all the ingredients and quantities, and adhere to the steps in the method.
  • Be aware that the final product is only as good as the ingredients that you use, so check the quality and freshness.
  • Use the correct process and cookery method to get the best outcome. The cookery method used is there for a reason.
  • Finish the recipe correctly, for example thicken the sauce, adjust the seasoning and plate correctly.

Preservation of meat and offal

To prevent waste and minimise losses, many different preservation methods are available for the chef:

  • Chilling – store food items in the fridge or coolroom following hygiene principles.
  • Freezing – extends lifespan 1–6 months. Items must be wrapped properly to prevent freezer burn.
  • Vacuum packing – removes air and prevents oxygen coming into contact with the items.
  • MAP – modified atmosphere packaging is used for portion-cut items in trays. The oxygen is replaced by a mixture of gases restricting bacteria growth.
  • Drying – air drying is popular for whole slated items such as a leg of pork for parma ham. Strips of meat can also be dried for jerky.

Preservation of meat and offal

  • Canning – used to cook the items and sealing them in for extensive storage. Used for spreads such as beef and game paste.
  • Smoking – either cold or hot smoking is used. The item is normally salted and then hung in the smoke to kill off further bacteria on the outside and to impart the flavour.
  • Salting – salt kills bacteria and draws out excess moisture. Used in dry salting and for brines, common process for ham, salami and terrines.

Tools and equipment

  • When preparing and cooking meat and offal dishes a range of knives are used. The most common are boning, French, paring and palette knives. Sharpening equipment is also essential.
  • A range of pans, trays and bowls are required depending on the recipe.
  • Small tools and equipment include ladles, whisks, chinois, perforated spoons and gastronorm containers.
  • Large equipment utilised includes stoves, ovens, steamers, grills and salamanders, deep fryers, sous vide cookers and meat slicers. Fridges, freezers and coolrooms are essential for storage.

Using tools and equipment

  • Ensure that you are aware of the standard operating procedures (SOP)
  • When using equipment ensure that the electricity is switched off and disconnected when cleaning, assembling or disassembling machinery.
  • Be very careful when using machinery with moving parts – you could be severely injured!
  • It is a legal requirement to use pushers or guards that come with equipment such as slicers, mandolines, food processors and juicers during operation.

Planning

  • When preparing and cooking meat and offal dishes a range of preparation and finishing tasks are required. As you have to multi-task it is important to plan out the day. Preparation lists and mise en place tasks should be set up. Then prioritise the tasks based on the length of time they take or their impact on further steps.
  • If you prepare a range of items, always prepare the one that will take the longest to cook first to ensure sufficient time for it to finish before service.
  • Work methodically and keep your work station clear.
  • Check everything before service to make sure you are ready to go.

Planning

  • Minimise pressure during service by focusing on the task and communicating with your team.
  • Be efficient but do not panic – this only exacerbates mistakes.
  • Debrief after service to make sure any problems are dealt with.
  • Clean up and get ready for the next shift.
  • Remember – good mise en place is good workflow and less stress!

Hygiene

The following measures will support correct hygiene:

  • Prepare meat and offal in one designated area of the kitchen. Ensure that all workbenches, trays and storage containers are clean and sanitised.
  • Clean and sanitise your knives and boards before commencing work as they can become contaminated with bacteria from raw food.
  • Prevent cross-contamination during preparation and storage. If hands and utensils are not carefully washed and are then used to prepare cooked food, the cooked food can become contaminated from the raw food. Prevent dripping of liquid from raw foods by storing food in clean, non-toxic, washable containers or cover with foil or plastic, clearly labelled. If it is not possible to store raw meat and offal separately, store on lower shelves below cooked foods.

Hygiene

  • Use colour-coded boards for prepared and finished products and never cut cooked items on boards that have been used previously for the cutting of raw meat and offal items – unless the board has been properly washed in between tasks.
  • Ensure storage temperatures below 4°C for raw meat and offal, and cooking above 70°C.
  • Minced meat and offal meat should be processed on the same day they are purchased and should always be cooked through. The increased surface area means mince goes off much more quickly.
  • Follow personal hygiene and wear clean uniform for hygiene and protection.

Storage

Storage of prepared ingredients for meat and offal dishes is vital for the safety of your customer. Food safety and hygiene regulations must be complied with at all times. Things to be aware of:

  • Temperature
  • Date labelling
  • Coverings
  • Positioning
  • Stock rotation.

Storage – fresh meat and offal

  • To maintain quality, always store vacuum-packed meat with the fat side up as it prevents discolouration of the fat and reduces weep.
  • Do not drop packs since this increases weep.
  • Store at 1–4°C to reduce bacterial growth.
  • Once meat has been purchased, it must be prepared according to the hygiene rules.
  • All raw meat must be stored in suitable containers on the bottom shelf of the fridge or coolroom.

Storage – frozen meat and offal

  • Store in the freezer at –18°C to –24°C.
  • Individually wrap items for ease of separation when defrosting.
  • Spread items out on a tray to speed up the freezing process. Once the items are frozen they may then be bulk packed.
  • Label and date all meat items placed in frozen storage and regularly rotate the stock.
  • Frozen meat and offal is best used within 3–6 months.
  • Never re-freeze meat after it has been thawed.

Temperature

The following needs to be considered for all meat and offal dishes:

  • Ideally used fresh daily. If not it must be:
    • chilled to below 5°C in 90 minutes
    • stored labelled in a fridge
    • –18°C or below if freezing.
  • If cooked meat and offal dishes are stored in a freezer, the meat must be reheated to legally required temperature.
  • Meat and offal dishes must not be reheated more than once, especially when they contain pork products.
  • Hot holding must be above 63°C.

Date labelling and coverings

When labelling any meat and offal dishes, the following guidelines should be followed:

  • Name of product
  • Date of production
  • ‘Use by’ date.

Ideally any meat and offal dishes being stored should be in an airtight container. However, other coverings include:

  • clingfilm
  • greaseproof paper.

Positioning and stock rotation

When storing any meat and offal dishes you must always consider the possibility of cross-contamination.

  • All meat and offal dishes not for immediate use should be:
    • covered to prevent cross-contamination
    • stored in a fridge or freezer.
  • Raw meat should be stored on the bottom shelf of the fridge to prevent spillage.
  • Cooked meat should be stored on the top shelf of the fridge.
  • Labelled to ensure stock rotation – ‘first in first out’.
