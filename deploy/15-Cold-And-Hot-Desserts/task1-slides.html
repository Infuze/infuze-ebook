Introduction to desserts and puddings

Providing a wide range of hot dessert products caters to the preferences of different customers. Hot desserts are popular throughout the year and especially during the cooler seasons.

Note that the term ‘pudding’ is used widely to refer to a dessert but is also a term used to refer to a variety of savoury items that are mixed and baked, for example Yorkshire pudding or suet pudding.

Types of desserts

Types of desserts include the following:

  • Pastry-based desserts
  • Cakes and tortes
  • Batter- and egg-based desserts
  • Custard and cream desserts
  • Fruit-based desserts
  • Frozen desserts
  • Rice and farinaceous desserts and puddings
  • Chocolate-based desserts.

All of these major categories include hot recipes. It is important to focus on the final product and its desired flavour, texture, appearance and colour as well as consistency.

Pastry-based desserts

Yeast pastry

This pastry needs to be made fresh every day as it goes stale fairly quickly. These pastries are usually eaten in the morning, for example croissants, or as part as an afternoon snack or break like cinnamon buns rather than as a straight dessert item.

Strudel pastry

Strudel is an Austrian dessert made of thin pastry rolled up round a fruit filling and baked. The dough needs to be well stretched and relaxed.

Pastry-based desserts

Puff pastry

The light and flaky texture of puff pastry is used in apple turnovers or mille feuilles. Various types can be produced for different menu purposes. This type of pastry puffs up when cooked due to the alternating layers of fat and dough created during production – this is called ‘lamination’.

Cake mixtures

A basic cake mixture is often steamed or baked in the oven. The mixtures usually contain baking powder or a high percentage of eggs to aid lift and are used in desserts such as a Victoria sponge.

Batter- and egg-based desserts

These include crêpes, soufflés pancakes and their various ethnic interpretations such as palatschinken.

The category also includes omelettes and sweets finished with a batter, such as fritters and waffles.

Custard- and cream-based desserts

This type of dessert include:

  • egg custard tart
  • crème brûlée
  • panna cotta
  • fruit fools
  • mousse
  • bavarois.

Fruit-based desserts

All fruit-based desserts have fruit as their main ingredient. The fruit can be prepared in different ways to suit the needs of the dish:

  • Stewing is used for harder fruit such as rhubarb, quinces and apples. The stewed fruit can be made into compotes or puréed and used for the traditional English fools.
  • Poaching fresh fruit allows us to highlight some of the flavours with the careful use of spices. Some examples would be poached tamarillo, pear, peach or apple. The poaching liquor used provides flavour or colour such as for pears in red wine sauce.
  • Fruit compote and preserved fruit enable us to use fruit when in season and preserve it for menu choices all year around.
  • Fruit purée is used in coulis, as part of ices, or bavarois.

Puddings

Puddings on the menu generally refer to a steamed, boiled or baked mixture. This can be based on a batter, rice, semolina, tapioca, etc.

These are filling and are often accompanied by a sauce. Typical examples:

  • Queen of puddings
  • Steamed puddings:
    • Spotted dick
    • Chocolate steamed pudding
    • Fruit sponge pudding.

Puddings

Queen of puddings:

  • Based on breadcrumbs and milk set with eggs
  • Top with jam and meringue
  • Grill until browned on the top.

Steamed puddings:

  • Make a basic batter and mix with dried fruit/chocolate etc.
  • Spoon or pipe the mixture into greased and sugared moulds and steam
  • Serve with apricot, custard, chocolate, syrup or vanilla sauce.

Commodities used in desserts and puddings

Desserts and puddings generally use a limited number of ingredients. However, a successful product depends on the accuracy of the weighing, the execution of the preparation and the final presentation.

This is really important to keep in mind as chefs often cook by feel. But desserts and puddings require an exact process and care must be taken to weigh everything correctly and follow the correct process. This also ensures consistency across different days and different chefs.

Quality of ingredients

Quality ingredients lead to a quality product.

Ensure you use the correct ingredients in the right amounts and follow the preparation steps to achieve a consistent quality outcome.

Utilise the correct cookery methods to ensure a quality end product.

Commodities

It is important to be aware of the basic commodities used in desserts and puddings. This awareness will enable you to create and judge a quality product.

The ratio of the basic ingredients used in baking influences the appearance and taste of the finished product. Commodities used include:

  • flour
  • fat
  • sugar
  • creams
  • rice
  • fruits.

Flour

Flour is produced in many forms and which will provide different outcomes when used in making desserts and puddings. The table provides some examples of the different types of flours available:

Weak (soft) flour ~9% protein Used for sponges or shortbread biscuits in which eggs provide additional structure
Medium flour ~11% protein Used for yeast goods, for example Danish pastries
Strong (hard) flour >12% protein Used in bread and puff pastry
High protein ~15% protein Used for pasta and high protein bread – durum wheat added to flour for this purpose

In most kitchens, baker’s flour is purchased. It can be weakened by the addition of cornflour or wheat starch or it can be hardened by the addition of semolina, durum wheat or dry gluten. By adding baking powder the flour is changed to self-raising flour.

Fat

Fats and oils are used in pastry as well as for frying of pastry products. As the fat prevents the gluten from forming long strands it is also referred to as shortening.

  • Shortening is made from vegetable oil which has been hydrogenated to make is solid. Virtually tasteless, it provides a baked dessert which is flaky and light.
  • Butter this is the best fat to use in baking. Due to its superior flavour it is ideal for shortcrust paste, Genoise and biscuits. It needs to be used with care due to its low melting point.

Sugar

Sugar is produced from sugar cane or sugar beet and is a carbohydrate. In cooking, sugar is used:

  • as a sweet seasoning agent or for conserving fruit in the production of jam – it prevents the growth of microorganisms after the fruit has been boiled
  • to increase tenderness and provide softer texture to the product
  • to promote browning and crust development
  • to retain moisture which adds to the texture of the finished product
  • to assist in the absorption of air when beating egg white or Genoise mixture
  • for garnishing such as sprinkling product with icing sugar, spun or blown sugar.

Creams

Cream is used for fillings such as crème Chantilly, as an addition to ice cream and for parfait bases. There are many types of creams used in making and finishing desserts and puddings:

  • Double cream – minimum fat content of 48%
  • Whipping cream – minimum fat content of 35%
  • Thickened cream – addition of gelatine makes for easier whipping
  • Crème fraîche – double cream ‘soured’ with the addition of yoghurt
  • Light cream – fat content of ~20%
  • Sour cream – contains 15% fat content and lactic acid
  • Imitation cream – made from vegetable fats. It requires no refrigeration but has no taste or flavour.

Rice

Various types of rice can be used in puddings:

  • Patna
  • Long grain/white rice
  • Short grain/rounded
  • Brown grain

Fruits

Fruits are used to flavour, decorate and provide texture to many desserts and puddings. Fruits are used according to their texture and their resistance to heat/cooking. Seasonality should be taken into consideration when planning dessert menus which include fruit. Fruits used in desserts and puddings include:

  • citrus fruits
  • dried fruits
  • candied fruits
  • poached fruits
  • apples
  • bananas
  • berries.

Flavourings, colourings and essences

Flavourings, colourings and essences all contribute to the final flavour or presentation of the dessert or pudding. For example:

  • Essences
    • Vanilla
    • Brandy
    • Liquorice
  • Flavourings
    • Orange
    • Chocolate
    • Lemon
  • Colouring
    • Yellow
    • Red
    • Blue.
