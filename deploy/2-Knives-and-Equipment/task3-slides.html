Introduction to large kitchen equipment

As a chef working in a professional kitchen, you will use a number of large pieces of equipment. The equipment you use will depend on the size and nature of the establishment.

It is important to use equipment that is of good quality and best fits your needs. You need a basic knowledge of how it operates; it is also beneficial to be familiar with the various models available. The information below should be helpful in selecting and maintaining your large equipment.

It is important when using large equipment that you are fully trained and fully understand how to use it safely.

  • Cold holding equipment (refrigerators and refrigerated displays, freezers)
    • A fridge should operate at a temperature range of 1 to 5°C. Its primary use is for the storage of food and beverages with the intent to extend freshness of the product.
    • A freezer should operate at a temperature range of -18 to -22°C. Its primary use is for the storage of food and beverages, with the intent of preserving the product.

Types and characteristics of large equipment

  • Deep fat fryers: A deep fat fryer is used to heat cooking oils so foods can be completely covered in hot oil to be cooked.
    • Most professional deep fat fryers have one or two removable frying baskets.
    • Most deep fat fryers will have containers and draining systems, making the removal of the hot oil safer and easier after use.
    • It is important when using deep fat fryers that you are able to control the temperature and allow for different settings for various types of food. Temperature controls may have ranges that begin at around 150°C and almost reach 190°C or more at the upper end.

Types and characteristics of large equipment

  • Grills: There are different types of grills. These fall largely into three categories: salamander, open flame grill/barbecue, contact grill. Grilling is an ideal method for cooking small cuts of meat, fish and vegetables.
    • Salamander: direct dry heat source at the top of the equipment, often used to finish and add colour to a dish
    • Open flame grill/barbecue: direct dry heat source at the bottom of the equipment. Gives a good smoking flame-grilled flavour to the food
    • Contact grill: direct dry heat sources at the top and bottom of the equipment; can be used for toasting paninis, etc.

Types and characteristics of large equipment

  • Hobs (induction, solid top, open range)
    • Induction: An induction hob uses electromagnetism to create a magnetic field between the pan and the hob. Induction hobs are quicker and cheaper to run because the heat is transferred directly to the pan.
    • Solid top: Solid tops are usually either powered by gas or electricity and heat a solid plate which distributes the heat. These can take a while to heat up and are less controllable than gas open-range hobs.
    • Open range: Open-range hobs are powered by gas; they provide instant, direct and controllable heat.

Types and characteristics of large equipment

  • Hot holding equipment (bain marie, hotplate): There are a variety of different ways to hold hot food at the correct temperature. It is important to hold food at the correct temperature to ensure that you comply with legislation and reduce the risk of food poisoning.
    • Hot holding equipment will have a heat source from the bottom and usually a heat source from the top, in the form of heat lamps.
    • It is usually located near the front of the kitchen, commonly known as the pass, where the chefs will hold and then plate up dishes for the customer.
    • Hot plates or bain maries can be used to serve food directly to customers, ie at buffets, canteens, deli counters or fast food restaurants.

Types and characteristics of large equipment

  • Ovens
    • Conventional, fan assisted (convection): A type of oven that uses convection currents to cook food. Fans are used to distribute hot air around the oven, which helps to cook the food more evenly and quickly than in a conventional gas-style oven. Usually contains two heating elements; others contain a heating element which is built around the fan so the air flows to a specific temperature level, keeping the air not only circulating but heating uniformly as well.
    • Combination (steam/dry heat), microwave): A combination-style oven allows a mixture of dry and wet heat. This is useful usually when using dough products or when the kitchen has limited space to have both a steamer and a oven.

Characteristics of different types of large equipment

When selecting or replacing new equipment, you need to consider the following:

  • Power source/fuel
  • Size of equipment
  • Usage of equipment
  • Ease of use
  • Ease of cleaning
  • Space, layout and kitchen design

  • Post-sale warranty and service plan
  • Serving and replacing
  • Ventilation requirements

Maintaining large equipment

Regulations require that all work equipment be maintained in an efficient state, in efficient order and in good repair, and that maintenance operations on work equipment can be carried out safely.

Without this information, the chef may not understand how to operate or clean the equipment.
  • Planned servicing, maintenance and authorised repairs: Often referred to as Planned Preventative Maintenance (PPM). All equipment in a professional catering kitchen should be serviced at least once a year and some equipment more often, according to the manufacturerâs guidelines. Maintenance work should only be undertaken by those who are competent to do the work and who have been provided with sufficient information, instruction and training.

Methods of maintaining large equipment

  • Cleaning: A spotless and clean professional kitchen is extremely important for health and food safety reasons. The best approach to cleaning large equipment in a professional kitchen is to divide the job into a series of smaller tasks. Cleaning should be undertaken on a regular basis; this will help to prolong the life of the kitchen equipment and prevent injury or damage to the kitchen.
  • Monitoring temperatures: It is important that the temperatures of certain kitchen equipment such as fridges, freezers and hot and cold holding equipment are monitored on a regular basis. This will prevent damage to the equipment and food spoilage occurring. Fridges and freezers should ideally be checked around three times a day to ensure that they are operating at the correct temperature.
  • Reporting problems or damage: Any problems or damage should be reported to your supervisor immediately. Do not attempt to fix or use the equipment if it is damaged, as it can lead to injuries.
