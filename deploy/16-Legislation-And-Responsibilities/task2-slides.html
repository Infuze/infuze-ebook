Introduction to temperature controls

As part of a food safety management plan, it is essential to consider temperatures as a major control in food safety. Food poisoning bacteria multiply at an optimal temperature of 37°C. Controlling the temperature at all stages of the process is key to providing safe food.

An effective food safety management plan will consider the following:

  • The importance of temperature controls
  • The methods of temperature control procedures that can be employed

Importance of temperature controls

The importance of temperature controls cannot be underestimated. In controlling the temperature of food, from ‘farm to fork’ a food business will:

  • Prevent or reduce the bacterial growth
  • Prevent food poisoning outbreaks
  • Keep food safe for all
  • Comply with food safety legislation
  • Control or reduce wastage
  • Meet the requirements of due diligence

Methods of temperature controls

It is essential to control food temperature to reduce the risk associated with a food business.

Methods include both policies and practices employed in the professional kitchen, such as:

Policies:

  • Using the appropriate methods of transport
  • Providing the necessary training to individuals
  • Using an effective maintenance contract for temperature-sensitive equipment, including fridges, freezers and probes
  • Recording relevant temperature information

Methods of temperature controls

Procedures:

  • Use fridges with a core temperature below 8°C
  • Use freezers with a core temperature below 18°C
  • Use hot holding equipment with a temperature above 63°C, including bain maries, hot cupboards and Norwegian flasks
  • Heating or cooling food through the danger zone 5–63°C as quickly as possible
  • Using a blast chiller to reduce the core temperature of food to below 5°C within 90 minutes

Methods of temperature controls

Other methods include:

  • The use of an electronic temperature management system, often employed in supermarket fridges
  • Ensure supervisors carry out visual checks where necessary
  • Manual control systems to control the temperature
  • Calibration of temperature-controlled equipment
  • The use of cook/chill method, where food is cooked, rapidly chilled, then stored at the correct temperature until required

Introduction to controlling and preventing contamination

The contamination and cross-contamination of foods must be avoided at all costs if we are to reduce instances of food poisoning in the hospitality and food industries.

Cross-contamination occurs when harmful bacteria are passed from a vehicle to the food. Vehicles include:

  • Humans
  • Pests
  • Equipment
  • Other food
  • Soils

Vehicles for cross-contamination

In order to reduce the occurrences of cross-contamination the professional chef must first understand how this may occur.

  • Humans
    Cross-contamination can occur through food handlers working with raw and cooked foods without washing in between, wearing soiled uniforms etc.
  • Pests
    Pests that gain access can pass on bacteria from their own bodies, through their urine and faeces.

Vehicles for cross-contamination

  • Equipment
    Equipment can pass on bacteria through poor practices by the food handler, such as not cleaning boards and knives in between uses or using dirty cloths.
  • Other foods
    Raw and cooked foods touching each other, liquids from uncooked food dripping on other foods while in storage.
  • Soils
    Vegetables being incorrectly prepared and soil contaminating other food products.

Controlling contamination and cross-contamination

In order for any professional kitchen to reduce the risk of contamination and cross- contamination, managers and supervisors need to understand the procedures required:

  • Buildings are pest proofed
  • Maintain a pest control contract
  • Work surfaces are smooth, impervious, non-tainting, easily cleaned, no crevasses, resistant to corrosion and fit for use in the commercial sense
  • Equipment that is easy to dismantle, clean and is in a good state of repair

Controlling contamination and cross-contamination

  • Developing a linear workflow pattern
  • Maintaining a clear separation between low-risk and high-risk areas
  • Maintaining an effective cleaning regime and employing a cleaning schedule that is monitored, supervised and reviewed on a regular basis
  • Using cleaning products (COSHH) correctly, with the correct mixes employed
  • Regular microbiological swabbing of food preparation areas

Controlling contamination and cross-contamination

  • Colour coding equipment, such as utensils, chopping boards and knives – also mops, cloths and buckets
  • Using separate sinks for hand washing, food preparation and cleaning equipment
  • Ensuring good personal hygiene is maintained through hand washing and wearing protective clothing
  • A visitors’ policy that ensures visitors are briefed and provided with the necessary protective clothing

Cross-contamination

Controlling contamination and cross-contamination

  • The use of sterilising sinks for equipment
  • Storing equipment in the correct manner, including chemicals
  • Have an internal and external waste food disposal procedure. This includes the need to provide measures to store waste cooking oil, ready for recyclin

The role of management and supervisors is to make sure any professional kitchen provides its customers with food that is safe to eat. Managing the possibility of contamination and cross-contamination will help to reduce that risk to your customers.

Introduction to personal hygiene

Personal hygiene in the professional kitchen is essential in order for the business to provide its customers with safe food. All food handlers are potential carriers of food poisoning bacteria. Supervisors need to understand the potential risks and how to overcome them.

This presentation will cover the importance of high personal food hygiene practices, including:

  • Preventing transmission
  • Convalescent and healthy carriers
  • The tainting and spoilage of food

Preventing transmission

Professional chefs must have high personal hygiene standards if they are to prevent transmission of pathogenic bacteria. This can be achieved by:

  • Only wearing clean protective clothing while working in the kitchen. This prevents cross-contamination from dirty outerwear
  • Scrupulously washing hands when:
    • entering a kitchen
    • after touching hair, nose or mouth
    • when switching from working with different types of food, especially raw and cooked products
    • after handling waste products
    • after using cleaning products
    • after using the toilet

Personal hygiene

  • Keep nails short to prevent dirt being trapped under them
  • Don’t wear jewellery, such as watches and rings, as these provide an ideal location to trap food and develop bacteria
  • Make-up should not be worn in the kitchen – if it is, it should be applied sparingly
  • Any skin abrasions or cuts must be cleaned and covered by a blue plaster
  • All illness must be reported to a supervisor or manager. This is required under legislation
  • Have a daily bath or shower

Types of bacteria

There are many types of bacteria found on or in the human body. These include:

Staphylococcus aureus
Found on the hands, nose, mouth and also in cuts and grazes. Essential personal hygiene practices will reduce the effect of this bacteria.

E-coli
Found in the intestines of humans. Washing hands after visiting the toilet will prevent the pathogens entering the food chain.

Salmonella
Found in the intestine but also on hands and equipment from cross-contamination.

Types of carriers

Supervisors must understand that there are two types of carriers of pathogens who might work in their professional kitchen.

Convalescent carriers
These are carriers who are recovering from an illness but are still carrying the bacteria.

Healthy carriers
These people are carrying the bacteria but show no signs of illness. This type may well be a carrier for life.

Both types of carrier must not be allowed to work in the professional kitchen, as they could infect others and the food.

Tainting and spoilage of food

Food can be tainted from many sources. For the professional chef this includes wearing too much:

  • aftershave
  • perfume
  • make-up
  • hair product

Any tainting of food will render the food inedible and therefore spoiled.

Supervisors must lead by example and ensure they train, supervise and enforce high personal hygiene standards throughout their brigade.

Introduction to cleaning and disinfection

Food premises require robust and thorough cleaning procedures, in order to ensure safe practices are applied in a professional kitchen.

To meet these requirements the professional kitchen requires:

  • Procedures for cleaning and disinfection
  • Procedures for supervising cleaning

There is also a need for procedures to manage waste and its disposal.

Policies for cleaning and disinfection

A professional kitchen should include the following policies to ensure effective cleaning and disinfection of a food premises:

  • A clear-as-you-go policy – good housekeeping practices should be employed to reduce waste in the kitchen
  • Clean-as-you-go procedures to reduce the build-up of grease and grime
  • Following manufacturers’ instructions
  • The correct procedures for cleaning large equipment, including:
    • disconnect from power supply
    • clean in place due to the size

Cleaning methods

Traditional cleaning stages for crockery and small kitchen equipment include:

  • Pre-clean, main clean, rinse, disinfect, rinse and dry.

Other methods include:

  • Clean, rinse and sanitise method
  • Double sink washing-up method:
    • pre-clean/main clean using detergent
    • second sink to disinfect using water above 82°C

The use of mechanical equipment is also used in the professional kitchen, including floor scrubbers and plate-wash machines.

Using chemicals for cleaning

When using chemicals it is vital that the correct procedures are followed. These include:

  • Clearing areas before employing chemicals, to prevent contamination from overspray
  • Correct dilution of chemicals to gain the optimum efficiency to aid the cleaning process
  • Using colour coded cloths for different chemicals or single-use cloths, if colour coded are unavailable

Control of Substances Hazardous to Health (COSHH)

The use of chemicals within the workplace is strictly regulated. Compliance with the COSHH regulations is essential and supervisors must ensure staff are trained and carefully watched when using chemicals for cleaning.

Supervisors must ensure that:

  • Chemicals are stored correctly in a secure area
  • Chemicals are stored in their original containers
  • The dilution and use of chemicals is in accordance with the manufacturers’ guidance
  • Safety data sheets and PPE is provided
  • Spillages are correctly dealt with
  • The mixing of chemicals is strictly controlled

Waste disposal

Waste disposal procedures are required to prevent contamination and to comply with legislation. Procedures used in the professional kitchen include:

  • A clean-as-you-go policy and waste removal at regular intervals
  • Allowing no waste to be stored in the kitchen overnight
  • Using bin bags to contain waste
  • Ensuring waste receptacles are serviceable, clean and kept on a hard surface to aid removal

Waste disposal

Outside of the kitchen, procedures are required to:

  • Separate waste into relevant streams, including:
    • cardboard
    • wet waste
    • glass
    • plastics
    • oil
    • metal
    • general

By separating waste, the food business can ensure the correct removal procedures are adhered to by waste contractors and that resources are recycled.

Waste storage areas

In order for food businesses to store their waste correctly, storage and areas should be:

  • Separate waste into relevant streams, including:
    • Made from an impervious material
    • Be pest proofed
    • Protected from the weather – both sunlight and rain
    • Oil storage should also include procedures for spillages, including spill kits and drip trays
    • Kept clean and tidy to prevent odours attracting pests
    • Have access to hoses to aid cleaning

Food businesses must also consider a procedure to collect oil waste from grease traps and ensure any contractor complies with this procedure.

Introduction to pest control

All food premises must ensure they have adequate controls to prevent unsafe food being produced and sold.

To meet this requirement employers and employees in the professional kitchen must understand:

  • The importance of pest control
  • The types of pests involved
  • The methods of control

The importance of pest control

Pest control is important in any food business to:

  • Meet the requirements of legislation and provide safe food for the consumer
  • Avoid contamination – this includes pathogenic bacteria and spoilage bacteria
  • Avoid food wastage
  • Avoid damage to the building and equipment

Pest control is also vitally important to the reputation of the business with consumers. A reputation for poor pest control could damage the business’ reputation beyond redemption.

It also plays a role in staff morale – no worker wants to be employed in a professional kitchen that has pest problems.

Types of pests

There are many pests that want to gain access to the professional kitchen. If they succeed, this may damage the reputation and name of the business. Some of these pests include:

Rodents
This includes rats and mice; they carry bacteria on their bodies and urinate continuously. Signs they are present include smear marks, droppings, smell, doors and equipment that have been gnawed. A mouse only needs a hole as large as its head to gain access.

Cockroaches
Cockroaches in the UK include the German and American cockroach varieties. Both prefer the dark and often hide in walls and machinery in a kitchen. Signs of infestation include an unpleasant smell, larvae and sightings in the kitchen. Their bodies are generally covered in bacteria and they infect anything they touch.

Types of pests

Insects
Insects include flies, ants, weevils and other flying insects. These pests are normally seen in the kitchen or, in the case of weevils, in storerooms and the products kept in them.

Domestic pets
These pets carry harmful pathogenic bacteria on their fur and coats. It is also in their urine and on their tongues when they lick people.

Birds
Birds can be seen and they also leave evidence through their droppings. They carry bacteria such as e-coli and salmonella.

Wild cats
Wild cats carry all manner of pathogenic bacteria. Due to their living habits, they may carry clostridium perfringens, salmonella and clostridium botulinum from soil.

Methods to control pests

In order for the professional chef to control pests that might get into their kitchen, they need to design methods to prevent entry or to eradicate them if they do enter. This can be achieved through:

Writing a pest control policy
This policy should direct management, supervisors and staff in how to plan, monitor and deal with pest control. The policy should include at its core the need for a clean-as-you-go to reduce food available for pests.

Building maintenance
Food businesses should ensure that access is denied to pests through a good maintenance programme, employing self-closing doors, fly screening and a clear path around the building for visual inspections. The use of electronic fly killers can also be employed.

Methods to control pests

Employing a pest controller

In today’s environment the prevention and eradication of pests should be left to professionals. The methods they employ will help a food business identify, remove and monitor pests. They will also provide a reporting structure for a food business to prove due diligence has taken place.

Pest controllers will use:

  • traps to catch mice and cockroaches
  • rodenticides to poison rats
  • pesticides for the removal of flies

In some cases only professionals can use some of the compounds now found in the UK to eradicate pests.
