Introduction

The preparation and production of paste products requires many different techniques. These include:

  • rubbing-in
  • creaming
  • folding
  • rolling
  • resting
  • beating
  • boiling/melting.

Rubbing-in

This technique is used when making a short paste. The fat should be cold which allows the chef to produce a fine crumb.

It can be achieved using mechanical means, a mixing machine or by hand.

Creaming

A creaming technique is used when making a sweet paste.

The fat and sugar is creamed together before adding the liquid component of the recipe.

The method is best achieved using a mixing machine but can also be done by hand.

Folding

Folding is used when making puff or flaky paste.

Puff pastry is folded six times with a resting period of 20 minutes between each fold. The resting period is to allow for the gluten to relax to ease the rolling of the paste.

The result of the folding is many layers of pastry – this is called lamination.

Rolling

Rolling is used to produce a paste such as puff pastry but also in the process of shaping other pastes such as a short or sweet paste.

When rolling any paste, care should be taken to:

  • roll using a small amount of flour, turning occasionally to prevent the paste sticking to the surface
  • apply even pressure at all times
  • treat the paste with care to prevent overworking.

Resting

With the exception of choux paste, all other pastes should be rested. This will:

  • allow the gluten to relax and therefore aid in the rolling and shaping process
  • prevent the paste from shrinking during the cooking.

Beating

The beating technique is employed when a smooth consistency is required for the paste. An example of this is a choux paste where the eggs are beaten into the mix once cooled.

Beating ensures:

  • there is a uniform mix of the ingredients
  • a smooth batter is achieved.

Boiling/melting

For making paste for pork or gala pies:

  • Water and fat are bought to the boil. The flour and salt are then added.
  • This paste should be shaped and rolled when hot.

For making a Genoise batter:

  • The eggs and sugar are whisked over hot water, until doubled in volume.
  • The mix is then cooled and the flour is folded in.
  • Melted butter is then carefully added.

Introduction

It is essential when making any paste products that the following is adhered to:

  • Correct weighing of commodities
  • Following the recipe
  • Correct cooking temperatures
  • Correct storage
  • Correct finishing methods.

Quiche Lorraine

A quiche Lorraine is made with a short paste.

  • Grease a flan ring and line the ring with the paste.
  • Dock by using fork prick the base to prevent rising.
  • Blind bake until cooked.
  • Add the bacon and cheese mix, then strain the milk, eggs, salt and pepper into the flan.
  • Return to the oven until browned.

Fruit tarts

Fruit tarts are made with a sweet paste.

  • Roll out the pastry to 3mm in thickness.
  • Cut into rounds with a fluted cutter.
  • Grease the tartlet moulds and blind bake.
  • Once cooled, fill with pastry cream and dress with fruit.
  • Glaze the top with the same flavour fruit glaze.

Profiteroles

Profiteroles are made using a choux paste.

  • Pipe walnut-size balls onto a baking sheet using a plain piping nozzle.
  • Bake until well risen and golden brown in colour.
  • Place on a wire rack and allow to cool completely.
  • Fill with Chantilly cream and dredge with icing sugar.

Eccles cakes

Eccles cakes are produced using a puff paste.

  • Roll out the puff paste until 3mm in thickness.
  • Cut into roundels.
  • Wet the edges and place filling into the centre.
  • Fold the edges, brush with egg wash and cut two incisions into the top of each Eccles cake.
  • Place on a greased baking tray and cook until golden brown.

Steak and kidney pudding

Steak and kidney pudding is produced using a suet paste.

  • Line a greased basin keeping 25% of the paste for the lid.
  • Mix the filling ingredients together and place in the basin, then add water to approximately 1cm from the top.
  • Moisten the edge and place a lid on top using the rest of the paste.
  • Cover the basin with foil or a pudding cloth.
  • Cook in a steamer.

Introduction to finishing paste products

When finishing any paste products it is essential that they are completed as per the requirements of the dish specification sheet.

It is at this stage that most care must be taken, as the visual impact has the greatest effect on the customer’s perceptions.

Products of the same type must be of the same:

  • shape
  • size
  • colour
  • finish.

Finishing paste products

Chefs must learn several finishing techniques to deliver a quality product. These include:

  • icing
  • dusting
  • egg Wash
  • glazing
  • piping
  • filling.

Icing

When finishing any paste products, icing allows the chef to use their artistic flair to improve the overall visual effect for their customers. Icing can take the form of:

  • a covering
  • piping
  • dusting
  • sprinkling
  • dredging.

Egg wash

Using egg wash on paste products will have different effects dependent on the mix of the egg wash:

  • Whole egg and water – browned slightly glossy
  • Whole egg and milk – browned and more glossy
  • Egg white only – evenly browned
  • Egg yolk only – brown and shiny
  • Egg yolk and milk – darkest colour and glossy.

Glazing

Glazing provides a glossy smooth finish to a product and can be achieved using:

  • apricot glaze
  • icing sugar dusted on the surface before cooking
  • water icing which provides a clear glaze that may be coloured and flavoured
  • a commercial glaze produced from pectin.

Piping

Piping is used as part of the preparation, for example in profiteroles, but it is also an essential finishing technique.

Piping allows the chef to add colour, shape, texture and flavour. Piping is not restricted to icing sugar. It can also be used for:

  • chocolate
  • fondant
  • buttercream
  • Chantilly cream
  • fresh cream.

Filling

Filling paste products provides the chef with the opportunity to introduce new flavours, textures and colour to a finished product. Fillings include:

  • chocolate
  • buttercream
  • Chantilly cream
  • fresh cream
  • ganache
  • fruits.

It is essential to not overfill as this will have a negative effect on the finished product.

A complex choux paste recipe

Croquembouche

A croquembouche, French for ‘something that crunches in the mouth’, is a complex recipe due to the different skills required.

First you are required to make a choux pastry for the profiteroles, then a pastry cream for the filling. This is then followed by caramel which will help to hold the piece together, add flavour and provides the decorative finish.

Choux paste – ingredients

  • 750g flour
  • 15 eggs
  • 625ml water
  • 250g butter

Pastry cream – ingredients

  • 1,300ml milk
  • 120g flour
  • 400g sugar
  • 14 eggs
  • Vanilla essence to taste

Caramel – ingredients

  • 300g sugar
  • 225ml water

Choux paste – method

  • Add the butter and water together and bring to the boil.
  • Over the heat, add the flour and beat the mixture ensuring there are no lumps and the flour has been fully absorbed. Continue to beat until the mixture leaves the side of the pan free.
  • Allow the mixture to cool.
  • Beat the eggs together then add, in small quantities, to the cooled mixture until it is absorbed and the mix is smooth.

Pastry cream – method

  • Separate the egg yolks and whites, then mix the yolks, sugar and essence using 120ml of the milk.
  • Add the flour to the mix and ensure the ingredients are thoroughly combined.
  • Boil the remainder of the milk then stir into the mixture.
  • Return to the heat and bring back to the boil. Ensure that you are constantly stirring the mixture.
  • Once thickened allow to cool.

Caramel – method

  • Place the sugar and water in a thick-bottomed pan and bring to the boil. Do not stir.
  • Using a sugar thermometer, achieve a temperature of 154°C. Then remove from the heat and plunge the base of the pan into cold water to stop the heating process.

Making the croquembouche

  • Using the choux paste, make approximately 75 profiteroles – bake at 200°C for 18–20 minutes until golden brown and well risen. Remove from the oven and place on a wire rack to cool.
  • Allow the profiteroles to cool then, using a plain nozzle, fill with the pastry cream.
  • Using the caramel as a glue, dip the base of each filled profiterole and build your cone of profiteroles. It is easier to build the croquembouche on the service tray rather than moving it later.
  • The croquembouche can then be decorated with either the caramel left over being drizzled on to it or alternatively you can make some spun sugar to decorate the finished dish.

Alternative takes on the croquembouche

A modern twist to a classic croquembouche uses macaroons instead of the profiteroles as the base. This twist also uses meringue as the base to hold the macaroons.

Puff paste recipe

Mille feuilles

Mille feuilles or in English translates to a ‘thousand layers’ are an extremely popular and challenging to make puff paste product. Another name for this product is ‘Napoleon’, possibly so named because this delicious sweet is fit for an emperor.

Mille feuille – ingredients

  • 625g puff pastry
  • 200g fondant icing
  • 25g apricot glaze
  • 150g pastry cream
  • Brown food colouring

Mille feuille – method

  • The puff pastry is rolled out to 3mm in thickness, then cut into three equal lengths 65mm wide.
  • The trimmings are then collected and rolled out to 3mm. This will be used for decoration.
  • The three measured lengths of puff paste should then be docked using a fork, allowed to rest and then cooked at 220°C until golden brown.
  • When cooked, remove from the oven and place upside down on a table and allow to cool.
  • Correct the shape of the three sheets of puff paste then crumble the sheet made from the trimmings.
  • Construct the slice by selecting the best sheet as the top layer and put this to one side. Layer the bottom two layers with pastry cream. Then place the top layer on upside down so the top surface is flat. Brush the surface with apricot glaze.
  • Warm the fondant and separate approximately 25%. Colour this with the food colouring. Next cover the top of the slice with the white fondant, then using a piping bag pipe lines of the coloured fondant on the white fondant approximately 25mm apart. Use the back of a knife to create a marbled effect on the surface.
  • Spread the remaining pastry cream on the sides of the slice cover with the crumbled flakes of pastry

Alternative takes on mille feuilles

The mille feuille has come a long way since Napoleon. Today’s modern chefs have managed to provide different types of mille feuilles using fruits and different creams and finishes.

Today’s chefs have even managed to use a mille feuille as a savoury using spinach, smoked salmon, mozzarella and tomato.
