Dry heat Cookery Methods

Roasting

  • Used extensively for meat and poultry. Whole poultry cuts are often cooked either bone-in or boned out and often stuffed and tied. Place on a trivet or bones and cook to the desired degree of doneness. Poultry is trussed and can be barded e.g. turkey breast to prevent excessive drying out. Cook until just done 45 minutes per kg

Pot roasting

  • An alternative to roasting. Place the primal or whole bird in a roasting dish or braisiere and smear with butter, cover with a lid and cook in a medium oven. Take the lid off the last 30 minutes to brown the food

Dry heat Cookery Methods

Baking

  • Popular for primal or portion cuts of poultry. The item can be sealed, enveloped in pastry e.g. puff, filo or brioche and then baked. Pies are a popular item using this method

En Papillote

  • Popular as a healthier cookery method due to the fact that no fat needs to be added as the item is cooked by the steam within the paper parcel. The item can be sealed, enveloped in parchment paper and then baked in the oven e.g. Chicken supreme en papillote with vegetable strips, chicken breast with Asian mushrooms

Dry heat Cookery Methods

Grilling

  • All first class portion cuts are suitable and should be placed on the grill. Poultry items such as boned breast, or thigh are suitable for grilling. Spatchcock or quail that has been flattened, is often grilled under a salamander
  • Poultry offal items such as kebabs, sliced liver, kidney are perfect for grilling

Dry heat Cookery Methods

Shallow frying

  • Suitable for all first class portion cuts of poultry. The poultry may be drawn through flour first to seal the item quicker and provide a crustier exterior. Crumbed items are very popular. Typical items are southern fried crumbed chicken breast and chicken Keiv.

Deep-frying

  • Used for marinated cuts e.g. sweet and sour chicken, fried chicken using either crumbs or batter coating. Farces can also be formed and coated e.g. chicken croquettes

Stir-frying

  • A healthy way to serve chicken breast and will and colour and flavour to any table. You can use flavours such as Soy sauce or Oyster sauce.

Moist Heat Cookery Methods

Boiling

  • Most poultry cuts are suitable and should be submerged in the desired liquid e.g. Court-bouillon. Typical examples are boiler chickens used for croquettes or chicken soup
  • Stocks are made from bones and trimmings

Poaching

  • The breast portion cuts of poultry are suitable for shallow poaching. The poultry may be placed on a bed of finely diced French onions or brunoise of vegetables. Typical items chicken supreme in Chablis sauce or chicken liver pâté
  • Deep-poaching can be used for whole poultry e g. poached chicken tom yum. Farces can also be used as a stuffing and placed inside boned poultry or a boned hock, poached, chilled and coated then decorated e.g. chicken galantine

Moist Heat Cookery Methods

Stewing

  • Second class cuts are suitable due to their higher collagen content. For stewing the item is usually diced or cut small. It is sealed or added after the onions have been sweated, covered with liquid and then cooked with barely perceptible movement. Typical examples are chicken curry

Braising

  • All second class cuts of poultry are suitable for braising. The poutry meat is sealed and placed in a braisiere on mirepoix. Stock is added to just come up half way of the item. Add a lid and cook until done. Poultry often apply this method for older birds or the drumsticks or Maryland e.g. Confit of duck.

Recipe key points

  • Ensure that you follow the instructions of the recipe; weigh off all the ingredients and quantities and adhere to the steps in the method.
  • Be aware that the final product is only as good as the ingredients that you use, so check the quality and freshness.
  • Use the correct process and cookery method to get the best outcome. The cookery method used is there for a reason.
  • Finish the recipe correctly e.g. Thicken the sauce, adjust the seasoning, and plate correctly.

Flavourings used for poultry

Poultry recipes vary across the globe and recipes are often created based on the climate and cultural preferences of a region or country.

Marinades are one of the oldest methods for preserving food. They preserve food by preventing bacteria and air coming into contact with the food item, whilst tenderising it at the same time. Marinades are also used to impart flavour to food. It is important to select a marinade that complements the flavours of your food. There are two types:

  • Instant Marinades
  • Soaking Marinades
Instant Marinades
  • Lemon juice
  • Worcestershire sauce
  • Soy sauce
  • Yoghurt and tandoori paste
  • Herbs and oil
A recipe example of an Asian style instant marinade:
  • 100ml Sweet Soy sauce
  • 20ml Honey
  • 2 cloves Garlic

Flavourings used for poultry

Soaking Marinades
  • Buttermilk
  • Red wine
  • Beer
  • Brines. (Used for older birds or as preparation for smoked poultry)
  • Pine branches
  • Juniper berries, rosemary, sage and other strong flavours are used for game birds
A recipe example of a red wine soaking marinade for a braised poultry dish:
  • 1 litre Red wine
  • 500g Mirepoix of vegetables
  • 1 clove Garlic
  • 100ml Olive oil
  • Peppercorns, thyme, parsley, bay leaf

Coatings used for Poultry

There are many ways to add simple flavours and textures to poultry, this can be achieved by coating the poultry:

  • Coating with seasoned flour
  • Coating with herb and spice seasoned flour (Southern fried chicken)
  • Coating in breadcrumbs - dipping chicken portions in seasoned flour, then beaten egg and finally in breadcrumbs (This is often known as Pane)

Stuffings used for poultry

There are many ways to add simple flavours to poultry, this can be achieved by stuffing the poultry:

  • Garlic butter for use in Chicken Kiev
  • Cheese and Ham for poached or breaded chicken
  • Garlic cheese
  • Apricots and mixed spices
  • Sage and onion stuffing

Unit 306: Poultry

Poultry Recipe - Complex and Modern

Coronation style chicken, curried sauce, Jasmine rice and apple and tomato

Ingredients and Method

Chicken – Ingredients

  • 4 x chicken breasts
  • 20g x curry powder
  • 20 ml x olive oil
  • 4 kaffir lime leaves, cut into julienne
  • Salt
  • Black pepper

Chicken – Method

Remove the skin from the chicken breasts. Season with salt and freshly-ground pepper.

In a frying pan over medium heat, cook the curry powder in the olive oil until fragrant. Set aside to cool.

In a large bowl, combine the curry flavoured oil with the kaffir lime leaf julienne. Add the chicken and toss in the marinade to evenly coat. Leave to marinate for 20 minutes.

Place the marinated chicken on a double layer of cling film and roll into a cylinder shape, securing each end with a knot. Poach in a large saucepan of simmering water until a core temperature of 75°C is achieved, about 20 minutes. Remove the chicken from the liquid, set aside to cool.

Curry Sauce – Ingredients

  • 20ml x oil
  • ½ x onion, chopped
  • 1 x garlic clove
  • ½ x red chilli, finely chopped
  • 20g x curry powder
  • 100ml x coconut milk
  • 1 x kaffir lime leaf
  • 1 x lime, peel and juice
  • 100g x natural yoghurt
  • 15g x mango purée
  • Salt
  • Black pepper

Curry Sauce – Method

In a frying pan, heat the oil and sweat the onion and garlic over medium heat until softened. Add the chilli and curry powder, cook for 1 minute.

Increase heat; add the coconut milk and kaffir lime leaf, bring to a boil. Strain through a fine mesh sieve and set aside to cool. Add the lime peel, juice, yoghurt and mango puree. Season.

Rice – Ingredients

  • 350g x jasmine rice
  • 600ml x water
  • Salt
  • 1 x lime, peel and juice
  • ½ x bunch coriander

Rice – Method

Place the rice into a heavy-bottomed saucepan, add water and salt. Bring to a boil, stir. Cover with a tight-fitting lid and reduce to a low heat. Simmer for 10-12 minutes, or until all of the liquid has been absorbed and the rice is cooked al dente.

Remove from the heat and leave to stand for 5 minutes. Fluff up the rice grains with a fork, Stir in the lime juice, peel and coriander.

Garnish – Ingredients

  • Micro leaves (or coriander)
  • 1 x apple, cut into small batons
  • 8 x cherry tomatoes, quartered
  • 50g x Sliced and toasted almonds

Garnish – Method

Remove the cling film from the chicken and carve into slices.

Spoon sauce in the centre of the plate and arrange small spoonfuls of rice.

Place the chicken on the rice and decorate with micro leaves (or coriander), apple batons, cherry tomato quarters and toasted sliced almonds.

Unit 306: Poultry

Feathered Game Recipe - Complex and Modern

Pan fried Supreme of Pheasant, fondant potatoes, spring cabbage and bacon with a Madeira and redcurrant jus (serves 4)

Ingredients and Method

Pan fried Supreme of Pheasant – Ingredients

  • 4 x Pheasant Supremes
  • 100g x Butter
  • 100ml x White wine
  • 50ml x Game stock

Pan fried Supreme of Pheasant – Method

Remove the suprêmes as for chickens. Season the suprêmes and sauté them in butter leaving them slightly under-cooked. Remove the suprêmes from the pan and keep warm.

Deglaze the pan with white wine, add a little game stock reduce by one third add the butter and allow it to just melt. Pour the sauce over the suprêmes to help keep in the moist and place in the oven at a low temperature for 6 minutes.

Fondant potato – Ingredients

  • 4 Large Potatoes
  • Sprig of rosemary
  • 200gms Salted butter
  • 200ml Chicken Stock
  • 2 Garlic cloves
  • Salt & Pepper

Fondant potato – Method

Add the butter and potatoes in a saucepan and put onto the heat and fry until deep golden-brown on one side, about 5-6 minutes.

Turn over the potatoes and cook for a further 5-6 minutes, or until golden-brown on both sides. Carefully pour in the stock, and then add the garlic cloves and rosemary sprigs.

Place the pan into the oven at 170 degrees until tender, then remove the potatoes from the pan using a pallet knife and keep warm.

Spring Cabbage – Ingredients

  • 1 x Spring Cabbage
  • 100ml x Vegetable stock
  • 50ml x Double Cream
  • 100g x Lardon of smoked streaky bacon
  • 25g x Butter

Spring Cabbage – Method

Wash, prepare the cabbage and thinly slice, blanch the cabbage in the vegetable stock, drain. Fry the lardon of smoky bacon in a little butter using a frying pan until crisp and then add the blanched cabbage. Adjust seasoning, add double cream and keep warm.

Game stock – Ingredients

  • 4 lb game bones; with attached meat
  • 3-tablespoon olive oil
  • 1-teaspoon salt
  • 6 whole black peppercorns
  • 5 fresh bay leaves
  • 3 garlic cloves
  • 1 onion, peeled and quartered
  • 2 celery stalks, cut in pieces
  • 1 carrot cut in pieces
  • 200gms ground beef
  • 2 pig trotters
  • Egg whites
  • Red wine

Game stock – Method

Put the game bones and few halved brown onions and halved carrots into a large roasting dish. Roast at 220°C until they are well browned. Turn them a few times and take care that they do not burn, as this will cause the stock to taste bitter. Transfer the bones to a large heavy-based stockpot. Fill with enough cold water so the bones are covered by about 5cm of the water. Pour off any fat in the roasting dish. Deglaze with a little wine or water. Scrape up any sediment and cook to reduce pan juices by half. Add to the stockpot. Add pig trotters and ground beef.

Bring the stock to the boil very slowly. Greyish scum will rise to the surface as the stock comes to the boil; use a wide ladle or large spoon to skim it away. Add egg whites and then strain. Reduce stock until correct consistency and taste.

Madeira & Redcurrant jus – Ingredients

  • 200ml Game stock
  • 50ml Madeira
  • 2 tablespoons honey
  • 200g Redcurrant Jelly
  • Red food colouring
  • Corn Starch to thicken

Madeira & Redcurrant jus - Method

Combine the Madeira and stock over a medium-high heat. Stir and bring to the boil. Add the redcurrant jelly to the mix, and stir well. Allow to continue boiling, again until mixture is thick and just about halved – another 15-20 minutes. Stir occasionally. Once nicely thickened and reduced, turn off heat. Strain if needed making sure the consistency and seasoning is correct.

N.B: Seasonal ingredients can be incorporated for variety and to control costs.
