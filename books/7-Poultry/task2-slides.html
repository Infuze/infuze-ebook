Introduction to preparing poultry

From a financial point of view poultry forms the major food expenditure in most catering businesses. It is imperative that the chef is aware of yields, portion control and waste minimisation.

A blend of first and second class cuts should be used to minimise the amount of wastage and maximise the amount of profit from a poultry carcass.

Preservation methods for poultry

To prevent waste and minimise losses many different preservation methods are available for the chef:

  • Chilling – store food items in the fridge or coolroom following hygiene principles
  • Freezing – extends lifespan between 1-6 months must be wrapped properly to prevent freezer burn
  • Vacuum packing – removes air and prevents oxygen coming into contact with the items
  • MAP – modified atmosphere packaging is used for portion cut items in trays. The oxygen is replaced by a mixture of gases restricting bacteria growth

Preservation methods for poultry

To prevent waste and minimise losses many different preservation methods are available for the chef:

  • Salting – salt kills bacteria and draws out excess moisture. Used in dry salting and for brines.
  • Canning – used to cook the items and sealing them in for extensive storage. Used for spreads such as chicken paste
  • Smoking – either cold or hot smoking is used, the item is normally salted and then hung in the smoke to kill off further bacteria on the outside and to impart the flavour

Hygiene

The following measures will support correct hygiene:

  • Prepare poultry in one designated area of the kitchen. Ensure that all workbenches, trays and storage containers are clean and sanitised
  • Clean and sanitise your knives and boards before commencing work as they can become contaminated with bacteria from raw food.
  • Prevent cross-contamination during preparation and storage. If hands and utensils are not carefully washed and are then used to prepare cooked food, the cooked food can become contaminated from the raw food. Prevent dripping of liquid from raw foods by storing food in clean, non-toxic washable containers or cover with foil or plastic, clearly labelled. If it is not possible to store raw poultry and meats separately, store on lower shelves below cooked foods.

Hygiene

  • Use colour coded boards for prepared and finished products and never cut cooked items on boards that have been used previously for the cutting of raw poultry items – unless the board has been properly washed in between tasks.
  • Ensure storage temperatures are below 4°C for raw poultry and above 70°C for cooked poultry.
  • Minced poultry meat should be processed on the same day it is purchased and should always be cooked through. The increased surface area means mince goes off much more quickly.
  • Follow personal hygiene and wear clean uniform for hygiene and protection.
  • You should avoid washing raw chicken and this can present a greater risk to food safety.

Cuts used for Poultry

Preparation skills applied are skinning and trimming of surplus fat and sinews.

  • Prepare poultry for sauté cuts by trimming off the bones from breast, thigh or drumstick.
  • Prepare a galantine by removing all bones except wing and drumstick bone, slicing thigh and breast meat and crumbing items, mincing offcuts and using them for farce e.g. fill boned necks, boned quail, boned Maryland.
  • Whole poultry can be trussed and barded to keep it moist

Equipment

Tools and equipment:

  • When preparing and cooking poultry dishes a range of knives are used. The most common are boning, French, paring and palette knives, sharpening equipment are also essential.
  • A range of pans, trays and bowls are required depending on the recipe
  • Small tools and equipment include ladles, whisks, chinois, perforated spoons, gastronorm containers, etc.
  • Large equipment utilised include stoves, ovens, steamers, grills and salamanders, deep-fryers, sous-vide cookers.
  • Fridges, freezers and coolrooms are essential for storage.

Equipment

Tools and equipment:

  • Ensure that you are aware of the standard operating procedures (SOP).
  • When using equipment ensure that the electricity is switched off and disconnected when cleaning, assembling or disassembling machinery.
  • Be very careful when using machinery with moving parts – you could be severely injured.
  • It is a legal requirement to use pushers or guards that come with equipment such as slicers, mandolines, food processors and juicers during operation.

Planning poultry dishes

  • When preparing and cooking poultry dishes a range of preparation and finishing tasks are required. As you have to multi-task it is important to plan out the day. Preparation lists and mise en place tasks should be set up and then prioritise the tasks based on the length of time they take or their impact on further steps.
  • If you prepare a range of items always prepare the one that will take the longest to cook first to ensure sufficient time for it to finish before service.
  • Work methodically and keep your work station clear.
  • Check everything before service to make sure you are ready to go.

Planning

  • Minimise pressure during service by focussing on the task and communicating with your team.
  • Be efficient but do not panic, this only exacerbates mistakes.
  • Debrief after service to make sure any problems are dealt with.
  • Clean up and get ready for the next shift.
  • Remember – good mise en place is good workflow and less stress!

Storing poultry

Storage of prepared ingredients for poultry dishes is vital for the safety of your customer. Food safety and hygiene regulations must be complied with at all times. You must consider:

  • Temperature
  • Date labelling
  • Coverings
  • Positioning
  • Stock rotation

Storage of frozen poultry

  • Store in the freezer at -18°C to -24°C
  • Individually wrap items for ease of separation when defrosting
  • Spread items out on a tray to speed up the freezing process. Once the items are frozen they may then be bulk packed
  • Frozen poultry is best used within 3 months
  • Never re-freeze poultry after it has been thawed

Storage of fresh poultry

  • Store in the refrigerator at 1-4°C
  • If polythene wrapped, as in whole chickens, store breast down. This way the breast will retain more moisture and the skin will not dry out
  • If the chicken is not wrapped, cover it to prevent drying out and spoiling due to contact with other food items
  • Store on drip trays and change regularly to prevent cross-contamination
  • Poultry is best used within 3 days of delivery

Temperature

The following needs to be considered for all poultry dishes:

  • Ideally poultry should be used fresh daily. If not it must:
    • Be chilled to below 5°C in 90 minutes
    • Stored labelled in a fridge
    • Frozen -18°C or below
  • If cooked poultry dishes are stored in a freezer, they must be cooked to the legal required temperature for 2 minutes before use.
  • Poultry must not be reheated more than once
  • Hot holding must be above 63°C

Date labelling & coverings

When labelling any poultry dishes, the following guidelines should be followed:

  • Name of product
  • Date of production
  • ‘Use by’ date.

Ideally any poultry dishes being stored should be in an airtight container, however, other coverings include:

  • cling film
  • greaseproof paper.

Positioning & stock rotation

When storing any poultry dishes you must always consider the possibility of cross contamination. All meat and offal dishes not for immediate use should be:

  • Covered to prevent cross contamination
  • Stored in a fridge or freezer
  • Raw poultry should be stored on the bottom shelf of the fridge to prevent spillage
  • Cooked poultry should be stored on the top shelf of the fridge
  • Labelled to ensure stock rotation, “first in first out”
