Introduction

When making fermented dough products, specialist equipment is available to make the process more effective and efficient. This includes the following:

  • Weighing scales
  • Mixers with dough hooks
  • Proving cabinets
  • Loaf tins
  • Baking trays/sheets
  • Pastry scrapers
  • Convection/deck ovens.

Weighing scales

This is an essential piece of equipment to ensure the correct weighing of commodities to meet the product requirements and ensure consistency each time the product is made.

Mixers with dough hooks

Most mixers these days, come with a dough hook.

This specialist equipment kneads the dough efficiently and rapidly develops the protein, gluten. This is the protein that provides an elasticity that traps the air bubbles in the mix.

Proving cabinet

Proving cabinets provide the chef with the ability to control the warmth and humidity.

This control ensures the perfect atmosphere can be obtained for the even expansion of the fermented dough products.

Baking tins and sheets

Baking tins come in many shapes and sizes. These also include baking sheets. They allow the chef to:

  • shape the dough before baking
  • control portion sizes
  • easily place and remove the baked dough from ovens.

Ovens

Bakers use several types of oven to bake the dough products in such as the following:

  • Convection ovens – provide a good all-round heat distribution
  • Deck ovens – heat is based in the floor of the oven. This type of oven is especially good when baking pizza dough.

Other equipment used

There is many other specialist equipment available to the baker including the following:

  • Pastry scrapers and horns will minimise waste and help you to work hygienically.
  • Palette knives, straight and cranked spatulas assist with the removal of dough.
  • Piping bags with a wide variety of nozzles allow for piping of softer dough such as a savarin dough.

Introduction to producing fermented dough

The preparation and production of fermented dough products are basic skills required by all bakers, pastry chefs and chefs.

Understanding the principles of production and key steps in the preparation will ensure that you can produce quality products and avoid costly mistakes.

Producing shaped dinner rolls

  1. Sieve the flour and dissolve the yeast in water.
  2. Dissolve the salt, sugar and milk powder in the remaining water.
  3. Add the yeast mix and the sugar-milk powder mix to the flour and knead. Allow to rest then knock back.
  4. Shape and then place on a baking sheet. Prove, egg wash then bake.

Producing tinned/shaped loaf

  1. Sieve the flour, add the salt and sugar. Rub in the butter, then add the yeast.
  2. Add the warm water and knead until a smooth dough is achieved. Allow to rest, then knock back.
  3. Place the mix into the loaf tin and then allow to prove.
  4. Cook at 220°C for approximately 40 minutes. Turn out on to a wire rack to cool.

Producing Bath buns

  1. Add the the bun spice to a basic bun dough mix, sultanas, 2/3 of the sugar nibs and egg yolks.
  2. Scale and portion the dough into 60g pieces using a pastry scraper.
  3. Roughly shape the buns and place on a paper-lined baking sheet. Sprinkle the remaining 1/3 of the sugar nibs (or alternatively caraway seeds) over the buns.
  4. Cook at 200°C for approximately 20 minutes. Once out the oven brush with bun wash.

Producing soda bread

  1. Sieve the flour, salt and the raising agent, bicarbonate of soda. Then add the buttermilk.
  2. Knead the dough mould into a bun shape. Mark the top, using the back of a knife, with a cross.
  3. Cook at 200°C for approximately 25 minutes.

Producing blinis (mini-pancakes)

  1. Mix 100g of strong flour with 70g of medium flour, 2g of yeast and salt.
  2. Add one egg and lukewarm milk. Whisk until the batter is smooth and all the commodities are incorporated.
  3. Using a spoon, drop the dollops of the mixture on to a non-stick pan.
  4. Cook until golden brown on either side.

Fault finding

The making of quality fermented dough products is an art in its own right and bakers need to be real specialists. Whilst chefs may produce quality goods, without having the specialist baking knowledge and experience, there is more chance of faults occurring.

Whilst some faults are easy to explain others are not. Below are some of the common faults found when making fermented products and their causes.

Lack of rise in the dough

  • Weighing of ingredients – the improper ratio of ingredients, especially yeast, will prevent a proper rise when proving and cooking.
  • Overmixing/undermixing – both these factors prevent the dough from retaining the gas produced by the yeast.
  • Proving – when proving a dough it is essential the the right temperature is achieved – too hot and it will kill some of the yeast. If the moisture within the prover is too wet this will prevent the gases developing and providing a proper lift.
  • Oven temperature – an oven that is too hot will kill the yeast before full development.

Too much rise

  • Cooking temperature too low, thus the yeast lives longer and produces too much gas.
  • Recipe versus tin size – when producing any fermented dough product if a tin is to be used (to shape a tin loaf for example) then too much mix in the tin will spill over during the cooking process.
  • Overproofing the mix before the cooking process begins may cause the mix to overdevelop.

Hollow bottom breads

  • Overmixing causing the dough to retain less of the gases produced.
  • Moisture on the bottom of the tin – this will cause steam which will push the mixture up.

Thickened crusts in bread

  • Overcooking the dough.
  • Placing the dough in an oven that is too cool, thus increasing the cooking time of the dough.

Other faults

  • Closed texture – due to a lack of kneading, insufficient yeast in the recipe or poor measuring of the liquids used.
  • Coarse texture of a dough may be caused by too much liquid or salt, overproving or underkneading.

All the above faults are common when preparing and cooking fermented dough products. The likelihood of these faults occurring is considerably reduced if the following tips are followed:

  • Use only fresh ingredients, especially yeast
  • Follow the recipe weights and measures accurately
  • Mix the ingredients thoroughly
  • Prove the dough in a prover if available
  • Remember to knock back the dough and then allow to prove again
  • Bake at the correct temperature
  • Once cooked, turn out and allow the natural steams to escape
  • Store under the correct conditions to extend the life of the fermented dough product.

As we can see above there are many faults that can occur when making any fermented dough products. However, if we follow the simple tips above, most of the faults can easily never occur.

Knowledge and experience will help develop individual skills and support the delivery of quality fermented products for your customers.

Handout 3: Enriched dough – brioche

Brioche is an enriched dough bread from France, with a high egg and butter content. It has a long resting period of approximately six hours to allow the butter to harden to shape the dough before baking.

Ingredients

  • 400g strong flour
  • 125g butter
  • 25g yeast
  • 4 eggs
  • 35g caster sugar
  • 5g salt
  • 100ml milk

Method

  • Whisk the eggs together sugar and salt together.
  • Heat the milk to 37°C (body temperature), then dissolve the yeast in this. Incorporate some of the flour to make a batter and place in a warm area to ferment.
  • Sieve the flour and add the batter and egg mix to make a smooth dough.
  • Mix in the softened butter until the dough is once again smooth. Allow to rest for six hours in a cool place.
  • Knock the dough back and then shape as required. Allow it to prove again until doubled in size.
  • Cook in an oven at 200°C for 25 minutes.

There are various alternative takes on the basic brioche.

Brioche à tête

  • Divide the mix into 50g pieces. Remove 15g from each then shape into balls, placing the smaller one on top.
  • Place the buns in a tin and allow to prove until doubled in size. Egg wash carefully then cook at 220C until golden brown.
  • Remove from the oven then cool on a pastry wire.

Brioche Nanterre

  • Divide the brioche mix into 50g pieces, then shape the pieces into balls.
  • Lay the balls next to each other in a loaf tin and allow to prove until doubled in size.
  • Egg wash the dough then cook at 200°C for 25 minutes.
  • When cooked remove from the oven and allow to cool on a pastry wire.

This is a perfect sharing loaf.

Brioche can also be shaped as a plait or as a simple bun.

Major food chains are now using brioche as a matter of course instead of hamburger rolls.
