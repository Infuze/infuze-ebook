Introduction

Breads and other types of yeast goods are made and consumed all around the world.

Yeast products come in many types, can be sweet or savoury, and can be eaten as part of a meal at any time of the day.

The preparation and production of yeast goods are basic skills required by all bakers, pastry chefs and chefs.

Understanding the principles of production and the key steps in the preparation will ensure that you can produce successful products and avoid costly mistakes.

Techniques used to produce fermented doughs

The production of fermented doughs involves a number of techniques, including the following:

  • Weighing/measuring
  • Kneading
  • Developing
  • Proving
  • Knocking back
  • Scaling
  • Dividing
  • Shaping
  • Glazing
  • Dusting
  • Scoring
  • Steam injection
  • Demoulding.

Weighing/measuring

The weighing and measuring of commodities used in fermented doughs is essential if a quality product is to be achieved.

Wholemeal flours will absorb more water than other flours.

The temperature of the liquids is also key as too cold and the yeast may not activate, too hot and it may kill the yeast.

Kneading and developing

Kneading is the term used when mixing the commodities together.

This technique is used to develop the formation of gluten which provides the bread with its strength and structure, trapping the gases created from the fermentation to allow the dough to rise.

Knocking back

Once the dough has been kneaded it must left to rise until at least doubled in size.

The dough is then knocked back – this produces a smoother dough and allows for easier handling and shaping to form the finished product.

Proving

Proving is the second rising of the dough.

The proving process allows the dough to rest and for the fermentation process to occur again.

The dough is shaped ready for baking and is then rested.

It is essential not to over- or underprove the dough as this will affect the final product.

Scaling and dividing

Scaling and dividing both refer to the portion sizes required of the finished product.

This is best seen when scaling or dividing dinner rolls from a bread dough before proving and baking.

Commercially this is seen when making bread loaves and the bulk mix is divvied between the bread tins.

Shaping and scoring

The shaping of the dough is achieved through physical means – shaping a bread roll or naan bread with hands or by the use of moulds such as bread tins or savarin rings, or of cutters to make a bagel.

Scoring a dough before proving breaks the ‘skin’ that will form as it proves, allowing for further expansion. It is also aesthetically pleasing for the finish of the end product. This method is used in artisan cooking especially soda breads.

Glazing

Glazing of doughs is done to enhance the finish of the final product. There are several ingredients used in glazing a dough:

  • Milk and salt water – this will darken the final products crust.
  • Butter or other oils – this will change the flavour of the bread slightly and also provide a golden crust. A good example of this is an olive bread.
  • Egg glaze – using egg glazes will provide a glossy finish to the product. This glaze is also used when binding other commodities to the bread such as oats and seeds, as in a challah bread.

Other techniques used

  • Dusting – this is the technique of dusting a surface to prevent the dough sticking during the kneading process. It is also used as a decorative technique just before dough is baked, where a bread loaf is dusted with flour.
  • Steam injection – commercial ovens can produce steam in an oven whilst the baking process occurs. Steam injection ensures the air in the oven is moist, which in turn ensures the crust of the dough does not dry out and improves the quality of the final product.

Introduction

There are numerous commodities used when making fermented doughs. However, they all start from a base of one of the following ingredients:

  • Flour
  • Water
  • Yeast
  • Salt.

Other commodities can be added to form products know as enriched doughs:

  • Eggs
  • Fats
  • Sugar
  • Milk.

Types of yeast

There are three types of yeast available to make a dough.

Each of the yeasts can be used in much the same dishes as the other. The significant difference is the amount required in each case.

Fresh yeast 2% of the weight of the flour
Dried yeast 1% of the weight of the flour
Fast Action yeast 0.5% of the weight of the flour

Fermentation

Fermentation is the reaction process of the sugar and the yeast, producing carbon monoxide and alcohol. This provides the taste and rise in fermented doughs. In order for this process to work certain conditions are required:

  • Starch present in the flour provides the food for the yeast
  • Moisture is added at 37°C – too hot and the heat will kill the yeast, too cold and the yeast will not react
  • The dough should be worked on at 22–30°C for effective production
  • Time must be allowed for the yeast to grow and ferment.

Flour

It is essential to use the correct flour when making any fermented dough products:

  • A strong flour is required for breads – this flour is high in protein and gluten
  • During fermentation this strong flour provides the gluten to create the structure and strength of the dough.

Flour

Different types of flour have different rates of gluten extraction. The table below shows the amount of protein (which forms gluten) which can be obtained from each type of flour.

Weak (soft) flour 9% protein Used for sponges or short bread biscuits in which eggs provide additional structure
Medium flour 11% protein Used for yeast goods, such as Danish pastries
Strong (hard) flour 12% protein Used for bread and puff pastry
High protein 15% protein Used for pasta and high protein bread – durum wheat added to flour for this purpose

Salt

Salt has many effects on the fermentation process and this commodity should be used carefully.

  • Salt strengthens the gluten in flour and promotes crust and colour in yeast products.
  • Salt must be weighed carefully, as more than 2% of salt in bread will retard the yeast too much and affect the end product
  • Salt retards fermentation so care has to be taken when making yeast products to prevent direct contact of the yeast with the salt.

Sugars

Sugars are required for the fermentation process to allow the creation of the carbon monoxide and alcohol. Sugar also:

  • raises the temperature – this is necessary for gluten to coagulate and thus increases the volume
  • retains moisture which adds to the texture of the finished product.

Water

Water is a requirement of the basic dough but care must be taken when adding this to the mixture.

  • Different types of flour absorb more or less water. For example, flour such as wholemeal will absorb more water than strong white flour.
  • The temperature of any water added should be 37ºC to prevent any negative effect on the yeast and fermentation process.

Eggs

Eggs are required when producing enriched doughs and therefore will impact on the fermentation process.

  • The protein content of eggs will set during baking, helping to provide structure in the product

The eggs will also:

  • improve the crust colour
  • improve the crumb colour.

Fats

As the fat prevents the gluten from forming long strands, it is also referred to as ‘shortening’. Most pastry fats used in baking are based on vegetable oils that have been hydrogenised to keep them firm at room temperature. Fats will also:

  • add to the flavour
  • aerate the dough
  • tenderise and enhance the texture.
