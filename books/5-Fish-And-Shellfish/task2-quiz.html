  1. Scale and gut the fish, then use coloured or seafood-only cutting boards for processing the fish.
  2. Remove the fins and fillets (meat from bone) and if desired, skin it.
  3. Place the fillets onto a perforated tray to allow the melted ice and fluids to drip off.
  4. Cover or vacuum pack and top with shaved ice, then refrigerate.

Cut Description
Fillet
Flesh of the fish is completely removed from the bone and most often skinned; it is generally accepted that there will be no bones when served, so remove pin bones.
Délice
Fillet is folded. Can have fillings or farces.
Paupiette
Fillet is rolled. Can have a farce on the skin side.
Goujon
Strips cut diagonally across the fillet approximately 50mm x 10mm x 10mm.
Suprême
Literally translated as ‘the best’. A larger fillet is cut on a slant to make it look bigger.
Darne
Cut through the bone of a whole round fish. Usually referred to as a cutlet.
Tronçon
Cut through the bone of a whole flat fish.

QUESTION 3

Provide a brief explanation for each of the preservation methods for seafood:

Enter your answers in the textbox below then check by clicking 'REVEAL'

Salting:

Cured using salt, dry versus wet (brine), this will draw out the moisture contained within the seafood

Smoking:

Usually pre-cured before smoking, then either hot or cold smoked over wood chips, which add flavour and colour

Pickling/marinade:

Use of vinegar or soy sauce, coconut milk and lime to cure the flesh

Canning:

Seafood cooked in a can/jar to seal it hermetically

Chilling:

Use of ice to extend lifespan

Vacuum packing:

Packed into specialist bags, often with flavouring and sealed to minimises oxidisation
