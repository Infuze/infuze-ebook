Introduction to seafood

Seafood is a very important part of any menu, providing excellent variety across a range of service styles and periods.

Seafood is popular with customers, especially as it is often not cooked at home.

From traditional starters, such as smoked salmon and prawn salad, to more elaborate items such as lobster ravioli in tomato consommé or the traditional seafood platter, there is a mouth-watering selection of cold and hot delicacies.

Seafood – nutrition

Why should we include seafood in our diet?

  • Seafood is very nutritious as it has high protein content, contains all essential amino acids and is an excellent source of omega-3 fatty acids. The omega-3 fatty acids are polyunsaturated and have been shown to have beneficial effects on human blood fat levels, heart disease and blood pressure.
  • Seafood is also low in cholesterol. It is easy to digest as the protein in seafood is broken down and absorbed by the body more easily than the protein in red meats and poultry. Fish contain between 17% and 25% protein; on average the protein content is about 19%.
  • Seafood is very low in fat (1–5% fat depending on the type). Oily fish such as salmon and trout contain fats and oils within their flesh and skin, and usually have a higher fat content than most whitefish such as cod, which only contain oil in their livers. 

Seafood – nutrition

  • Seafood is a source of vitamin A, D and the B complex vitamins niacin (B3), B6 and B12.
  • Minerals such as calcium, zinc, iron, copper, potassium, iodine, phosphorus and selenium make seafood an important food source.
  • Fresh seafood is also low in sodium, however smoked and processed seafood can have high levels of salt added during processing. Seafood forms an important part of a balanced diet and many bodies recommend eating seafood at least twice a week.

Selecting seafood – important factors to consider

  • Sustainability – check whether seafood is harvested responsibly and not endangered as a species
  • Enterprise and customer expectations – standard of product and ‘green’ expectations of customer
  • Availability and type required – local supplies and food miles, lesser known varieties
  • Quality, price and market fluctuations – fish of the day, regular menu changes.

Visit http://www.goodfishguide.org/ to see the importance of seafood sustainability.

Choosing the most appropriate species
Being aware of the classification of seafood, quality indicators, prices and seasonal impacts on price and availability is important for the competent chef.

Classification of seafood

Seafood is classified into three major groups:

  • Fish (fin)
  • Shellfish
  • Other

These groups have sub-classifications to group similar species. These classifications will help you with your cookery methods and menu writing, since common principles apply.

Fish sub-classification

Fish are sub-classified into two major groups: bony fish and cartilaginous fish. Bony fish have a skeleton and the flesh is attached to the bones. Typical examples are trout and salmon.

Cartilaginous fish have no skeleton but cartilage that provides the structure of the fish. Sharks and rays are examples of this type of fish. The advantage of these species is that you end up with boneless fillets.

Fish can further be classified in a number of ways depending on preference:

  • Freshwater and saltwater fish
  • Flat and round fish
  • White and oily fish.

Freshwater fish

Freshwater fish incorporate all varieties that live in fresh water such as:

  • trout
  • salmon
  • tench
  • carp
  • eel
  • bream.

Most freshwater fish are oily fish.

Saltwater fish

Saltwater fish include any variety that lives in the ocean and estuaries. Many of these fish are white-fleshed fish and have a clean, fresh taste. These include:

  • sole
  • cod
  • snapper.

However, many cheaper species are oily fish and have a softer texture and slightly stronger taste. These include

  • herring
  • mullet.

The ‘king of oily fish’, yellow fin tuna, is used for sashimi due to its outstanding taste and achieves very high prices, particularly in the Japanese fish markets.

Flat and round fish

Round fish are those that are symmetrical and have eyes on both sides of their heads. They have a fillet on either side of their body, which are identical in size. Typical examples are trout, salmon, snapper, cod and herring.

In contrast, flat fish are generally born shaped like a round fish but one eye migrates to the other side of the head and the body flattens out. Typical examples are sole, flounder and turbot.

Flat fish are usually bottom dwellers, usually burrow in the sand and have protective colouring on their skin. The skin is mottled and darker on the top where the eyes are and very light on the underside. They have four fillets – two on top and two underneath. The top fillets are thicker and are usually referred to as the primary fillets. The fillets of the white side are thinner and are called secondary fillets.

The fillets vary in size and should be mixed for starters with one primary plus one secondary fillet per serving to even out the weight.

Shellfish sub-classification

Shellfish are marine animals that have a protective shell or exoskeleton. They can be sub-classified into crustaceans and molluscs.

  • Crustaceans have an external skeleton for protection, are usually dark green to coppery in colour and live along the coastline and estuary regions.
  • Molluscs are invertebrates which means they possess no backbone. They are instead protected by an outer shell or are soft-bodied. One particular sub-classification of molluscs is the cephalopod. Common cephalopod species are squid, octopus and cuttlefish. They usually have a prominent head with tentacles or arms.

In general shellfish live in coastal and estuarine regions. However, some such as the Alaskan king crab and scallops live on continental shelves far out to sea.

Other seafood types, classification and quality points

  • Bêche de mer is also referred to as sea cucumber or trepan and is mainly used for Asian dishes. Only the roe is eaten. It varies in colour from yellow to orange. The finer the grain the better the quality. Used for sushi and in Asian dishes and sauces.
  • Sea urchin is prepared using a heavy glove to protect the hand from spines. With the point of a pair of scissors cut a circle large enough to spoon out the roe meat inside. Make sure that none of the shell or the spines are included. Process as required afterwards.

Introduction to seafood quality points

Seafood can be purchased fresh, live or frozen. Seafood quality indicators differ for fresh, live and frozen seafood and between fish, crustaceans and molluscs. In general:

  • fresh seafood quality signs are based on appearance, odour, texture and taste
  • live seafood quality can be checked for smell, movement, bruising or injuries and relative weight to size, as live seafood will lose weight as it no longer feeds
  • frozen seafood must be inspected for freezer burn and date of purchase, ‘use by’ date and appropriate storage and delivery.

Live seafood

Live seafood is preferred by many connoisseurs, and many Asian restaurants are renowned for their live seafood available for direct orders by the customers. Waste can also be reduced as the fish is kept alive until ordered but any faulty equipment can cause considerable costs if the seafood dies.

Note: To kill live seafood humanely you need to minimise suffering. Fish from a tank should be stunned with a quick blow to the head or brain spiked, then the intestine removed. Crustaceans should be placed in the freezer, ice slurry or freshwater to slow body functions. Then submerge the head into boiling liquid, brain spike or cut through the longitudinal mid-line to kill the seafood quickly.

Fresh seafood quality indicators

  • Handling can affect some quality signs so you need to examine carefully to get an actual indication of freshness.
  • Sometimes you might find that the gills are discoloured or the eyes dull, the fish has been handled poorly or bruised and suffered in quality.
  • It is still fine for consumption. However it is now of secondary quality and the purchase price and selling price must reflect that.

Frozen seafood quality

The quality of frozen seafood deteriorates fairly rapidly and frozen seafood should be used up within 1-3 months.

Crustaceans may dry out quicker and therefore may need more frequent turnaround cycles.

Crustacean quality indicators

Note: Fish processors generally will not accept shellfish from fishermen if they are missing more than one or two limbs.

If green crustaceans are discoloured with dark spots, it is known as melanosis. This is due to oxidisation of the crustacean and does not initially affect eating quality. However, once it has started it it will progress rapidly and change the flavour of the meat.

Peel the crustacean and cook it to avoid further deterioration. It is advisable to store fresh green prawns or other crustaceans in ice slurry to prevent oxygen from affecting the seafood.

Mollusc quality points

Mollusc quality indicators Good quality

Bivalves
  • Bivalves such as mussels should be closed by the natural reaction of their muscles.
  • Two exceptions are NZ green lip mussels, which are frozen before transportation and thus have been killed and oysters that have been opened beforehand.
  • Should be free of dirt, mud or barnacles.
  • Must have a clean seafood smell.

Soft-bodied molluscs
  • Ink fish can be judged by smell in regards to their quality.
  • If they start to smell ‘milky’ or of ammonia they are possibly off, in which case you must discard them.
  • The flesh of squid should be firm to the touch and untenderised octopus flesh should be springy. The surface should look glossy.

Mollusc quality indicators

Molluscs are filter feeders, which mean they filter food particles from the water that surrounds them. If the water is impure, the mollusc will contain possible poisonous substances.

Several oyster scares have been associated with health risks in the past. For example, an outbreak of gastroenteritis was caused by river oysters which had become polluted after heavy rain.

Introduction to fish species

There are thousands of different edible fish species around the world. The availability of different fish will depend on where you are. However, with modern transportation links much of the fish can be freshly packed and flown around the world making it readily available to be used in the kitchen. This handout explores a number of common edible fish.

Anchovy is a small fish. Salted and canned in brine it is consumed all over the world. Native to all seas, it prefers temperate waters. There are about 140 different species of anchovies.

Angler fish are a group of fish found all over the world. The name comes from a kind of fleshy rod protruding from the head acting as a lure.

Bass are a number of species that are both freshwater- and saltwater-dwelling. Bass belongs to the perciformes family. It exists in all continents except Antarctica. The big brother in the family is the ‘grouper’ which can weigh over 100kg.

Billfish include the species swordfish, sailfish and marlin. They all have a sword-like beak used for hunting. They are a migratory species that are found in temperate to tropical waters around the world.

Bluefish is found in temperate to tropical waters around the world. It is a popular edible fish.

Bream are a group of both freshwater- and saltwater-dwelling fish. The ‘gilt head bream’ is common in the Mediterranean is the most important for food. The freshwater bluegill is common in North America.

Carp include several species. Carp have been cultivated for thousands of years and are native to Europe and Asia. They are a favourite with the eastern European community.

Catfish are a number of freshwater species that vary a lot in size. They are native to all continents except Antarctica and have been cultivated for hundreds of years.

Char are closely related to salmon and trout. Char dwell in really cold freshwater and are native to North America, Europe and Asia. In culinary terms, they are one of the most appreciated edible fish.

Cod is one of the UK’s more popular fish. It can be eaten dried, smoked or fresh. It is a collective name for a number of different species found in the Northern Atlantic, the Arctic and the Western Pacific oceans. An alternative to cod, you can use pollock or coley which are similar in taste and flavour. The commercial fishing of the Atlantic cod species has led to overfishing which has created problems in supply.

Common ling is a large fish native to the North Atlantic.

Common sole is native to the Eastern North Atlantic as far as the Mediterranean. It’s a very popular edible fish but the meat is expensive.

Eel consists of over 800 species found in freshwater and saltwater all over the world. It looks snake-like. Many times the term refer to eels of the species anguillidae that live in freshwater and procreate by migrating to certain saltwater areas. The biggest species are morays.

Flounders are several species of flatfish native to the Northern Atlantic and the Pacific.

Haddock is a popular food fish dwelling in the North Atlantic.

Halibut is native to the the North Pacific and the North Atlantic. It can weigh up to 300kg. It is often confused with the flounder although they are not the same fish.

Herring are various species native to the North Atlantic and the North Pacific. Smoked, salted, fried and pickled, herring has proved an important staple food.

Hilsa is a saltwater- and freshwater-dwelling species. It is only found in Bangladesh and on the Indian subcontinent where it is commonly eaten.

Lophius are various species native to the coasts off Europe. Strange and sinister-looking they are known as ‘monkfish’, ‘goosefish’ and ‘sea devils’. In Sweden fishermen used to fill them with stones and throw them back in, believing they were bad luck. Today they are prized for their tasty meat.

Mackerel is a collective name for number of species, most of them of the scombridae family that include bonito and king mackerel. They are found in temperate and tropical seas around the world.

Patagonian toothfish is an endangered species. They dwell in temperate to cold southern oceans in depths of up to almost 4,000 metres.

Perch is a large family containing among many others walleye and pike-perch. They are freshwater-dwelling and found around the world.

Pike is a number of species found in North America, Europe and Russia. Freshwater dwelling, it is also found in the brackish Baltic Sea.

Plaice are a species of flatfish native to the northern hemisphere. They are a popular food fish.

Salmon are a large species of the salmonidae family that migrate between saltwater and freshwater. The steelhead kind is most common in Europe. Domestic to the Northern hemisphere, they are also cultivated in Chile and Australia.

Sardines or pilchards are several species of small oily fish. They are most commonly eaten canned in brine or grilled and native to all oceans.

Snakehead is a group of freshwater-dwelling fish. They were originally native to Africa and Asia but have spread around the world.

Snapper are several species, the most famous being red snapper. They are domestic to tropical and sub-tropical waters around the world.

Tilapia is a generic term for hundreds of species of freshwater fish with African origins. They have been cultivated for thousands of years and thus have been spread around the world. They only live in warm water.

Trout are the freshwater-dwelling species of the salmonidae family. They are native to Europe, Asia and North America although they have been spread to other continents by sport fishing enthusiasts.

Tuna are several species of fish belonging to the scombridae family. A very important commercial fish, several species are endangered. Domestic to all oceans, they prefer temperate and tropical waters. They have recently started to be bred commercially.

Introduction to shellfish species and other seafood

There are hundreds of different edible shellfish species around the world. The availability of the shellfish will depend on where you are. However, with modern transportation links much of the shellfish can be freshly packed and flown around the world making it readily available to be used in the kitchen.

Shellfish

Barnacles are species belonging to the family cirripedia.

Clawed lobsters are saltwater-dwelling species only found in the Atlantic north of the equator. Prized for their good taste.

Crab is a group of several thousand species. Living in saltwater or freshwater around the world. Commercially very important. The largest is the red king crab.

Crayfish are several species living in freshwater. They are related to true lobster.

Langoustine, also called spiny lobster or rock lobster, are a group of crustaceans related to true lobster. They are found in warm waters in all oceans.

Mantis shrimp is not a shrimp but belongs to another family of crustaceans. Found in oceans around the world, it lives in warm waters.

Prawn is a collective name for various species that can be saltwater or freshwater dwellers. Found in all oceans, few are commercially fished in Europe where shrimp are dominant. The big tiger prawn and the Atlantic white shrimp are common. Many species are cultivated. The terms ‘shrimp’ and ‘prawn’ are often used interchangeably although biologically there are differences.

Shrimp is a collective term for various species. They can live in fresh- or saltwater. They are found all over the world and are also farmed in many places.

Slipper lobsters are a number of species found in all oceans. They are sometimes called ‘bugs’ since they resemble insects.

Squat lobster is the name for a number of species. Found all over the world in saltwater water.

Other seafood

Abalone are large sea snails found in oceans all over the world. They dwell best in cold waters. Most are found off Australia, New Zealand, South Africa, Western US and Japan. They are also a successfully farmed seafood.

Chiton are a species of primitive shellfish. They are found worldwide in oceans and are traditionally eaten by indigenous people in the Americas.

Clam are bivalves that burrow in sediment, which separates them from bivalves that cling to other things or that swim freely (which would be scallops).

Cockles are a group of clams that belong to the cardiidae family. Found all over the world, they are a very popular food in China.

Cuttlefish are related to squids and octopuses. They are found in saltwaters in East and South Asia, Europe, Africa and Australia.

Geoducks are large saltwater clams. A fleshy member can protrude up to 1 metre. Native to Northwestern North America, they have become very popular in East Asia.

Mussels are several species of clams that all have an almost oval shape. Brown and blue mussels are the common commercial species which are also cultivated. They thrive best in temperate waters and are native to all oceans.

Octopuses are a group of molluscs that have eight arms. They are quite intelligent and commonly can use ink as a defence mechanism while fleeing. The larger species can have an arm span of over 4 metres and weigh up to 70kg. They are found in all oceans and are readily caught as food.

Oysters are several species belonging to the ostreidae family. Many are eaten raw or cooked. They are found in saltwaters and semi-saltwaters in all oceans. They are also cultivated on a big scale.

Scallops are found in all oceans and are cultivated. All scallops are migratory which means they don´t burrow themselves but swim around. They are prized food.

Sea cucumbers are found in all oceans. They are readily eaten and very popular in East Asia. They are cultivated, mainly in China.

Sea snails are part of a huge family that includes land-dwelling species as well. The abalone is the most famous but many others are harvested and eaten throughout the world. They are found in all habitats including oceans and freshwater.

Sea urchins belong to a big family called echinoidea. They are found in all oceans and are a popular seafood.

Squid belongs to a family called teuthida. Erroneously they are often described as having 10 arms, separating them from octopuses. However, that is only true for some of them; most of them have eight arms and two long tentacles. They are not as intelligent as octopuses and they don’t have ink bladders. The biggest species are deep sea-dwelling and can weigh up to 500kg. They are found in all oceans.
