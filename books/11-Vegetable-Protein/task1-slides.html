Introduction to Vegetables Proteins

Vegetables Proteins are used extensively on menus as a meat replacement and in most cases are suitable for all types of vegetarian diets.

They provide colour, texture and balanced flavours and supply the essential protein nutritional balance to a vegetarian meal.

Quality is an extremely important consideration as the colour, dietary value and customer appeal deteriorate incrementally with a lower quality product.

Vegetable protein is becoming popular using soy beans, or soy bean products such as tofu.

If you're attempting to eat less meat in your diet or adapt dishes to meet the needs of a diverse customer base, vegetable protein can be incorporated into a number of main dishes without much change in the texture or flavour of the dish.

Types of Vegetables Protein

Vegetables Proteins can be classified into different groups. They can be distinguished quite easily through their appearance and different textures.

Soya Quorn Tempeh Seitan Tofu - both firm and soft

Soya

Textured Vegetable protein is also known as TVP. It is also known as Textured Soy Protein (TSP), soy meat, or soya chunks is a defatted soy flour product, a by-product of extracting soybean oil.

It is often used as a meat analogue or meat extender. It is quick to cook, with a protein content comparable to certain meat.

Textured soy protein maybe made into unflavoured or flavoured, meat or chicken flavoured, and appear in chunks, slices, mince, flakes, crumbles or bits.

Quorn

Most people think ‘Quorn’ is a type of vegetable protein, when in fact it is sold as a retail brand product.

All Quorn foods contain mycoprotein as an ingredient, which is derived from the Fusarium Venenatum fungus and is grown by fermentation. In most Quorn products, the fungus culture is dried and mixed with egg albumen, which acts as a binder, and then is adjusted in texture and pressed into various forms.

Tempeh

Tempeh is a Indonesian staple, made by fermenting soybeans in banana leaves until a firm, earthy patty forms, has been a common meat substitute since around the 12th century.

Tempeh contains more protein and dietary fibre than traditional tofu, and its mild nutty flavour works great in a wide array of recipes.

Seitan

Seitan is also known as wheat gluten, seitan first appeared during the sixth century as an ingredient in Asian cuisine and has been a common meat substitute for more than a thousand years.

Traditionally, seitan was the product of rinsing and cooking wheat dough to remove the starch, leaving a protein-dense substance that turned out to be an excellent meat substitute/alternative. It can be used to create delicious mock meats.

You can also buy wheat gluten powder which is protein-packed, cholesterol-free, and low-carb.

Tofu - both firm and soft

Tofu is also known as bean or soya curd, tofu is a soft, cheese-like food made of condensed soy milk pressed into tight blocks and mixed with nigari. While this product can be rather bland, tofu easily absorbs flavours of other ingredients, such as spices.

Tofu has been a staple in Asian countries for more than 2,000 years!

Because of its versatility, tofu can be used in a multitude of dishes to make vegan versions of omelettes, desserts, cheeses, and smoothies.

Storage of Vegetables Proteins

Storage of Vegetables, Fruit and Nuts

  • Fresh Vegetables proteins such as tofu should be stored in their original containers in the fridge or cool room.
  • Dried Vegetable protein that are dehydrated and require rehydrated, should be kept in dry storage.
  • Prevent unnecessary handling of vegetables proteins.
  • Vegetable proteins are often high in fat content and are therefore perishable and can become rancid quickly in hot weather. They should be stored under the correct conditions.
  • The available kitchen, storage and fridge / cool room space, as well as the size of the menu and the number of kitchen staff will influence how you purchase vegetables proteins.

Storage Considerations for vegetable proteins

  • In general, fresh vegetables proteins can be stored for a week to a fortnight in the fridge / cool room.
  • Dried vegetables proteins need to be stored in their original containers or in sealed bins.
  • Most frozen vegetables proteins are fine for 3-6 months at -18°C.
  • To minimise handling, store the produce in the containers in which they have been delivered. Move all stock to comply with HACCP regulations. Consider contamination issues such as wooden crates.

Storage Considerations for vegetable proteins

  • Handle all produce with care.
  • Temperature control is also important.
  • When receiving ingredients, ensure that you verify that the delivery reflects the delivery paperwork/document. Take random checks of the quality of the product (including from the bottom of the box), sign the paperwork if it is correct and then store the stock as quickly as possible to minimise spoilage and bacterial growth.
  • Chef’s hint: Cold air sinks, so the coldest part of your refrigerator is on the bottom shelf. In a cool room, the shelf furthest from the door and nearest the floor will be the coldest spot

Temperature

The following needs to be considered for all vegetable protein ingredients and dishes:

  • Ideally used fresh, if not:
    • Must be chilled to below 5°C in 90 minutes
    • Stored labelled in a fridge
    • If freezing must be -18°C or below
  • If cooked vegetable proteins ingredients and dishes are stored in a freezer must be cooked to the legal required temperature for 2 minutes before use.
  • Must not be reheated more than once.
  • Hot holding must be above 63°C

Positioning & stock rotation

When storing any vegetable proteins ingredients and dishes, you must always consider the possibility of cross contamination, all vegetable proteins dishes not for immediate use should be:

  • Covered to prevent cross contamination
  • Stored in a fridge or freezer
  • Labelled to ensure stock rotation, “first in first out”
  • Stored on the top shelf to prevent spillage

Date labelling and coverings

When labelling vegetable proteins and dishes, the following guidelines should be followed:

  • Name of product
  • Date of production
