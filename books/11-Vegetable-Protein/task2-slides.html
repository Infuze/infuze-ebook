Preparation methods of vegetable proteins

  • Health, safety and hygiene requirements must always be considered during the preparation of vegetable proteins. Cutting and cooking food always involves some risks, however, these can be minimised by supervision, training, reinforcement and following correct procedures.
  • Tofu takes on the flavours that it is cooked with or marinated with, so adding spices, herbs and flavours will change a bland tasting product into a versatile ingredient.
  • Many vegetable proteins dishes will use vegetables along with the vegetable proteins to make the dishes.
  • You must also consider, washing vegetables for use in vegetable protein dishes prior to use is essential for removing dust, bacteria, pesticides, wax and other contaminants.

Preparation methods of vegetable proteins

  • One cup boiling or hot water or vegetable stock poured over 1 cup of small TVP granules will reconstitute in five to 10 minutes. For larger chunks or slices, use 2 cups of liquid to 1 cup of chunks/slices and simmer for 20-30 minutes until tender, but not mushy. TVP or TSP granules or flakes can be rehydrated in a microwave, by covering the moistened granules and cooking on high for about five minutes.
  • TVP or TSP will equal about 1:2 in weight of minced lamb/beef once it has been fully soaked i.e. 1kg of TVP will represent 2kg of traditional meat once fully reconstituted. Unflavoured textured soy protein easily assumes the flavour of other liquids and foods. As you soak the TVP, it will become plump and look more like real meat or poultry.
  • It can also be combined with normal meat or poultry to boast protein values for non-vegetarians.

Tofu

  • When it comes to texture, tofu is incredibly versatile. A simple way to alter the consistency is by slicing the tofu before pressing, as opposed to pressing the whole block. This will push a lot more water out of the tofu, making the tofu pieces considerably firmer.
  • Switching up the shapes and sizes of tofu pieces before pressing can also really have an effect on texture.
  • Marinating pressed tofu for at least 30 minutes will ensure it soaks up strong flavoured marinades, such as curry pastes, soy sauce, garlic or heavily spiced mixes. This gives time for the flavours to penetrate into the nooks and crannies of the tofu.

Vegetable proteins used as stuffings

Stuffing vegetables such as courgettes, peppers or tomatoes means they need to be de-seeded or hollowed out first. Flat mushrooms can also be stuffed and then grilled. Vegetarian stuffings may include:

  • Tofu
  • Herbs
  • Tomatoes
  • Quorn
  • TVP
  • Rice

Coatings

Coating a vegetable protein such as tofu can improve the texture, taste and colour of a vegetables protein, the can be coated in:

  • Breadcrumbs including Panko Japanese breadcrumbs
  • Seasoned flour
  • Sweet batter – i.e. cider batter
  • Savoury batter – beer/tempura batter

Equipment needed for vegetable protein dishes

  • Equipment use has to be considered in the context of the establishment. Design the menu with the available equipment in mind. Always plan your menu so you use a range of cookery methods which utilise different equipment, to spread out the load. This also allows the usage of different staff skill levels as deep-fried tempura is more difficult to cook than TVP spaghetti bolognaise.
  • Large equipment used for in the cooking of vegetables proteins dishes such as:
    • Bratt pans are used for the production of large portions of vegetable protein dishes such as TVP curries.
    • Pots are used for boiling and blanching, whilst frying pans are perfect for all stir-fry items.
    • The deep-fryer for fried vegetable protein dishes such as deep fried tofu and other fried items.
    • The bain-marie is used to keep sauces and batches of vegetable proteins warm during service.
    • Grills and salamanders are common to finish items such as TVP moussaka whilst the stove or solid top or gas burner is used for all pan based dishes.

Equipment needed for vegetable protein preparation

  • Tools used in the preparation are cutting boards, knives and special cutting tools like a parisienne cutter for specific shapes.
  • Bowls and containers are used for storage and batters. These also require whisks and spiders, firstly in the production, secondly when removing fried items or spare batter.
  • Palette knives and spatulas are good for lifting delicate items such as tofu, whilst ladles are needed for sauces.
  • Tools vary based on the finishes of the food items. Vegetable proteins are often chopped using knives or can also be blended or minced.
  • Rolling pins may be needed for dishes that are encased in pastry such vegetable wellington
  • Trays are used for crumbing, or drawing items through a crust.

Cookery Methods used for Vegetable Proteins

The restaurant industry is very competitive and often the little touches provided on a plate make all the difference. Cooking vegetables differently can provide an inexpensive option for differentiating your establishment.

  • Nutrition – steamed vegetable protein dishes retain more nutrients, such as steamed oriental vegetables and vegetable protein with a honey and soy glaze and steamed rice.
  • Eye appeal – glazed vegetable protein dishes are glossy and look appetising, such as soy glazed tofu. Coat the tofu in a sauce then reducing the amount of liquid by heating either in a sauté pan or in the oven to drive off the moisture and concentrate the flavours.

Cookery Methods used for Vegetable Proteins

  • Colour retention – blanching vegetables and then adding vegetable protein maintains and even enhances their colour.
  • Flavour – vegetable protein with a combination of spices and herbs, e.g. in ratatouille, provides satisfying taste.
  • Contrast – utilising different cookery methods enhances the final menu item
  • Always select the most suitable equipment, i.e. smaller pots for smaller amounts.

Cooking Tofu

Boiling

  • Pre-boiling tofu can help create a slightly tougher texture which maybe suitable for some recipies. Place the tofu cubes or slices in well salted boiling water for 5-10 minutes. Drain and gently squeeze the water from the tofu with kitchen roll to remove as much water as possible, then use as required in the dish.
  • Using this method will lightly season your tofu throughout – just remember not to add too much extra salt during or after cooking.

Cookery Methods used for Vegetable Proteins

Steamed

  • Vegetable proteins such as tempeh or tofu can be bitter straight out of the package, so steaming them can help. Place sliced tempeh of tofu on a tray in a saucepan and cover with a small amount water or vegetable broth at the bottom of the pan. Bring liquid to a boil, reduce heat, and simmer for 10 minutes.
  • Then remove and proceed with marinating, grilling, baking, or otherwise seasoning and cooking.

Cookery Methods used for Vegetable Proteins

Grilling or Pan-frying or BBQ

  • Thinly sliced. Grilling or pan-frying tempeh until the edges turn crispy enhances its natural nutty flavours usually associated with vegetable proteins.
  • Different recipes call for various slice sizes and techniques, but in general slice tempeh or tofu to about 0.5-1cm thick. At this thickness, they can acquire just the right amount of crispiness on the edges while retaining a nice, chewy interior.

Cookery Methods used for Vegetable Proteins

Stewed and Braised

Crumbled, minced or cubed vegetable proteins can be cooked in sauces and stews. The vegetable protein will take on the flavour of whatever it's cooked in, making it a versatile way to add heartiness and protein to a vegan or vegetarian meal. It can be crumbled, grated, or cubed to a sauce, stew, chili, or curry.

Cookery Methods used for Vegetable Proteins

Deep-/Shallow-frying

The majority of vegetable proteins are suitable for deep-/Shallow-frying. They can be coated, through dry and/or wet ingredients, such as flour, egg, breadcrumbs, batter, etc.

There are a few reasons for coating; the coating applied to the vegetable proteins acts as a barrier that keeps the item from overcooking as it cooks; it enables the exterior of the food to become crisp and darken evenly without burning; and it prevents the texture of the food from becoming tough.​

Before coating, the vegetable protein should first be lightly dried by patting the food item with paper towels or left to drain to absorb excess moisture.

Cookery Methods used for Vegetable Proteins

Stir-Frying

The stir-frying method of cooking is widely used in Asian cooking and is said to encourage the sealing in of the flavours, nutrients, as well as adding colour to the dish. Most vegetable proteins can be stir-fried in one form or another, either from fresh or after they have been marinated, cooked and refreshed.​

Handout 1 – Sweet Ginger, Garlic and Chilli Tofu Ramen Noodles Recipe

Sweet Ginger, Garlic and Chilli Tofu Ramen Noodles – Serves 4

Ingredients

  • 400g Tofu, cut into cubes approximately 1 cm thick
  • 50g Fresh Ginger, julienne
  • 2 x cloves of Garlic, crushed
  • 2 tablespoons of sweet honey
  • 1 Litre vegetable bouillon
  • 1 tbsp miso paste
  • 300g ramen noodles
  • 4 tbsp sweetcorn kernels
  • 2 handfuls of beansprouts
  • 2 x spring onions, finely sliced
  • 2 handfuls of baby spinach
  • 2 x red chillies, finely sliced
  • Bunch of fresh coriander, chopped

Method

  • Heat the vegetable bouillon in a large pan and add the Ginger, Garlic and Sweet honey, the miso paste, noodles, sweetcorn kernels, beansprouts and onion.
  • Cook for 2 minutes then stir in the Tofu.
  • Cook for a further 2 minutes until warmed through.
  • Add the spinach just before serving and garnish with sliced red chillies and fresh coriander. Season to taste with salt and freshly ground black pepper.

Handout 4 – Textured Vegetable Protein Mince Wellington Recipe

Textured Vegetable Protein Mince Wellington – Serves 4

Ingredients

  • 350g Textured Vegetable Protein Mince
  • 300g Cooked White beans, roughly chopped
  • 1 tbsp Vegetable Oil
  • 1 large white onion, finely diced
  • 3 carrots, diced
  • 2 cloves of garlic, finely chopped
  • 200g mushrooms, cleaned and diced
  • 200ml vegetable stock
  • 3g porcini mushrooms, finely chopped and added to the vegetable stock to rehydrate
  • 200ml white wine
  • 100ml single cream
  • ¼ tsp chilli flakes (if required)
  • Salt and black pepper to taste
  • 100g fresh breadcrumbs
  • 1 egg, beaten
  • 2 tbsp fresh parsley, finely chopped
  • 1 x 500g block ready to roll puff pastry
  • 1 egg, beaten to glaze the pastry

Method

  • Preheat the oven to 200°C
  • Heat the oil in a large pan and gently cook the carrots and onions for 5 minutes until softened but not browned. Add the garlic and mushrooms and cook for a further 5 minutes. Stir in the vegetable stock, porcini mushrooms and white wine.
  • Bring to the boil then simmer for around 5 minutes until the liquid has reduced by half. Add the cream and chilli flakes cook until the liquid has reduced by half again. Season with salt and black pepper. Remove from the heat
  • Stir in the Textured Vegetable Protein Mince, chopped white beans, breadcrumb, egg and parsley
  • Roll out the pastry into a rectangle shape to approximately half a centimetre thickness and carefully spoon the mixture into the centre of the pastry rectangle. Draw the furthest edge of the pastry over the mince mix towards you forming a tube and carry on gently rolling until the pastry completely covers the mince and it is possible to tuck the edge of the pastry underneath the roll. Fold the outer edges of the tubes inwards to seal the ends and then place on a greased baking tray.
  • Brush generously with beaten egg and bake in the centre of the oven for 30-35 minutes until golden brown
