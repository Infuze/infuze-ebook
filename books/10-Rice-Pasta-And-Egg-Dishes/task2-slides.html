Pilaf

  • Method found in Turkey, the Middle East, Central Asia and India. Also known as pilau or pulao in different cultures. It is a process of cooking long grain rice.
  • Ratio of 1:1.5 or 1:2 depending on the quality and starchiness of the rice. It is a braised rice dish that uses stock or broth.
  • Diced onion is sautéed in butter and the rice is added off the heat and stirred through to coat all the grains to prevent them sticking together. Hot stock or broth and other flavourings (bay leaf, saffron, spices) are added. It’s then covered with foil or a lid then placed into the oven at 15°C or cooked slowly on a simmer plate.
  • Pilaf is used as an accompaniment and is ideal for function service, as the absorption method reduces the amount of equipment needed.

Chinese-style boiled or steamed rice

  • Slightly sticky, which helps when eating with chopsticks.
  • Cooked like pilaf rice but without the addition of fat (see below for method).
  • It is important to wash the rice to remove excess starch but again the quality of the rice plays a role and may mean that rinsing is not necessary.

The first knuckle rule: Touch your finger to the surface of the rice in a pot. Then add water until it reaches the first knuckle/joint of your finger.

The rice is now brought to the boil and then turned down to a simmer. Do not stir the rice or take the lid off. After 10 minutes, remove from the heat and let it steam for another five minutes. Fork the rice through to separate the grains. This principle is also used in modern rice cookers.

Fried rice

Fried rice is a common accompaniment in Chinese cooking. Other Asian cuisines have their own versions.

  • Minimises waste as utilises steamed rice from the previous day, as well as adding other leftovers to provide extra flavours and nutrients.
  • Cut all ingredients such as vegetables, cooked meat leftovers, ham and peeled prawns into even size and stir fry until just cooked through.
  • Add the rice, toss through then season with soy sauce.
  • Rice needs to be cold before frying to prevent the soft outside of the grains absorbing moisture and making the finished product sticky.
  • Fried egg and spring onions are used for garnish or mixed through the rice.
  • It can be used as an accompaniment or a dish by itself.

Risotto

  • A classical Italian rice dish cooked by absorption.
  • Flavours are obtained from the stock used in cooking the risotto, and the additional ingredients such as bay leaf, mushrooms and wine.
  • Risotto is eaten more in the north of Italy, whilst the south eats more pasta.
  • Risotto is made with short grain rice:
    • Arborio (most commonly variety, traditionally used in Italy but can become more gooey if overcooked)
    • Carnaroli (excellent absorption rate but still maintains an al dente centre)
    • Vialone nano (retains quite a bit of bite when cooked and is often used for very moist risotto).
  • Use good quality stock for optimum flavour.

Risotto

The ratio used for risotto is 1:3 meaning 1 part rice and 3 parts stock.

  1. Heat the butter (in Italy’s south olive oil is used) and sweat finely diced onions.
  2. Add the rice on low heat sealing in the starch and letting the grains absorb the flavour of the onion. This is referred to as ‘tostatura’ (Italian for toasting).
  3. Gradually add the hot stock (or a bit of wine first), roughly ¼ to ⅓ of the stock first, add a bay leaf and stir regularly to absorb the liquid into the dish.
  4. Add the next lot of stock, cook it into the risotto and repeat until it has absorbed the liquid and is moist and creamy.
  • Risotto should not be too dry or too gooey and generally is finished with parmesan cheese and fresh herbs.
  • Risotto Milanese uses saffron to provide a vibrant colour and aroma.
  • Mushrooms such as ceps, chanterelles or even truffle are excellent for extra flavour.
  • The top can be gratinated with parmesan.

Condé rice

Condé rice is used for sweets and has a ratio of 1:5 to 1:7 of rice to liquid.

  • Short grain rice is used as its glutinous properties are required and the dish has to be cooked slowly to absorb the liquid.
  • The rice is mixed with milk, sugar and lemon zest, and slowly brought to the boil.
  • A cartouche is used and the pot sealed with a lid. It is placed in a slow oven at 120–150°C. Watch the heat control and do not colour or brown the rice.
  • Stir through every 10 minutes until all of the liquid has been absorbed. It can take up to an hour to cook.
  • Remove from the oven and mix egg yolk through to bind the mixture. Cool to room temperature and then add the whipped cream and mould.
  • It is often garnished with fresh fruit or mixed with dried or macerated fruit. For rice pudding simply omit the egg yolk.

Equipment for rice dishes

Depends on the dish and cultural origin:

  • Asian cuisine uses steamers, woks and chopsticks.
  • Pilaf rice requires a pot, lid, small knife, cutting board and wooden spoon.
  • Risotto also requires a pot, lid, small knife, cutting board and wooden spoon; an additional pot is needed for the stock.
  • Rice steamers such as commercial steamers are easy to use and ensure a perfect product.
  • Chinese boiled or steamed rice only needs a pot with a lid and a fork.
  • Condé rice requires a zester, pot and lid, wooden spoon and a whisk.
  • All mise en place requires bowls and containers. Cooking of rice dishes is done on commercial steamers or gas ranges, wok burners and stoves or induction tops.
  • Trays are essential for storage in fridges or the coolroom. Cover with clingfilm.

Timings and costs of rice dishes

  • Rice dishes are easy to cook and do not take a lot of time to prepare – 12 minutes is needed to boil rice.
  • Cheap per portion – steamed rice only has rice, water and a bit of salt.
  • Incorporate all ingredients into the costing – consider the cost of the stock used, mushrooms or truffle, fresh vanilla beans or fruit used.
  • Consider the time taken for preparation and add the staff cost into the equation.
  • Consider the cost of the energy used.
  • Bulking up dishes with filling accompaniments like rice can also affect the food cost of your main course items as you can cut more expensive components.
  • Applying correct hygiene processes and personal hygiene will reduce the risk of having to dispose of rice products unnecessarily through bad practices.

Grain dishes – ingredients

  • Breakfast items often use milk, yoghurt or fruit in addition to the cereals.
  • Lunch and dinner can be made entirely of grain with sauces and other ingredients added when plating up, for example polenta served with tomato sauce or pesto, quinoa stir fry with vegetable jardinière, barley risotto with mushrooms and smoked pork hock, couscous with Moroccan lamb, soba noodles with steamed fish fillet in soy sauce.
  • Dessert items can use grains for a filling finish of semolina slice in egg crust, coeliac-friendly sago pudding with apricots, or oats pancakes
  • As with all recipes measure ingredients and follow the preparation steps carefully. Apply the correct method during cooking and ensure you check that the finished dish meets enterprise requirements.
  • Grains can vary in their absorption rates so more or less liquid may be required to achieve the correct outcome. Adjust amounts accordingly.

Grain dishes – timings and cost

  • Cheap per portion – steamed quinoa only requires water and a bit of salt.
  • Incorporate all ingredients into the costing – consider the cost of the stock used, mushrooms or truffle, meat or fresh vanilla beans, or fruit used
  • Consider the time taken for preparation and add the staff cost into the equation.
  • Bulking up dishes with filling accompaniments like polenta can also affect the food cost of your main course items as you can reduce the amount of more expensive components.
  • Ensure you have sufficient mise en place as the extended cooking time of some products makes it impossible to cook extra during service periods.

Pasta dishes – timings and cost

  • Simple pasta dishes can be very cost effective and require limited skills of the chef.
  • Incorporate all ingredients into the costing – consider the cost of the stock or sauce used, mushrooms or truffle, meat or fresh vanilla beans, or fruit used.
  • Consider the time taken for preparation and add the staff cost into the equation.
  • Bulking up dishes with filling accompaniments like pasta can also affect the food cost of your main course items as you can reduce the amount of more expensive components.
  • Ensure you check the final product and adjust the consistency and taste prior to serving it.
  • Applying correct storage and food and personal hygiene will reduce the risk of having to dispose of pasta products unnecessarily through bad practices. Store farinaceous dishes above raw product in the fridge or coolroom. Store in appropriate containers, cover and label with the date of production for FIFO.

Functions of egg

  • Aerating – when whipped, egg will capture and hold air bubbles, making mixtures lighter. This can be observed in meringue, pavlova, sponge and soufflé. Mousses and farces based on vegetables or meat also require aerating with eggs.
  • Binding – eggs help to bind ingredients together, for example in a meatloaf or farce. Eggs also bind the ingredients in dough and batter, and provide structure for baked goods.
  • Setting – egg can set twice its own weight and is used in custards and quiche for this purpose. Use a ratio of 1:1 egg to liquid for a firm custard and 1:2 egg to liquid for a soft mixture, such as for bread and butter pudding.

Functions of egg

  • Coating – the stickiness of the egg helps to attach crumbs or other items to food. Egg can also be used as a coating for seafood or poultry to keep the centre moist.
  • Enriching – egg provides extra flavour and body in sauces and desserts. Liaison is used to provide the final flavour lift and smoothness in a sauce. The anglaise base used for ice cream provides a lingering taste.
  • Emulsifying – egg yolks are used to emulsify (combine) liquids that don’t normally mix. If egg is used, the oil and vinegar stay combined and sauces such as mayonnaise can be made and stored.
  • Glazing – the egg yolk is used in sauces before browning the dish under the salamander to achieve a golden colour in the finished product, for example for oysters Mornay or for covering pastry (such as puff pastry fleurons) with egg wash before baking.

Functions of egg

  • Clarifying – egg white will hold impurities and bring them to the top of a simmering liquid. This property is used when clarifying soups and sauces.
  • Thickening – eggs are used to thicken sauces. The fat in egg yolk adds flavour and thickens the liquid. Liaison is the most commonly used thickening agent but egg yolks are also added to custards or pastry cream for thickening.
  • Garnishing – eggs are used as a garnish in many dishes. Small poached quail eggs can be used for an individual Caesar salad, veal schnitzel Holstein-style requires a fried egg on top. Eggs can even be used as a base for creating interesting shapes – use your imagination!

Egg preparation

Eggs are available in various forms:

  • Fresh shell eggs
  • Fresh, liquid pulp
  • Dried/dehydrated.

Fresh shell eggs versus egg pulp:

  • Fresh egg will aerate better
  • Fresh eggs can be separated into yolks and whites
  • Liquid egg pulp is available in a container and can be easily dispensed
  • Frozen egg pulp can be stored at –18°C for up to six months
  • Once thawed, egg pulp must be kept refrigerated (between 0°C and 5°C) and used within three to four days.

Convenience egg products are popular and there is less risk of salmonella due to pasteurisation. The consistency also makes bulk production a lot simpler.

Egg preparation and storage

Special care must be taken particularly when cooking large amounts of food for bulk production or for special client groups. When cooking with fresh eggs the following key points should be considered:

  • Storage – store in a cool and dry place away from strong-smelling items; coolroom is preferable but ensure that they are separate from raw meat; store in original containers; apply FIFO procedures.
  • Preparation – do not use any cracked eggs; crack eggs into a small container first in case of contamination or ‘off’ odours; use product within 30 minutes at room temperature; do not use any egg shell; avoid cross-contamination.
  • Service of cooked/prepared product – serve product as soon as possible raw or partially cooked eggs may form a health risk, particularly to high risk groups. Store egg products away from strong-smelling items.
  • Be aware of high risk customers – elderly; sick people; pregnant women; small children and babies.

Egg preparation and storage

Often recipes call for the eggs to be separated into whites and yolks:

  • Emulsion sauces, anglaise and sabayon will use yolks.
  • Meringue will use just the whites.
  • Some recipes will use varying amounts of both yolks and whites such as sponge fingers and iced orange parfait.
  • When using up leftover egg whites go by the weight called for by your recipes. If the recipe asks for the whites of four eggs you would need to calculate the amount of white produced by each egg. Since the weight percentages of an egg are egg white (60%) and egg yolk (30%), then a 60g egg would be ~36g egg white and ~18g yolk. You would require 4 x 36g = 144g egg white.

Egg preparation – egg white

  • Carefully separate the whites from the yolk as the fat content in yolks will prevent the whites from whipping properly.
  • Your bowl and any equipment used must be also free from fat residue so it must be thoroughly cleaned and you may also rub the equipment with lemon juice to break down any residual fat.
  • It is advisable to use a small container to collect each white before mixing it the other whites. Any impurities can be removed easily this way without soiling the whole mixture.
  • The egg whites may then be transferred into a large container, covered and put in the fridge or freezer for longer storage.

Egg cookery methods

The most common methods are boiling, poaching, baking (en cocotte) and pan frying.

  • Boiled eggs can be cooked by the cold water start or hot water start method. If cooking large numbers of eggs, use a cold water start. Once they reach boiling point, it will take ~3 minutes to soft boil (depending on the number of eggs) and ~10 minutes to hard boil. Always reduce the heat to simmering for even heat penetration and to prevent cracking.

Egg cookery methods

  • Poached eggs should be cooked until they are set on the outside and soft in the centre. Place vinegar in the water in the ratio 1:10 to aid the coagulation process and put the egg in carefully. Use a sauce boat or tea/coffee cup for control.
  • If preparing poached eggs for functions, refresh in iced water and then reheat in salted water (this will prevent a vinegary taste). Do not use too much vinegar as it affects the taste. Egg poachers are also used in commercial kitchens for bulk production.
  • Chawan mushi (steamed egg pot) is a traditional Japanese dish made from dashi and eggs. It is equivalent to a royale mixture which is set by steaming.

Egg cookery methods

  • Fried eggs need careful heat control to prevent a hard, leathery bottom forming and to avoid overcooking. Fresh eggs are necessary for frying so that the thick albumen holds the egg together and reduces spreading. Egg rings can be used. Fried eggs ‘sunny side up’ show the egg yolk; ‘over easy’ means they have been flipped.
  • Scrambled eggs can be cooked in a pan or the microwave. The eggs are whisked through with a fork, a bit of cream can be added, then finish quickly in a non-stick pan or the microwave.
  • Omelettes should have no or only light colour, no wrinkles and a soft, slightly runny centre (known as ‘baveuse’). This perfect finish requires practice. They are commonly rolled into a cigar shape but Spanish omelettes are flat.

Egg cookery methods

  • Deep-fried eggs can be used as garnish or as an Asian-style omelette with fresh vegetables and soy sauce, or in Scotch eggs.
  • Grilled eggs can be done on a flat grill like fried eggs. Frittata is an egg mixture that is cooked in a pan under a salamander. Crème brûlée is a classical dessert.
  • Baked eggs en cocotte uses buttered ramekin dishes. The egg is cracked into the dish and then finished in a medium oven (a bain-marie can be used). Meringue is also baked in the oven, either without colour for shells or slightly browned for lemon meringue pie. Baked custards and puddings are old favourites and a soufflé is always impressive.

Points of care for egg dishes

The humble egg dish requires good skill level by the chef. It is cooked fairly quickly but can be cooked incorrectly being either too raw or too hard and leathery:

  • Peel boiled eggs by cracking the shell all over and then carefully removing the shell. Doing this with the egg under water works well.
  • Shaping a poached egg can be done by whisking the water into a whirlpool before adding the egg as it closes the egg white around the yolk. Once poached trim off any loose white.
  • Portion size for fried eggs is two eggs per serve. Either a two-egg or three-egg omelette is made depending on customer preferences.
  • Moulds are used for custards or crème caramel. Cook carefully to avoid air bubbles. Ensure you remove the product carefully for a smooth outside appearance.
  • Popular sauces for egg dishes are vanilla sauce for sweet dishes and tomato sauce or condiments, for example Worcestershire sauce, for savoury dishes.

Handout 5: Rice recipe – complex and modern

Risotto with pan-fried chicken livers, parmesan, garden peas and pea shoots

Risotto – ingredients

  • 200g chicken liver
  • 150g risotto rice
  • 50g cooked garden peas
  • 30g shallots
  • 15ml olive oil
  • 1 bay leaf
  • 500ml chicken stock
  • 25g butter
  • 50ml double cream
  • Salt
  • Pepper
  • Parmesan shaving
  • Pea shoots

Risotto – method

Peel and finely chop the shallots and cook them without colouring in the oil.

Add the rice and cook for one minute then add the chicken stock and seasoning and bring to the boil. Add the bay leaf and simmer gently on a low heat.

As the stock is absorbed stir the rice from time to time. Should the rice become too stiff add a little more stock. Cook the rice until it is tender and slightly moist.

Stir in the double cream and butter and cooked garden peas with a fork and transfer to a heated bowl for service.

Pan fry the chicken livers in a little olive oil until brown on the outside but still pink in the middle, season well and place on top of the creamy risotto. Garnish with parmesan shavings and pea shoots.
