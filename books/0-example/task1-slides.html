Introduction to nutrients and dietary requirements

In today’s modern society, chefs are required to provide customers with a varied choice of food that is both healthy and balanced to meet their needs.

In some cases, chefs must provide specific foods to certain people because of food allergens and intolerances, illness, their beliefs or their lifestyle choices.

Chefs need to provide what their customers want, while also being mindful of their dietary needs.

Customers

Chefs must be able to identify various types of customers and their needs. Certain groups may have specific nutritional requirements.

  • Vulnerable people: expectant mothers, young children, elderly people and those with a weakened immune system
  • People with health/medical issues: obesity, diabetes, high cholesterol, intolerances, allergens
  • Religious people: Muslim, Jewish, Buddhist
  • People with particular lifestyle choices: vegans, vegetarians, those on a low-fat diet

In restaurants and cafés customers are normally transient, and providing a balanced healthy diet can be even more of a challenge.

A balanced diet

A balanced diet is one that provides a body with the nutrients it needs to grow, repair and produce the necessary energy. A balanced diet will include all the main food groups:

  • Fruit and vegetables
  • Starches, such as bread and potatoes
  • Dairy products, such as milk and cheese
  • Protein, such as meat and fish
  • Foods and drinks high in fat and/or sugar

A healthy diet

Many consumers today are interested in nutrition and, thanks in part to the internet, have the knowledge to provide themselves with a balanced and healthy diet.

Those who work in the food service industry have a moral obligation to provide appropriate choices to their customers. Chefs should:

  • use appropriate cooking methods
  • where possible, reduce the amount of added salt and sugar in recipes
  • be familiar with techniques and products for reducing the fat content in meals
  • provide customers with relevant information
  • train their staff in nutrition

The Eatwell guide

Fruit and vegetables

The UK government recommends that everyone should eat five portions of fruit or vegetables every day. One portion for an average adult is approximately 80g. Fruit and vegetables are:

  • low in fat (with some exceptions, eg avocados and olives)
  • a good source of vitamins (especially vitamin C)

Cooking can destroy vitamins and nutrients, so care should be taken not to overcook.

Starches

Starches, or carbohydrates, include potatoes, breads, pasta and cereals.

  • They are the main source of energy in the average diet (as much as 50%).
  • White breads have added vitamins and minerals, by law:
    • calcium
    • iron
    • thiamin – B1
    • niacin – B3

Dairy products

Dairy is a major source of calcium and protein and includes:

  • milk
  • cheese
  • cream

Whilst this food group is essential to a healthy diet, chefs can consider using low-fat substitutes such as:

  • skimmed or semi-skimmed milk
  • cottage or low fat cheese
  • crème fraîche

Meat, fish, eggs and pulses

This group is considered to be the main source of protein in the diet.

  • Meat provides:
    • zinc
    • iron
    • vitamins (B group and D)
  • Fish provides:
    • Omega-3 fatty acids
    • vitamins A and C
    • calcium (canned fish)

Meat, fish, eggs and pulses

  • Eggs provide protein. Egg yolks are high in cholesterol, and so should be avoided by those with heart disease, diabetes or raised cholesterol levels.
  • Pulses (beans, peas, lentils and nuts) provide:
    • protein
    • iron
    • calcium
    • fibre
    • polyunsaturated and monounsaturated fats (in nuts)

Current UK nutritional guidelines

The UK government provides nutritional guidelines for the public, including the following points:

  • Reduce the total daily fat intake to 95g for men and 70g for women.
  • Reduce salt to 6g daily.
  • Reduce sugar to 60g daily.
  • Increase starchy carbohydrates to 37% of energy-based food.
  • Increase fibre in the diet.
  • Eat five to seven portions of fruit and vegetables a day.
  • Eat two portions of fish in a week.

Special dietary requirements

Some consumers require specific dietary requirements. This may be for a number of reasons, including:

  • preference for a balanced diet
  • food intolerance
  • allergies
  • health and lifestyle preferences
  • medical needs
  • religious requirements
  • cultural requirements.

Food intolerances and allergies

  • Food intolerances and allergies are adverse reactions to foods. It is imperative to keep customers safe and provide them with information on their meals.
  • Food establishments must cater for those with intolerances and allergies.
  • Chefs must consider all the ingredients they are using and ensure that customers are fully aware.
  • Some customers have an allergy to sesame seeds. In this case, a chef should consider using a vegetable oil rather than sesame oil.

Health and lifestyle choices

Many consumers follow a particular lifestyle and diet for their own reasons, even if they have no medical need to do so. This may include:

  • vegetarians – ethical choice
  • vegans – ethical choice
  • low fat
  • fruitarians
  • high carbohydrate – sports people
  • high protein – body builders

Medical reasons for specific diets

In some cases there is a medical reason for a customer’s diet. Some examples of this are:

  • low sugar diet for those suffering from diabetes
  • low fat diet to reduce cholesterol
  • gluten free diet for those suffering from coeliac disease
  • high calcium diet for those with osteoporosis
  • high calorie diet for those who are underweight
