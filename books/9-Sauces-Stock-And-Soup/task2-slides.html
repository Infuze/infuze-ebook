Introduction

The correct preparation and storage of stocks, soups and sauces is vital to the quality of the final dish.

Whilst preparation methods will vary according to what is being made, the following points should be noted:

  • Only quality ingredients should be used
  • Correct storage methods for all ingredients is required
  • Never rush the preparation of stocks, soups and sauces.

Preparing stocks

The preparation process of all types of stock is the same. It is the ingredients and cooking time that differ depending on the stock.

White stocks use:

  • small chopped blanched bones
  • peeled whole vegetables.

Brown stocks use:

  • small chopped browned bones
  • mirepoix of vegetables, browned.

Fish stocks use:

  • washed fish bones in cold water
  • finely chopped vegetables.

Preparing soups

The most important principle when preparing any soup is to begin with a high quality stock.

Soups are then enhanced by the addition of other flavours and ingredients. It is key that the additional ingredients are weighed and measured correctly, ensuring the final product meets the dish requirements.

Preparation methods

Preparation methods for soups differ depending on the type of soup.

Clear soups

  • Broth – prepared using a stock, and vegetables and meats cut to an even shape, such as paysanne for a Scotch broth
  • Consommé – prepared using minced beef, finely chopped vegetables, egg whites and stock.

Preparation methods

Thickened soups

  • Purée – prepared using a good stock and sliced vegetables
  • Cream – prepared using a stock and roux, béchamel or purée
  • Bisque – prepared using the carcasses of shellfish, mirepoix and stock.

Preparation methods

Miscellaneous soups

These are soups that do not fall into other categories an have their own particular preparation methods. These include cold soups and regional varieties.

For example, gazpacho is prepared using finely chopped vegetables and using tomato juice as the stock, regional varieties also serve this blended with the addition of chopped tomato, pepper and cucumber.

Preparing sauces

Different sauces will require different methods for preparation:

  • Reduction sauces traditionally use a brown stock thickened with a starch and then reduced to intensify the flavour, for example demi-glace.
  • Roux-based sauces are prepared using equal quantities of fat and flour, cooked to the desired colour, producing a white/blond/brown colour. Stock is then incorporated.

Preparing sauces

  • Emulsified sauces use egg yolks, an emulsifying agent and water, wine or vinegar which are mixed. Fat is then added very slowly until the emulsification process is complete (for example, hollandaise sauce).
  • Miscellaneous sauces are different in their production to other sauces or do not fit clearly into any of the other categories. They can be broken into two major groups:
    • Hot (for example, curry sauce)
    • Cold (for example, apple sauce).

All miscellaneous sauces will have their own preparation methods which must be followed.

Storage

The storage of prepared ingredients for stocks, soups and sauces is vital for the safety of your customer. Food safety and hygiene regulations must be complied with at all times. Always consider the following points:

  • Temperature
  • Date labelling
  • Coverings
  • Positioning
  • Stock rotation.

Temperature

All stocks, soups and sauces should ideally be used fresh daily. Otherwise:

  • They must be chilled to below 5°C within 90 minutes of cooking
  • They should be stored in a fridge and correctly labelled
  • If freezing, it must be at a temperature of –18°C or below
  • Stocks stored in a freezer must be boiled for two minutes before use.
  • They must not be reheated more than once
  • Hot holding must be above 63°C.

Date labelling & coverings

When labelling any stocks, soups and sauces, the following information should be included:

  • Name of product
  • Date of production
  • ‘Use by’ date.

Positioning and stock rotation

When storing any stocks, soups and sauces you must always consider the possibility of cross-contamination. All stocks, soups and sauces not for immediate use should be:

  • covered to prevent cross-contamination
  • stored in a fridge or freezer
  • labelled to ensure stock rotation following the ‘first in, first out’ principle
  • stored on the bottom shelf to prevent spillage.

Introduction

The production of quality stocks, soups and sauces will be achieved through using the correct procedures and using only quality ingredients.

Production methods will vary according to the stock, soups or sauce that is being made. However to achieve the best outcome the chef must:

  • only use high quality ingredients
  • take great care to follow recipes
  • finish the soup or sauce ensuring the correct:
    • consistency
    • seasoning.

Cooking times

Cooking times for stocks vary depending on the size of the bones used and the intensity of the stock required.

Stock Time
Fish Approximately 30 minutes
Vegetable 1 hour
White Up to 8 hours
Chicken Up to 12 hours
Brown 8 hours +

Making a white stock

  • White stocks should not contain colour. If bones are used then they may need blanching in boiling water first to set the protein and impurities.
  • If the bones are not washed and blanched, the impurities will not be clarified out and the stock will appear grey and murky.
  • White stocks are used for soups, sauces and as a cooking liquid for many dishes such as rice pilaf or a fricassée to intensify the flavour. The basic steps for producing a white stock are:

Making a white stock

  1. Cover the bones with cold water.
  2. Bring to the boil, reduce the heat to a simmer and skim.
  3. Add the aromatics such as vegetables, herbs and spices.
  4. Cook for the appropriate time to extract the flavours.
  5. Strain through a fine sieve into containers and label.
  6. Store correctly in small batches.

Cooking a brown stock

Brown stocks are also made from bones and aromatics but these are browned first to enhance the flavour and colour of the resulting stock.

Brown stocks are cooked longer than white stocks to extract more flavour. It is important to chop the bones into small pieces to maximise surface area and optimise the flavour.

Making a brown stock

  1. Brown the bones.
  2. Brown the mirepoix.
  3. Cover the bones with cold water.
  4. Bring to the boil, reduce the heat to a simmer and skim.
  5. Add the aromatics such as vegetables, herbs and spices.
  6. Cook for the appropriate time to extract the flavours.
  7. Strain through a fine sieve into containers and label.
  8. Store correctly in small batches.

Cooking a fish stock

Fish stocks are used to make soups and sauces, including prawn bisque, fish velouté and white wine sauce. The traditional method to make a fish stock is:

  1. Wash bones in cold water.
  2. Sweat of the vegetables.
  3. Add the bones and sweat for a further three minutes
  4. Add white wine and water to cover the ingredients.
  5. Simmer and skim to remove any impurities.
  6. Add herbs and peppercorns.
  7. Infuse.
  8. Strain through and reduce by half.
  9. Label and store in small batches.

Quality points for a good stock

The following are characteristics of a good quality stock:

  • There should be a pronounced flavour of the main ingredient.
  • They should be fat-free.
  • They should look clear.
  • They should be cooked for the required time to extract all the flavour.

Production and finishing of soups

Cooking techniques will vary to the type of soup that is being made, below are some of these techniques used today:

Consommé

  • This clear soup is produced from a good stock or broth, egg white, meat and aromatics. It is clarified to enhance presentation and flavour. Clarification is achieved through simmering the liquid and allowing a skin to form, known as the raft. This will collect the impurities ready for removal.
  • Once the consommé has been clarified the seasoning and colour should be corrected. You can then enhance the soup using pasta, beetroot, duck, beef or lamb. Various derivatives are:
    • Consommé julienne – vegetable strips
    • Consommé brunoise – vegetable dice
    • Consommé profiteroles – small choux pastry balls.

Production and finishing of soups

Cream soup

These types of soups are made from a stock and a roux or a purée. The soup is traditionally cooked and then liquidised. The soup is then finished by adding cream and garnished with herbs or with some of the base ingredient, such as julienne of chicken for cream of chicken soup.

Broth

This is a clear soup of bones, meat and vegetables. Once the meat is tender it is removed and cut for garnish. The vegetables are generally cut the same size using a paysanne shape. The soup is then skimmed and served using the meat as the garnish.

Production and finishing of soups

Purée soup

This type of soup is made of a vegetable base which has been blended and the consistency altered using stock or cream. The soup is finished with a cut of the base flavour and croutons.

Bisque

A bisque is a very rich creamy soup usually made from lobster or other shellfish. The soup is roux-based and cream is added at the end to improve the consistency and finish the soup.

Velouté

A velouté is made with stock and finished with a liaison of egg yolks. This allows the soup to have creamy texture and rich flavour. A typical chicken velouté will be finished by passing it through a strainer and garnishing it with julienne of chicken breast and chopped parsley.

Production and finishing of sauces

There are a variety of sauces available to the chef in the modern kitchen, both hot and cold. The techniques employed in their production and finishing are varied and wide. Some examples of the types of sauces are:

  • béchamel
  • brown
  • purée
  • gravy
  • velouté sauce
  • mayonnaise
  • tomato.

Production and finishing techniques for béchamel

Béchamel is a basic white sauce using flour, butter and milk. It is made using the following method:

Basic ingredients Method
  • Milk
  • Studded onion
  • White roux
  1. Heat the studded onion in the milk, produce the roux and add the liquid gradually.
  2. Cover with a buttered cartouche and cook with slow heat for 20-30 minutes.
This can be done at the back of a solid top or covered in the oven at 150°C. This helps to cook out the floury taste but be careful not to burn the sauce.

The finishing method is to strain the sauce and add diced butter to prevent a skin forming. Some derivatives include:

  • cardinal – truffle essence, lobster butter and cayenne pepper
  • soubise – chopped cooked onions
  • Scotch – julienne of egg whites and finely passed egg yolk.

Production and finishing techniques for brown sauces

Brown sauce, also known as espagnole, is a combination of brown stock and brown roux. In today’s modern kitchen a reduced veal stock is used to remove the need for flour.

This sauce is produced by creating a brown roux and adding brown stock to it. The thickened sauce is then strained and used as a base for many derivatives such as:

  • chasseur – shallots, white wine, mushrooms and tarragon
  • piquant – shallots, white wine, vinegar, gherkins and herbs
  • charcutière – chopped gherkins.

Production and finishing techniques for purée sauces

Purée sauces can either be fruit- or vegetable-based, such as apple or butternut squash purées.

These sauces will be produced and finished dependant on what is required of the final sauce. Some examples:

  • Apple sauce – This sauce is produced by peeling, coring and chopping the apples, adding them to butter and sugar and then cooking them to a purée. The sauce can be finished by passing it through a sieve.
  • Cranberry sauce – This sauce is produced by slowly simmering the fruit until soft with water and sugar. It can be served either whole or liquidised.
  • Green sauce – The ingredients in this sauce are all blended. It can be finished with the addition of water or lemon juice dependent on the consistency required.

Production and finishing techniques for gravy/jus

The common term used in English cooking is gravy, although traditional gravy was thickened with flour, whereas jus is thickened by reduction.

The juices of the meat form a sediment on the bottom of the roasting pan. The unwanted fat is removed (de-greased) then the sediment is released (de-glazed) with stock or wine. The bones and mirepoix are simmered slowly for approximately one hour.

The resulting juices should be very clear and have an excellent flavour of the meat item used.

Production and finishing techniques for velouté sauces

A velouté is a white sauce, velvety in texture and is made from a blond roux and white stock. The basic method to produce a velouté is as follows:

Basic ingredients Method
  • Blond roux
  • Stock
  1. Produce a blond roux and let it cool slightly; add the boiling stock gradually.
  2. Place a buttered cartouche on top and cook on the stove top or in the oven at ~150°C.
  3. Cook for 30–60 minutes, depending on the amount of sauce, to create a less floury taste. This applies to all roux-based sauces.
  4. A velouté is traditionally finished with a liaison which is a mix of egg yolk and cream used to enriched and further thicken the velouté
Depending on the stock added to the roux you can produce chicken, veal or fish velouté.

Some of the derivatives of a veloute are:

  • Bercy sauce – sweated shallots, reduced fish stock and white wine. Add the fish velouté and mount or monter au beurre, with butter and chopped parsley.
  • sauce suprême – reduce velouté with chicken and mushroom stock, finished with cream and monter au beurre, passed through a muslin cloth.

Production and finishing of mayonnaise

A mayonnaise is a good example of a cold emulsion sauce. The basic ingredients and preparation method is:

Basic ingredients Method
  • Egg yolk
  • Mustard
  • Vinegar
  • Oil
 The basic ratio for these ingredients is 8 yolks to 1 litre of oil.
Whisk the yolks with the mustard and vinegar and then incorporate the fat slowly. The sauce can be finished with a small amount of boiling water to stabilise it more. The oil used will provide the underlying flavour. Traditionally olive oil is used.

Some of derivatives of a mayonnaise include:

  • tartare sauce (sauce tartare) – mayonnaise mixed with chopped, cooked egg yolk, purée of green onions or chives
  • Chantilly sauce (sauce Chantilly) – mayonnaise, seasoned with lemon juice, finished with whipped cream
  • Andalusian sauce (sauce andalouse) – mayonnaise mixed with tomato purée, garnished with diced capsicum.

Quality points of sauces

All sauces should have the following quality characteristics:

  • Smooth
  • Glossy
  • A light texture
  • A definitive flavour.

When making any sauces, only quality ingredients should be used and care should be taken not to over-season.

Combinations

As a chef you will need to understand the flavours that will enhance your dishes for the customers. Some examples include:

  • breadcrumbed fish with sauce tartare
  • battered fish with a tomato sauce
  • lamb cutlets with mint sauce
  • roast beef with horseradish sauce
  • pork cutlets and apple sauce
  • beef steak with pepper sauce.
