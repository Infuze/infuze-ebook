Introduction to preparation techniques

There are many preparation techniques used in the making of desserts and puddings:

  • Emulsifying
  • Churning
  • Applying heat
  • Crystallising
  • Rolling
  • Piping
  • De-moulding
  • Beating
  • Chilling/setting
  • Caramelising
  • Moulding/shaping

Essentials

The preparation techniques used for different desserts can vary widely. However, the production of any quality dessert has the same essential requirements:

  • Using clean equipment
  • Following each of the recipe step by step and accurately
  • Measuring the ingredients correctly
  • Using tools which enhance the finished dish
  • Applying the correct temperatures
  • Cooling the dishes to meet the food safety standards.

Techniques used in preparing desserts and puddings

Emulsifying

This technique is used when making a cake batter. It describes the process of combining two ingredients that don’t naturally mix. The eggs must be added slowly so the yolk will emulsify the creamed butter and sugar.

Churning

This term is usually used to explain the vigorous mixing of cream to produce butter. It can also refer to the making of ice cream or sorbet when the mix is stirred continuously while cooling down in an ice cream maker.

Techniques used in preparing desserts and puddings

Applying heat

Applying heat is using a cooking process to transform the texture of an ingredient or product and make it edible, for example when cooking crème caramel.

Crystallising

In pastry-making, controlling crystallisation (the formation of crystals) is a key factor in quality as it relates to texture, with some foods requiring the promotion of crystallisation (chocolate and its cocoa fat content through controlled tempering) and others its prevention (sugar for example when making caramel).

Techniques used in preparing desserts and puddings

Rolling

Rolling is used predominantly in the preparation of pastry cases but also when decorating desserts to roll out fondant and icing.

Piping

Techniques such as piping can be used to create chocolate and fondant garnishes and also to pipe desserts such as baked Alaska.

Techniques used in preparing desserts and puddings

Demoulding

This technique is applied when removing a mould which has been used during the creation of some desserts or puddings such as steamed pudding or Charlotte russe.

Beating

Beating of egg whites, for example, allows the mixture to aerate and increase in volume to make a meringue.

Techniques used in preparing desserts and puddings

Chilling/setting

This technique is used when making mousses or bavarois and other cold desserts. It is used to help a dessert keep its shape by cooling it down. The dessert usually contains ingredient(s) which will help the product become firmer when cold such as butter, whipped cream or a setting agent like gelatine.

Caramelising

Caramelising is the addition of heat to a sugar to reach the temperature of around 160–180°C at which point the sugar turns golden brown. This is best seen when making crème caramel or crème brûlée.

Techniques used in preparing desserts and puddings

Moulding

The use of specific moulds to create a finish for the end dish. This technique is used when making cakes, flans, steamed puddings etc.

Shaping

Shaping is essential for the preparation and finishing of desserts and puddings. An example of shaping would be the use of spoons to create a quenelle of mousse.

Techniques used in preparing desserts and puddings

Aerating

This technique is used to create air within a dessert or pudding such as a mousse or meringue.

Whisking

Whisking is used to ensure the thorough combining of ingredients. This technique is used when preparing a batter for a Genoise or in the preparation of buttercream for finishing.

Techniques used in preparing desserts and puddings

Sugar boiling

Sugar boiling is used to create decorative features of desserts and puddings. These include sugar baskets and run-outs. The principle is the same for caramelising sugar for crème brûlée although this is achieved by grilling or with a blowtorch.

Blending

Blending is a technique used to combine two or more ingredients together to create a uniform mixture. A good example of this is when making a pancake batter for a crêpe Suzette.

Techniques used in preparing desserts and puddings

Passing/straining

This technique will ensure no foreign bodies are left in your mix. This is used when passing/straining an egg custard mix before using it in a bread and butter pudding.

Lining

This technique is used to stop a cake mix, such as a Genoise sticking to the cake mould or tin. It may also be used with an oil/fat to prevent the mix sticking.

Techniques used in preparing desserts and puddings

Creaming

Creaming is the technique used when mixing the sugar and butter together to form a creamy texture before adding the flour and other ingredients to a steamed pudding.

Folding

Folding is the technique used to incorporate an ingredient with others that have already been aerated. Folding (instead of stirring) helps prevent the air in the mix being reduced and the dessert becoming heavy. An example which uses this technique is a mousse, where the cream is folded in after the gelatine mix has been cooled and strained.

Introduction

Cooking desserts and puddings uses a wide variety of specialised equipment – the preparation and finishing of sweets differs from the standard chef toolbox:

  • Mixers/food processors
  • Tins/moulds
  • Cutters/silpat mat
  • Ovens/pans
  • Steam blender
  • Specialist machinery.

Mixers/food processors

There are a wide variety of mixers available to the chef, including handheld and industrial-sized machines.

Food processors are also available in most modern kitchens and allow the chef to work efficiently.

Tins/moulds

Tins and moulds come in a variety of materials such as metal, rubber and, if used for cold dishes, also plastic.

They allow the chef to create a finish before the ingredients have set, this can be seen when making any of the following desserts or puddings:

  • Steamed sponge pudding
  • bavarois/mousse
  • Cakes.

Cutters/silpat mats

Cutters are used to create a finish for the end product and are used in the making of decorative work to finish a dessert or pudding.

Silpat mats are specialised re-usable mats which items can be cooked on without sticking. They are made from silicone rubber.

Ovens and pans

There are many different types of ovens such as:

  • deck ovens
  • combination ovens.

Specialist pans used in the making of desserts and puddings include:

  • crêpe pans
  • poaching pans.

Specialist equipment

Ice cream machine

Ice cream machines are now available in many different sizes and types. They allow chefs to create their own ice creams or sorbets in house.

Steam blender

A steam blender is a blender with an integrated cooking function – it allows chefs to blend and cook items simultaneously and much faster, for example sauce anglaise.

Other equipment

Other equipment available for the preparation and cooking of desserts and puddings includes:

  • induction hobs
  • micro-scales
  • microwaves
  • palette knives
  • pastry scraper
  • ready-made icing bags.

Storage

Storage of prepared ingredients for desserts and puddings is vital for the safety of your customer. Food safety and hygiene regulations must be complied with at all times. The following must be taken into consideration:

  • Temperature
  • Date labelling
  • Coverings
  • Positioning
  • Stock rotation.

Temperature

The following needs to be considered for all desserts and puddings:

  • Ideally use fresh ingredients purchased daily. If not they must be:
    • kept at 5°C or below if they are high risk items
    • stored labelled in the right conditions and temperature (fridge or dry store)
    • –18°C or below if freezing.
  • Cooked products must be cooled down to 5°C within 90 minutes if not to be served hot.
  • Cooked products must not be reheated more than once.
  • Hot holding must be above 63°C
  • Cold holding below 8°C.

Date labelling and coverings

When labelling any desserts and puddings the following guidelines should be followed:

  • Name of product
  • Date of production
  • ‘Use by’ date

Positioning and stock rotation

When storing any desserts or puddings you must always consider the possibility of cross-contamination. All desserts and puddings, especially high risk ones where no further cooking is required, should be:

  • covered to prevent cross-contamination
  • stored in a fridge or freezer
  • labelled to ensure stock rotation – ‘first in first out’
  • stored on the appropriate shelf away from raw items.
