Introduction to cold foods

When working in a professional kitchen, it is likely that you will be expected to produce both hot and cold food.

It is important that as a chef you understand the different types of ingredients generally used to produce cold products and dishes.

It is also important that you know the different techniques used to prepare and finish cold food in a safe and hygienic manner.

Techniques to produce cold food products

  • Coating: There are certain items that may need to be coated to add texture and flavour to the finished product. The coatings should complement the different flavours and textures that they are covering. Coatings may include breadcrumbs or glazes. For example, cold BBQ chicken pieces or breadcrumbed herb chicken or prawns for a buffet.
  • Combining: This is used a lot when producing cold food products, especially when making compound salads where different ingredients are combined or bound together to form one product. For example the raw ingredients of a coleslaw (carrots, onion and cabbage) are combined with a dressing or mayonnaise.
  • Cooking (boiling, poaching, steaming, baking, roasting, grilling, deep/shallow frying, stewing, braising): The majority of cold food products will have to be prepared, cooked and cooled in some manner. For example, homemade sausage rolls could be cooked, cooled and served for a buffet.

Techniques to produce cold food products

  • Cooling: The majority of cold food products will have to be prepared and cooked and cooled in some manner, unless they are entirely raw products such as a French salad. It is important that these are correctly cooked and cooled to maintain food safety; usually to between 3ºC and 8ºC within 90 minutes, to minimise bacterial growth. This can be achieved through the use of a blast chillier. For example, a joint of beef could be cooked, cooled and sliced for a meat platter for a buffet.
  • Cutting: The majority of cold food products need to be prepared by cutting in some form, using knives and small cutting equipment. Cutting techniques may involve chopping, coring, cutting into batons, dicing, filleting, jointing, marking and scoring, peeling, skinning, slicing, trimming and turning/shaping. For example, shredding cabbage for a coleslaw.

Techniques to produce cold food products

  • Drying: This could be used in a variety of cold dishes using dehydrating foods as garnishes; for example, dried dehydrated apples could add texture to a Waldorf salad. Another and more simple drying technique could be drying lettuce before use in a salad. If the lettuce is added to a salad with excess water, it could change the texture and flavour of the overall dish.
  • Folding: Similar to combining, folding in is the gentle mixture of ingredients together. Alternatively, folding could refer to the folding of a ingredient; for example, when making sausage rolls you would fold the pastry over the sausage roll before baking.
  • Grating: Grating is used for a variety of cold food commodities to reduce them in size. It is usually used for commodities that are hard in texture, such as cheese or carrots. For example, a block of cheese could be grated to make a simple cheese sandwich, or carrots grated for a coleslaw.

Techniques to produce cold food products

  • Measuring: It is important to always measure ingredients especially when mixing liquids together; for example, producing a vinaigrette for a salad. You need to measure out the correct ratios of oil and vinegar to ensure the product is of the correct consistency and flavour.
  • Mixing: Similar to combining, this refers to the mixing of ingredients together to form another product or dish. For example, mixing mayonnaise with grated eggs to produce egg mayonnaise.
  • Portioning: One of the key techniques when producing cold food products. It is essential that food is correctly portioned to ensure that the right amount of customers are served from the dish/products; this will also ensure that budgetary requirements are met, and that you don’t over- or under-produce a dish. Portioning will also ensure that the correct size of the product is achieved; for example, cutting a cooked and cooled quiche into smaller individual portions to be served on a buffet.

Techniques to produce cold food products

  • Storage and temperature control: These are very important when handling, storing and serving cold food products. Many cold products require correct temperature storage between 3°C and 8°C to avoid the multiplication of harmful bacteria. This can be achieved through the use of cold storage display units or fridges.
  • A lot of cold foods, especially cooked and cooled meat, fish and diary products, can be deemed high-risk, as they do not require any further processing or cooking and contain large amounts of proteins which are favourable to for bacteria growth.
  • Weighing: It is essential to weigh out ingredients correctly to the recipe specification. This will help to achieve the correct balance of flavours and textures when preparing cold foods. For example, weighing out the portion size for individual plated Caesar salads, or weighing out the ingredients for a cooked egg and bacon quiche.

Producing different types of cold food products

  • Cold desserts (fruit-based, egg-based): These make a great addition at the end of a meal and would usually be served in individual portions for ease of service. Fruit such as pears or plums could be poached and cooled in the cooking syrup and served with a sweet sauce or ice cream, garnished with fresh mint or baby basil.
  • Cold fish and shellfish: Seafood such as salmon or tuna fillets can be poached, grilled or roasted with seasoning and then cooled and flaked into a salad, or mixed with a light lemon mayonnaise for a sandwich filling. Cooked prawns could be combined with a cocktail sauce and used in a simple prawn cocktail, or as a sandwich filling served with a wedge of lemon.

Producing different types of cold food products

  • Cold meats and poultry: These are cooked, cooled and served at buffets or used to form parts of different dishes or fillings. Joints of meat or poultry can be served whole and carved on a buffet in front of the customer, or can be pre-sliced for easy of service. They can be combined with other ingredients for other dishes; for example, chicken Caesar salad.
  • Compound salads: These involve a number of different items; the ingredients are usually prepared and cooked separately and then combined/mixed together to form a salad bound with a sauce, dressing or oil; for example, a cold couscous salad with roasted vegetables and a lemon and herb dressing.

Producing different types of cold food products

  • Hors d’oeuvres: These involve a number of different items; the ingredients are usually prepared and cooked separately, and then combined/mixed together to form hors d’oeuvres such as prawn cocktail or seafood terrine.
  • Oils and dressings: These don’t usually involve any cooking, and are simple to prepare by the mixing of different ingredients. Oils and dressing such as French dressing can be prepared in advance, and some will store for a long period of time.
  • Pastry products: These are prepared, cooked and cooled before serving. It is important that they are baked or fried correctly, and that the pastry and filling are fully cooked; for example, sausage rolls, quiche or samosas.

Producing different types of cold food products

  • Sandwiches (open, closed, afternoon tea): These are prepared prior to service in most cases, but can be made to order when serving small numbers of customers. The fillings are prepared, and the bread products are then filled with the desired fillings. They should not be prepared too early, as this can lead to sandwiches going soggy.
  • Simple salads: These usually involve raw salad ingredients such as lettuce and tomato, which are cut, trimmed, or sliced and then combined and dressed with simple dressings and oils. The ingredients can be prepared in advance and combined at the last minute to ensure the correct texture and flavours are maintained in the final salad.

Finishing methods for cold food products

It is important to finish cold food products correctly. The following factors should be considered when serving cold food products:

  • Decorations
  • Garnishes
  • Portions
  • Sauces
  • Appearance & Colour
  • Aroma
  • Texture
  • Temperature
  • Taste
  • Seasoning

Finishing methods for cold food products

  • Decorating: Decorating is a finishing technique usually associated with cold desserts or sweet pastry. It can involve piping, small decorations such as spun sugar, or chocolate runouts. You can also decorate cold savoury items – for example, a whole poached salmon for a buffet, which can be decorated with flavoured butters and sliced cucumber.

Finishing methods for cold food products

  • Garnishing: Garnishing is important when finishing cold products, as it can add flavour and texture as well as improving the appearance of the overall dish. It is important that a suitable and correct garnish is used when garnishing cold products. Don’t garnish a finished product just for the sake of it; the garnish should represent part of the product, and complement the dish. For example, individual prawn cocktails could be garnished with a slice of lemon and a sprig of dill; or a simple compound salad such as coleslaw could be garnished with chopped parsley.

Finishing methods for cold food products

  • Portioning: One of the key techniques when producing cold food products. It is essential that food is correctly portioned to ensure that the correct amount of customers are served from the dish. This will also ensure that budgetary requirements are met, and that you don’t over- or under-produce a dish. Portioning will also ensure that the correct size of the product is achieved; for example, cutting a cooked and cooled quiche into smaller individual portions to be served on a buffet.

Finishing methods for cold food products

  • Saucing: Saucing is an important factor when finishing cold food products because it can add flavour and texture, enhance the appearance of the food and stop it from drying out.
  • It is important that a suitable and correct sauce is used for cold dishes. Don’t add a sauce just for the sake of it; it should represent part of the product and complement the dish.
  • It is possible to serve a sauce separately and allow the customer to add their own sauce or dressing. For example: a French salad could be served with a simple vinaigrette, or a cold, poached and sliced chicken breast could be served with a Caesar sauce.
