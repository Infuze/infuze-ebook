Introduction to grilling

Grilling is the process of cooking with direct and indirect heat. It is important to understand the differences between the two methods to ensure success when grilling. Grilled food is most often cooked with one or the other of these cooking methods, but occasionally both methods are alternated to grill certain foods; this is particularly useful when grilling thick cuts of meat. The temperature required and the grilling method used will depend on the type of food and the thickness of the cut.

Types of food suitable for grilling

  • Fish: A medium heat should be used when grilling fish. Using too high a heat will cause some parts to cook too quickly and dry out, while other parts will not be done all the way through.
  • The thicker the piece of fish, the further away from the heat source it should be; or the heat source should be at a lower temperature to prevent the outside of the fish from burning before the inside is properly cooked. You will also have to decide whether to use direct or indirect heat during the grilling.

Types of food suitable for grilling

  • Meat: The high heat of grilling sears the surface of beef, creating tender meat with a flavourful crust. The ideal cooking temperature and method of grilling (direct, indirect or a combination) will depend on the cut of meat and the quality of the meat.
  • Some meat cuts may require preliminary preparation such as boning, trimming, tying or tenderising before they are grilled.
  • Example dishes:
    • Lamb chop with mint sauce
    • Pork chop with apple sauce
    • Beef steak with mustard sauce

Types of food suitable for grilling

  • Poultry: Grilling infuses chicken with a smoky flavour. As with fish, a medium heat should be used when grilling chicken. Using too high a heat will cause some parts to cook too quickly and dry out, while other parts will not be done all the way through.
  • An example of a grilled chicken dish might be grilled Cajun chicken breast with a mango and chilli salsa.

Types of food suitable for grilling

  • Vegetables: Grilling vegetables is a great way to bring out their natural sweetness and add a smoky flavour. 
  • Harder vegetables such as sweetcorn may need to be cooked before grilling to ensure they are done at the same time as the other vegetables.
  • Brushing vegetables with butter or oil during grilling helps to keep the vegetables from sticking to the grill grate.
  • Try and experiment by sprinkling different herbs and spices over vegetables while grilling, to enhance their flavour and appearance.

Preparation methods for grilling

  • Batting out: Some items, such as meat or poultry, may need to be batted out to reduce their thickness in order to encourage even, quick cooking. This process also helps to reduce the toughness of meat fibres in a cut of meat or poultry by tenderising the meat. Tenderising breaks down the fibres and softens the meat, making it easier to chew and more palatable.
  • Meat can also be batted out with the use of meat mallets or meat utensils, typically made of steel or similar alloys. The tenderising tools are hammer-like handled utensils, made with a flat or point-protruding surface that can be pounded into the meat to break apart the meat fibres. The meat items can be put between two pieces of clingfilm or parchment paper and batted out to reduce the damage to the surface of the meat. Items that are typically batted out are chicken breasts for escalopes or veal steaks for schnitzel.

Preparation methods for grilling

  • Cutting: Some food items, before they are grilled, should be prepared by cutting with knives or small cutting equipment. Cutting techniques may involve chopping, coring, cutting into batons, dicing, filleting, jointing, marking and scoring, peeling, skinning, slicing, trimming and turning/shaping. An example is cutting a chicken into portions before grilling.
  • Marinating: Marinating is the process of soaking food in a seasoned liquid for the purpose of flavouring and tenderising it before cooking. Meat is often marinated in preparation for grilling, and can be soaked in a marinade for anywhere from two to twelve hours (even more in some circumstances). The marinade should totally cover the meat in order for it to work effectively. It is important to store the meat in its marinade in the refrigerator to minimise the growth of bacteria.

Preparation methods for grilling

  • Oiling/buttering: To achieve a good flavour and colour during the grilling process, and to stop them sticking to the grill pan or tray, items are usually buttered/oiled before being exposed to the heat.
  • Seasoning: Meat, poultry, fish or vegetables for grilling should be thoroughly seasoned before the cooking process; this will enable the flavours to soak through during grilling.

Techniques to produce grilled products

  • Positioning: As with an oven, there will often be ‘hot’ or ‘cold’ spots within a grill. The grill gives direct heat from either the bottom or the top, and it is important that the food is positioned correctly in relation to how you want it cooked. If you want a lot of colour quickly, it would make sense to place it close to the flame/heat; if you want to cook more slowly, you would position it slightly further away.
  • Temperature control: A grill will generally be very hot so as to provide quick, high heat, so it must be managed accordingly. During the cooking process it is important to control the heat to ensure even cooking and colouring of the product.
  • Turning: When grilling, it is important to turn the food so as to ensure even colouring and cooking and avoid over-browning. This can easily be done using a slice, a cooking fork or a pair of food tongs.

Quality checks during the grilling process

  • Appearance and degree of cooking: Ensuring that the product is cooked but not overcooked is key when it comes to grilling time. Regular checks should be undertaken to ensure that the product is not overcooked.
  • Texture: Always check the texture of grilled products throughout the cooking process to ensure that they do not overcook. This can be done easily, especially with meats, vegetables and poultry, by inserting a small knife or skewer to see if the product is cooked. If the knife or skewer comes out easily and cleanly and no blood emerges from the product, it would suggest it is done.

Finishing methods for grilled products

  • Garnishing: Garnishing is important when finishing grilled foods, as it can add flavour and texture as well as improving the appearance of the overall dish. It is important that a suitable and correct garnish is used when garnishing grilled products. Don’t garnish a finished product just for the sake of it; the garnish should represent part of the product, and complement the dish. For example, a grilled fillet steak could be garnished with grilled tomatoes, mushrooms and a pepper sauce, while grilled tuna fish could be garnished with a slice of lemon and a sprig of parsley.

Finishing methods for grilled products

  • Saucing: Saucing is an important factor when finishing grilled products. Like garnishing, it adds flavour and texture, improving the appearance of the overall dish. For example, pan-grilled fish fillets could be served with a butter sauce such as a hollandaise, or grilled vegetables could be served with a chilli and lime dressing.
  • It is important that a suitable and correct sauce is used when saucing grilled products. Don’t add a sauce to a finished product just for the sake of it; it should represent part of the product and complement the dish. It is possible to serve the sauce separately, allowing the customer to add their own sauce or dressing
