Introduction to science and technology

Science and the use of technology now play a much greater part in the delivery of food and food products to customers.

As science has developed and technologies improved, there have been major improvements in food availability, with chefs having more choices in the procedures and practices they can utilise. This includes improvements in:

  • Preparation methods
  • Cooking
  • Storage

Equipment

Technology has now fully entered the professional kitchen. The impact of this technology is supporting organisations to provide an efficient and safe environment to deliver a product to the customer.

Preparation methods

Technological advances have meant that preparation methods have improved and become more efficient and safer for the health of the professional chef. Examples where technology have improved the preparation of food include:

  • Using blenders to chop ingredients
  • Mixers instead of a hand whisk
  • Electronic combination ovens to prove and cook breads
  • Food processors to purée soups etc

Equipment

Cooking

Cooking methods have developed beyond the chef’s imagination of 20 years ago, with the introduction of pre-programmed and temperature-sensitive equipment to provide the accuracy required to produce the final dish. This type of equipment includes:

  • Water baths for sous vide cooking
  • Vacuum-packaging machines for sous vide and storage
  • Thermal blenders and mixers to prepare and cook dishes
  • Ice cream machines that can provide portions in a matter of minutes

Equipment

Storing

Science and technology have had a major impact on the ability of organisations to store food for longer and provide safer food for their customers. This includes the use of:

  • Centrally controlled and monitored refrigerated systems to delay the growth of food spoilage bacteria
  • The use of nitrogen in packaging to remove oxygen and prevent bacterial growth
  • Freezing food to remove the water, again deterring the growth of bacteria
  • The canning process, heating food to 121˚C for 3 minutes to kill bacteria, known as a botulinum cook

In each case this allows the professional chef to store food safely for their customers and to reduce waste.

Food processing management

Food processing management has now developed numerous methods in order to deliver food across the world in a fresh and safe manner. It has achieved this through the use of science and innovative technologies. Some examples include:

  • Scientists have managed to produce enzymes that both quicken and delay the ripening of some foods to allow farmers to shorten the harvest period, or to delay ripening during transportation
  • New scientific methods have been developed so livestock has a reduced level of fat before being slaughtered
  • Fertilisers have been produced to increase yields

Food processing management

  • Pesticide and insecticides have been developed to allow farmers to spray crops safely and increase resistance to pests and diseases
  • Herbicides control the growth of weeds and other such plants, allowing more room for crop growth
  • The shelf-life of food has been increased through the use of technology. Peas are harvested and frozen in situ and then transported around the world
  • Foods are now also irradiated – that is treated with gamma rays and X-rays. This can prevent the ripening process but, more importantly, it kills bacteria

Food processing management

  • Intensive farming has also allowed farmers to increase yields. This is achieved by increased labour and equipment, using high levels of chemicals and storing animals inside
  • Another form of intensive farming is called hydroponics. This is where the soil is substituted with a mineral mix. The farmer has control over growth but it is only used for high-yielding crops due to the expense
  • Large food processing companies also produce ready meals for individuals to buy or commercial businesses to supply their canteens

Food processing management

Genetically modified (GM) foods are foods that have been modified at the genetic level.

Scientists have developed methods that identify individual genes and they then change these to act in a different manner. Scientists have managed to modify plants to:

  • Resist chemicals such as herbicides and pesticides
  • Create their own toxins, which are harmless to humans but can kill pests
  • Provide a longer shelf-life

The future will see further developments in this area. Scientists hope to produce crops that are more healthy for humans and also crops that can grow in all conditions.

Molecular gastronomy

Molecular gastronomy employs science and technology to improve the preparation and cooking of food. The term was first used in 1969 by Hungarian scientist Nicholas Kurti and French scientist Hervé This.

Some notable practitioners include:

  • Heston Blumenthal
  • Pierre Gagnaire
  • Thomas Keller
  • Emmanuel Stroobant
  • Ferran Adrià

The overall aim of this type of cooking is to provide diners with unusual and original recipes. In some cases this includes the whole dining experience enhanced by the use of aerosols to add a subtle aroma to the air in the restaurant.

Dishes created using molecular gastronomy include:

  • Seared foie gras with a 24-carat golden egg
  • Beetroot, dark chocolate, raspberry and black olives
  • Charcoal, ashes and 64° egg
  • Creamy kazu Parmesan gnocchi with pea water

In some cases, the dishes that have been developed using this method have made it to mainstream cuisine – most famously ‘triple-cooked chips’ by Heston Blumenthal.

However, in the main, the development of this type of cooking is generally only seen in Michelin star restaurants across the globe.

Dishes of simple origin have now been worked and developed by chefs using molecular gastronomy. An example of this is the simple shepherd’s pie. Instead of a dish that was made from lamb, potatoes, onions and seasoning, the new drive for a unique flavour and experience has developed recipes that contain – among other ingredients – beetroot and double cream.

There has also been a drive where a dish is deconstructed and served separately. This includes the use of foams, or ‘espuma’, which is another use of molecular cuisine.
