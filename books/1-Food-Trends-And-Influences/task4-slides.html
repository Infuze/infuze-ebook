Introduction to pairing food and beverages

There are no set rules when endeavouring to match food with a beverage – there is just guidance and, more importantly, personal choice. Customers will always have preferences when it comes to choosing what they eat and this is also true of the beverage they wish it served with.

As a chef you need to consider the characteristics of different types of food and beverages to be able to make an informed decision on the matching process.

In some fine-dining restaurants, a specialist position to undertake the matching process has developed over the years – this is a wine waiter or sommelier.

Wines

As a general rule of thumb some wines are better served with specific types of food, for example:

  • White wines – fish/chicken/pasta
  • Red wines – beef/lamb/venison
  • Rosé – salads/seafood/pasta
  • Desert wines – deserts
  • Port – cheese

Whilst the above examples are accepted practice, the customer’s choice should always be followed.

Some customers never drink red wine and therefore will always prefer white with all dishes. Some customers do not enjoy the body (thickness) of desert wines and will not enjoy any matching process.

Wines

In all cases the following must be considered when endeavouring to match drinks with certain foods:

  • Body – this is the consistency of the wine: white wines are generally light and darker wines become more full bodied
  • Acidity – this term refers to the flavours of the wine, the freshness, sour notes etc
  • Intensity – this term refers to the colour and aroma of the wine

The art of matching is to find food and wine that complement, rather than overpower, each other. This is very difficult with the introduction of a sauce to a dish.

Wines

Some matching examples:

  • Beef bourguignon – Burgundy or Merlot
  • Sweetbreads – Chardonnay
  • Roast duck – light Pinot Noir
  • Chicken chasseur – Chianti
  • Caviar – Champagne
  • Scallops – Sauvignon Blanc
  • Grilled lobster – Rosé Champagne

Beers

Beers can also be matched with food. This is often the case in regional areas where local dishes and beers are matched just as wines are in different areas of the world. Some examples include:

  • Blonde beer – salads and other light dishes
  • Pale ales – strong spicy food such as curry
  • Brown ale – hearty food such as roast pork
  • String ale – beef and game
  • Pilsner – chicken and salmon
  • Witbier – seafood

Pairing food and beverages

There is no absolute science to the pairing of food and drink; it is down to the taste of the individual. As a chef you need to have a good understanding of what drink will possibly pair with the food you are preparing.
