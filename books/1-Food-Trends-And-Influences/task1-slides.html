Introduction to influences

The modern dining experience in now regarded as a stimulation on the senses of people who choose to eat out. Today people think of the dining experience as part of the social society we live in.

Professional kitchens have taken this on board and now develop their offering to guide customers through the stimulation of senses, such as:

  • Taste
  • Sight
  • Smell

Influences on people selecting different food and drink include:

  • Historical perspective
  • Contemporary perspective
  • People’s choices

Historical influences

From a historical perspective many regions around the world have played their part in the advancement of cooking. This includes their influence on others due to their:

  • Cooking techniques
  • Commodities
  • Menu structures
  • Cultures

In each case, these influences have driven the world of gastronomy to develop and enrich the palates of modern customers.

Cooking techniques

Cooking techniques have developed and changed throughout history, from Romans to modern-day national cuisines.

Initially, Romans boiled or stewed their meats. However, with the advent of war and the conquests of other countries, their techniques began to develop. They tended to adopt local techniques to feed their troops and people. Eventually these techniques found their way back to Rome for all to enjoy.

This process continued throughout the middle ages and still continues to this day, as travel has become more prevalent.

Cooking techniques

Today, cooking techniques from far and wide are now influencing food preparation all over the world. These include:

  • Africa – wrapping food in banana leaves and steaming
  • Indian subcontinent – the use of numerous spices to impart flavours
  • Asia – quick frying techniques to retain the colour and taste

Following the voyages by Europeans, some of these techniques were then adopted in their homelands. In turn, the techniques used in Europe influenced the inhabitants of countries thousands of miles apart:

  • South America – influenced by the Spanish and Portuguese
  • Far east – influenced by the British
  • Pacific Islands – influenced by British, French and Dutch

Commodities

Menus and recipes have developed over many years and in different regions of the world. This is due in part to the availability of commodities. Transportation – initially ships – allowed the sharing of commodities throughout the ‘known’ world. These included:

  • Potatoes from South America
  • Spices and herbs from the Indian subcontinent
  • Fruit from the Caribbean
  • Rice from China
  • Melons from Africa

All of these, and many more, have influenced cooking, both historically and in modern times.

Menu structures

The structure of a menu is influenced by many factors such as culture, commodities and the occasions.

In early times the main focus of the menu was on simple foods that had been adopted from other regions, especially so in the Roman era.

During the middle ages the European menu was focused on meat, with less emphasis on vegetables.

During the 17th century, food menus developed with the onset of classical French cuisine. This was shared later in the 18th century as French chefs migrated overseas.

The 19th century saw the development of individual courses on a menu, substituting the list of food. It was at this time that signature dishes began to appear.

Cultures

The historical influence that had the largest impact on eating and drinking out was culture. Throughout history all parts of the world have adopted differently and the effect of their own cultures has spread.

  • In ancient times meals lasted for hours
  • Continental Europe saw mealtimes as a gathering of the family
  • In the Far East it was acceptable to eat with your fingers
  • French chefs were held in high regard in the 17th and 18th centuries
  • Different cultures have different meanings for the names of a meal. A snack in Europe may be considered a meal in the Far East

Current influences

Modern chefs and their customers are influenced by factors such as:

  • The impact of geography on commodities:
    • soil
    • climate
    • lakes
    • rivers
    • sea
    • terrain

Another current influence are people’s beliefs and religions, including:

  • Types of commodities used
  • Preparation methods
  • Religious meaning

A healthy diet

Many consumers today are interested in nutrition and, thanks in part to the internet, have the knowledge to provide themselves with a balanced and healthy diet.

Those who work in the food service industry have a moral obligation to provide appropriate choices to their customers. Chefs should:

  • use appropriate cooking methods
  • where possible, reduce the amount of added salt and sugar in recipes
  • be familiar with techniques and products for reducing the fat content in meals
  • provide customers with relevant information
  • train their staff in nutrition

The Eatwell guide

Fruit and vegetables

The UK government recommends that everyone should eat five portions of fruit or vegetables every day. One portion for an average adult is approximately 80g. Fruit and vegetables are:

  • low in fat (with some exceptions, eg avocados and olives)
  • a good source of vitamins (especially vitamin C)

Cooking can destroy vitamins and nutrients, so care should be taken not to overcook.

Starches

Starches, or carbohydrates, include potatoes, breads, pasta and cereals.

  • They are the main source of energy in the average diet (as much as 50%).
  • White breads have added vitamins and minerals, by law:
    • calcium
    • iron
    • thiamin – B1
    • niacin – B3

Dairy products

Dairy is a major source of calcium and protein and includes:

  • milk
  • cheese
  • cream

Whilst this food group is essential to a healthy diet, chefs can consider using low-fat substitutes such as:

  • skimmed or semi-skimmed milk
  • cottage or low fat cheese
  • crème fraîche

Meat, fish, eggs and pulses

This group is considered to be the main source of protein in the diet.

  • Meat provides:
    • zinc
    • iron
    • vitamins (B group and D)
  • Fish provides:
    • Omega-3 fatty acids
    • vitamins A and C
    • calcium (canned fish)

Meat, fish, eggs and pulses

  • Eggs provide protein. Egg yolks are high in cholesterol, and so should be avoided by those with heart disease, diabetes or raised cholesterol levels.
  • Pulses (beans, peas, lentils and nuts) provide:
    • protein
    • iron
    • calcium
    • fibre
    • polyunsaturated and monounsaturated fats (in nuts)

Current UK nutritional guidelines

The UK government provides nutritional guidelines for the public, including the following points:

  • Reduce the total daily fat intake to 95g for men and 70g for women.
  • Reduce salt to 6g daily.
  • Reduce sugar to 60g daily.
  • Increase starchy carbohydrates to 37% of energy-based food.
  • Increase fibre in the diet.
  • Eat five to seven portions of fruit and vegetables a day.
  • Eat two portions of fish in a week.

Special dietary requirements

Some consumers require specific dietary requirements. This may be for a number of reasons, including:

  • preference for a balanced diet
  • food intolerance
  • allergies
  • health and lifestyle preferences
  • medical needs
  • religious requirements
  • cultural requirements.

Food intolerances and allergies

  • Food intolerances and allergies are adverse reactions to foods. It is imperative to keep customers safe and provide them with information on their meals.
  • Food establishments must cater for those with intolerances and allergies.
  • Chefs must consider all the ingredients they are using and ensure that customers are fully aware.
  • Some customers have an allergy to sesame seeds. In this case, a chef should consider using a vegetable oil rather than sesame oil.

Health and lifestyle choices

Many consumers follow a particular lifestyle and diet for their own reasons, even if they have no medical need to do so. This may include:

  • vegetarians – ethical choice
  • vegans – ethical choice
  • low fat
  • fruitarians
  • high carbohydrate – sports people
  • high protein – body builders

Medical reasons for specific diets

In some cases there is a medical reason for a customer’s diet. Some examples of this are:

  • low sugar diet for those suffering from diabetes
  • low fat diet to reduce cholesterol
  • gluten free diet for those suffering from coeliac disease
  • high calcium diet for those with osteoporosis
  • high calorie diet for those who are underweight
