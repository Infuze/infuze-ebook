Introduction to selling and net price

The selling price and net price are both achieved through calculations:

  • Net price – the net price is the cost of the dish including all direct and indirect costs associated with making the dish.
  • Selling price – the selling price is the cost to the customer and is calculated by including the net price, profit margins and any taxes such as VAT.

Net price

The factors that contribute to a net price include:

  • direct costs – materials and labour
  • indirect costs:
    • administration
    • marketing
    • rent/mortgage
    • insurance
    • energy
    • shared labour costs.

Calculating the net price

When calculating the net price of any product or dish, all costs associated with the product or dish must be included. This includes the purchase costs of commodities to make the product or dish, and both the direct and indirect costs.

No taxes or profit are included at this stage.

Selling price

The selling price is the cost to the customer. This includes all costs and any discounts you may apply to the product or service.

The selling price is calculated adding together the:

  • net price
  • profit margin
  • taxes – VAT.

Calculating a selling price

Take the following example:

  • Net price = £2.00
  • Profit margin = 65%
  • VAT @ 20%.

In this example where the net price is £2, in order to meet a set profit margin (65%) on our selling price, the following calculation is required to work out the selling price:

  • 200 (£2) x 65/100 = 130 (profit margin in pence)
  • 200 + 130 = 330 (total net price and profit)
  • 330 x 20/100 = 66 (VAT)
  • 330 + 66 = 396 = £3.96 (Total selling price: net price with profit + VAT).

Net profit on sales

Take the following example:

  • Net sales = £10,000
  • Net costs = £6,500.

Net profit on sales is calculated as percentage using the following calculation:

  • 10,000 – 6,500 = 3,500 (net sales – net costs = net profit)
  • 3,500/10,000 = 0.35 (net profit/net sales)
  • 0.35 x 100 = 35% (net profit as a percentage).

Introduction to sales analysis and planning

Sales analysis and menu planning are key to the financial control of any kitchen.

  • Sales analysis provides an opportunity look at a set of figures to see any differences in sales or profits.
  • Menu planning provides an opportunity to deliver a menu to meet the needs of the organisation and the customer.

Sales analysis

Sales analysis:

  • allows an organisation to see which dishes or products have or haven’t sold well
  • will support the purchasing cycle and stocking of commodities
  • can measure the effectiveness of the production
  • will analyse against organisational goals.

A sales analysis is conducted against a previous report to show differences after the introduction of new products or services, or new production techniques or marketing materials.

Menu planning

The art of menu planning is a skill that allows a restaurant/hotel to meet the demands of the customers and their organisation. Menu planning needs an understanding of:

  • sales mix
  • menu engineering.

Considerations when menu planning include:

  • seasonality
  • costs
  • resources
  • target marketing.

Sales mix

Sales mix is the proportion of different dishes or products which make up the total sales of the organisation.

An analysis of the mix may show that some dishes or products do better than others, especially those with a greater profit margin.

If a decision is made to adjust the sales mix then the supply chain, personnel and output must first be looked at and taken into consideration.

Menu engineering

Menu engineering is a technique used to visually encourage customers subconsciously to purchase a target set of dishes, normally the highly profitable ones.

This is achieved through ‘engineering’ a menu to place certain dishes in what is known as ‘sweet spots’, drawing customers’ attention to them.

Another technique is to calculate the costs of a food, labour and packaging. From this the most expensive items will be placed either at the top or bottom of the menu.

Other considerations

Seasonality

A menu that is created must consider the seasonality of the available produce. Although commodities are now available throughout the year, when planning a menu the seasonal availability of these products should be considered due to:

  • increased cost associated with commodities that are out of season within the UK
  • the environmental impact of purchasing commodities out of season from abroad.

Other considerations

Costs

Costs have an impact on all aspects of menu planning including:

  • planning time
  • purchasing costs
  • development costs
  • production costs
  • delivery costs.

Other considerations

Resources

When planning any new menu, resources will need to be considered, including:

  • labour costs to plan, produce, train and deliver a new menu
  • the cost of supplying any new equipment
  • the cost of borrowing money to develop and deliver the new menu
  • the availability of commodities for the new menu.
  • time to plan the new menu in detail.

Other considerations

Target market

The planning of any new menu requires a focus on the target market. This is achieved by:

  • market research
  • staff knowledge
  • management experience.

Introduction

The resources of any kitchen need to be carefully managed if the organisation is to maximise its profitability. Resources include:

  • commodities
  • equipment
  • energy
  • staff.

Commodities

The cost of commodities can fluctuate throughout the year. Careful planning can help to offset this variation by:

  • selecting a good supplier
  • menu planning to use seasonal commodities
  • training staff to increase the yield by using the correct cooking and storage techniques.

Equipment

Equipment, especially static equipment, is very expensive and management should plan for a maximum lifetime of each piece in order to reduce costs. This is achieved by:

  • training the staff
  • planned maintenance activities
  • compliance with manufacturers’ instructions.

Purchasing power to reduce initial costs will also support this. Large companies can often drive down the costs of equipment due to the large amounts they are purchasing from suppliers.

Energy

The cost of energy has continually risen in recent years and this is now one of the largest indirect costs of producing dishes and products. The reduction of energy costs can be achieved by:

  • sourcing the best price
  • training the staff in correct storage, preparation, cooking techniques and energy conservation
  • purchasing energy-saving products such as ‘A’-rated microwaves and dish washers
  • employing energy wardens in large establishments.

Staff

The cost of staff is the single biggest expense associated with the hospitality industry. Staff productivity can be increased by:

  • motivation – time off, pay rise etc
  • training – made to feel valued
  • team-building exercises
  • time and motion exercise – being careful not to alienate staff during this process.

Staff

Other costs associated with staff include:

  • cost of recruitment
  • sick leave
  • poor attendance levels
  • poaching of staff by other organisations.

All the above must be carefully managed if the costs of your staff resource are not going to inflate your direct costs.

Value Added Tax

What is Value Added Tax (VAT)

Value Added Tax, or VAT, is a tax on services and goods within the European Union. According the European Directive VAT must be at least 15% but all member states are free to set their own rate either at 15% or above. Each state is also allowed to have two reduced rates. Within the United Kingdom the basic rate of VAT is 20%, there are also two reduced rates of 5% and 0%.

Within the hospitality industry, food supplied to the customer is taxed at a VAT rate of 20%. This is then collected from the customer, as part of their bill, and forwarded to the treasury.

Calculating VAT

Two calculations are required to in order to ensure the correct amount of VAT is paid to the treasury.

The first is the VAT paid by customers on goods or services provided by the organisation. This calculation is called the gross price and is calculated using the following sum:

Cost of goods or services multiplied by 20% then added together = Gross price of goods or services

The second calculation required is how to calculate VAT on goods and services paid for by the organisation. This is important as VAT paid by an organisation on any goods or services can be deducted from the VAT owed to the treasury on goods and services supplied by the organisation. This is called the net price and is calculated as follows:

Costs of goods or services including VAT divided by 1.2 = Net price of goods or services
