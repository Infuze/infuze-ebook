Introduction to purchasing

Purchasing is one of the most important factors in achieving a profitable business. Without the correct purchasing strategy, a business may never make a profit. Factors to consider include:

  • selection of suppliers
  • quality of goods
  • quantity of goods
  • price
  • storage
  • contract/conditions of supply.

Selection of supplier

The size of any food service business will have an effect on who they may choose as a supplier. Considerations on selecting a supplier include:

  • reputation
  • costs
  • quality of commodities
  • availability of commodities
  • delivery days
  • discounts
  • turnaround times.

Quality of goods

When selecting any supplier one of the most important considerations is the quality of goods or commodities as this can impact heavily on the final product.

The quality of any commodities purchased will also impact on storage, as poor-quality products will usually have a poor storage life.

Poor storage life can then lead to more wastage, reducing yield and affecting the overall profitability of the organisation.

Quality of goods

Can a supplier provide you with the number of commodities required of the correct quality and at the specified time?

A lack of supply will ultimately mean that the food business will not be able to supply their customers, both losing money and affecting their reputation.

Has the food business enough of a turnover to order large quantities of commodities in order to drive down the price?

The availability of storage in any food business will also need to be considered.

Quality of goods

Money is the driver of any business. Therefore the price that a supplier may charge is normally the most important factor when writing a purchasing plan.

Larger organisations can drive down the price from a supplier due to the large purchasing power they have. In some cases large organisations can receive a percentage of their purchasing costs back from the supplier at the end of the financial year.

Smaller companies must develop their relationship with their supplier in order to receive preferential rates or otherwise ‘shop around’.

Storage

Storage is important in purchasing, as the amount of storage space available will impact on:

  • number of delivery days – increase in costs if deliveries are required more often
  • multi-temperature deliveries – reduced costs due to reduced deliveries (because multi-temperature deliveries enables delivery of different types of commodities – fresh/frozen/dry – delivered on one vehicle, instead of using different vehicles for the different commodity types).

Other considerations due to storage include the ability to hold enough stock in case of ‘curve balls’ such as lack of deliveries or urgent customer requests.

Contract/conditions of supply

The contract/conditions of supply are extremely important when choosing the right supplier. The contract will include:

  • price
  • minimum spend/order
  • payment terms
  • quality specification
  • delivery schedule
  • transport and packaging.

Purchasing cycle

The purchasing cycle is the process that should be followed to ensure the efficient and effective supply of commodities or goods. This cycle includes:

  • requisition
  • purchase specification
  • quotation
  • purchase order
  • delivery/receipt of goods
  • returns/credit note
  • invoice
  • payment
  • statement.

Requisition

A requisition is an internal document used in larger organisations when requesting commodities or goods.

Within a purchase cycle the kitchen will identify the commodities or goods required and complete a requisition form which will then be forwarded to the purchasing department.

The purchasing department will have the financial authority to place a purchase order to meet the request.

Quotation

A quotation is the offer from the supplier as to what the costs of commodities or goods supplied to a customer will be.

A quotation or quote is normally provided before a purchase order is confirmed.

Purchase order

A purchase order is created to place an order for commodities or goods from a supplier. Purchase orders include the following information:

  • Supplier’s name
  • Supplier’s account number
  • Order number
  • Date
  • Stock code
  • Description
  • Unit of measure (UOM)
  • Quantity ordered
  • Unit cost
  • Signature.

Delivery/receipt of goods

When any goods or commodities are delivered they are accompanied by a delivery or receipt of goods form.

It is essential that the goods are received correctly and that the following is checked:

  • Quantity
  • Quality
  • Temperature.

The form will include all the details from the purchase order as well as the addition of space for signatures of the deliverer and the recipient.

Returns/credit note

A returns note is a form that a supplier may produce when commodities of goods are returned for various reasons, including:

  • quality
  • temperature.

The returns note will normally be signed by the deliverer (driver) to confirm they have received the goods back.

Following the return of commodities or goods a credit note will be issued by the supplier to credit the account of the food business.

Invoices

Invoices are compiled by the supplier to confirm to the food business the goods they have supplied. The information will allow the food business to check:

  • the costs are as agreed in the purchase order
  • the delivery note/receipt to prove that they were received in a satisfactory condition and in the correct quantity.

Invoices, like all other purchasing forms, will be numbered to make the accounting practices more efficient.

Payment

In most cases payments are made by the purchasing department, normally through the banking system, especially so in larger organisations.

Smaller organisations may pay in cash and will therefore require a receipt to confirm payment has been received.

Statement

Suppliers will normally produce a statement on a monthly basis for food businesses to reconcile their accounts. Statements will include the following details:

  • Date of statement
  • Recipient of the commodities or goods
  • Date of each invoice/credit note
  • Code of each invoice/credit note
  • Costs of each invoice/credit note
  • Total costs for the period in question.

Introduction to income statements and balance sheets

It is important that managers have the ability to look at the financial health of an organisation at any given time. This is achieved by organisations keeping their financial data up to date and in a preset format. These data include:

  • income statements
  • balance sheets.

Income statements

An income statement can also be known as a profit/loss account. It is a financial statement that summarises:

  • revenues
  • costs of sales
  • gross profit
  • labour costs
  • apportioned costs: administration, marketing, rent/mortgage, insurance, energy costs, banking costs
  • net profit/loss.

Profit/loss account

  • Revenue – this is the amount of money that the food business receives from selling its goods to customers.
  • Cost of sales – in the case of a food business, this is the cost of purchasing the commodities needed to deliver their goods.
  • Gross profit – the difference between revenue and cost of sales.

Profit/loss account

  • Labour costs – these are the costs an organisation pays for its employees. These are known as direct costs.
  • Apportioned costs – these include administration, marketing, energy, banking costs etc. These are known as indirect costs.
  • Net profit/loss – this is the difference between the gross profit and the costs, both direct and indirect costs.

Balance sheet

A balance sheet is a financial statement which shows the organisation’s assets, liabilities and the shareholders’ equity. Within the United Kingdom the law requires balance sheets to show the following information:

  • Fixed assets
  • Current assets
  • Current liabilities
  • Long-term liabilities.

A balance sheet will show the assets owned, the liabilities owed and the capital left in the organisation.

Fixed assets

Fixed assets include:

  • tangible assets
    • buildings and land
    • machinery and IT equipment
    • fixtures and fittings
  • intangible assets
    • intellectual property rights
    • long-term investments.

Current assets

Current assets include:

  • stock
  • work in progress
  • money owed by customers
  • cash in hand or in the bank
  • short-term investments
  • pre-payments such as rent.

Current liabilities

Current liabilities are those amounts of money owed or due to be paid within 12 months. These include:

  • taxes – VAT, PAYE, NI
  • money owed to any suppliers
  • any short-term loans
  • overdrafts or other finance.

Long-term liabilities

These include:

  • any creditors who are due to be paid after one year including bank loans and other finances
  • pension liabilities
  • bond issues – bonds are another type of loan from an investor who will receive interest and the original amount back at the end of a set period.

Capital

Capital is also known as the shareholder equity and reserves. It is also sometimes called the ‘book value’ of an organisation.

The capital represents the overall value that is left when all the liabilities have been paid.

This value represents how strong or weak an organisation is financially.

What is a profit/loss account?

Organisations will keep various financial records to monitor the performance and the profitability or loss-making of their activities. One of these records is a profit/loss account.

A profit/loss account provides organisations with the necessary data to understand the following:

  • Where the organisation is making or losing money
  • The cost of selling the products or services.

The account can be divided into three distinct areas:

  • Sales
  • Cost of sales
  • Overheads.

Sales

The first section is the amount of money that an organisation has received from selling their goods and/or services. This is the money they receive from their customers.

Cost of sales

This section covers the cost of providing the goods or services. Cost of sales are also known as ‘direct costs’. They include:

  • costs to purchase ingredients
  • staff costs to provide the dish
  • electricity/gas costs.

Overheads

Overheads are those costs that cannot be attributed directly to the supply of the product or service. They are also known as ‘indirect costs’ and can include:

  • administration
  • advertising
  • insurance
  • training
  • rent/mortgage
  • interest on any loans.
