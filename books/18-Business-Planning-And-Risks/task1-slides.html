Introduction to costing and waste

Catering operations must not only appeal to customers; they must also return a profit.

The menu and pricing must also take into account the variations in cost of food. Meat, vegetables and seafood fluctuate according to their seasonal availability and demand. If demand is high the prices will also rise.

In order for any establishment to remain in profit, then careful costing of dishes and any wastage must be calculated correctly.

Dish costing

In order for any dish to be correctly costed there are a number of factors that must be considered, including:

  • quality of products
  • waste
  • yield.

Quality of commodities

The quality of any commodities purchased will affect any costing:

  • Poor quality commodities provide a less quality product, so you may not be able to deliver the dish to specification.
  • Poor quality commodities generally have a shorter shelf life.

Waste

Waste will affect the yield available from any given product. Waste takes the form of:

  • wastage of the commodity when trimmed or peeled
  • wastage due to incorrect weighing of ingredients, poor storing, over-ordering, incorrect cooking techniques used, over-production etc.

Dish costing

In order for a chef to calculate the costs of any dish they are required to know the following:

  • Ingredients required
  • Specification
  • Weight/size of each ingredient
  • Cost per weight/size
  • Number of portions the recipe provides.

Dish costing

Below is an example of a costing for scrambled eggs.

Item Specification Weight kg/L/unit Cost per kg/L/unit Actual Cost
Eggs Large 60g free range 4 each £0.60 each £2.40
Butter Salted 0.050 kg £4.00 kg £0.20
Salt Sea salt 0.002 kg £10.00 kg £0.02
Pepper Salted 0.050 kg £25.00 kg £0.05
Total Cost £2.67
No. of serves 2 Cost per portion £1.34

The calculation is as follows:

  • Multiply the weight or unit by the cost. For example the eggs would be 4 × 0.60 = £2.40.
  • Add up the actual costs for all the items: 2.40 + 0.20 + 0.02 + 0.05 = £2.67.
  • Divide the total by the number of servings: 2.67 ÷ 2 = £1.34 (rounded up) per serving.

Yield

Yield is the total amount of a commodity that is available once the preparation of the commodity has taken place, for example, the total weight of a carrot after it has been peeled. Two examples are provided below of the calculation required to estimate the yield of a commodity (in both grams and %).

Product Gross (g) Waste/ bones (g) Usable offcuts (g) Yield (g) Waste/ bones (%) Offcuts (%) Yield (%)
Peeled carrots 180g 102g 0g 78g 57% 0% 43%
Beef striploin 2,890g 830g 0g 2,060g 29% 0% 71%

Yield and costing

When costing any dish the yield of the commodity is most important. Without knowing the correct yield the chef will not be able to provide a realistic costing of the finished dish or product. Chefs must consider the:

  • actual weight required of a commodity
  • amount of wastage in the preparation method
  • weight loss during the cooking phase
  • portion size according to the specification sheet.

Introduction to stock management

As a chef working in a professional kitchen, it is important to consider stock management and its importance.

  • Availability of suppliers and frequency of delivery: It is important that your establishment can trust its suppliers. The length of time between placing an order and receiving the food commodities is called the lead time, or the delivery time. The lead time and the frequency of ordering will affect the quantities you order.
    • The less frequently you order, the larger the quantities you will need to order to maintain stocks until the next delivery.
    • On the other hand, if orders are placed frequently, you will need to order smaller amounts to maintain levels between deliveries.

Introduction to stock management

  • Commodities that meet organisational/customer requirements: You need to decide whether to order food commodities locally or nationally, and to select the best supplier you can. Consider the following when making these decisions:
    • price
    • quality
    • quantity
    • delivery times
    • sustainability reputation
    • reliability of the product
    • customer expectations

Introduction to stock management

  • Financial impact: Before placing an order, the restaurant will need to do a cost estimate to check they are within budget. Do not select a supplier on the basis of price alone. The cheapest suppliers may provide poor quality products, delivery and service.
  • Remember that excess stock on the shelves will have a cost attached to it. You must keep a balance between the stock you require and the amount of stock you hold.

Stock management considerations

  • Managing waste: If stock is not correctly managed and stored there may be wastage, which is a cost to the business.
  • When you have decided what food commodities and equipment you need, you then need to calculate or estimate what quantities of each of these items is required. It is important to order the right quantities.

Stock management considerations

  • Ordering too little (understocking) will result in shortages and your restaurant will be unable to provide an effective service, undermining staff and customers’ confidence in the restaurant.
  • Ordering too much (overstocking) will result in a build-up of stock and possible food wastage – for example, the food commodities that are not used before their best before or use by date, or that become spoiled if unused – as well as tying up valuable funds unnecessarily.

Stock management considerations

  • Quantity of commodities: The stock level is the quantity of a food commodity that is available for use in the kitchen in a given period of time.
    • The reserve stock is the lowest level of stock for each food item, and quantities should not be allowed to fall below this level.
    • Your reserve stocks are essentially extra supplies to ensure that there are no cases of stock not being available if there is an unexpected increase in demand, or a delay in delivery of food supplies.
    • The quantity of reserve stock you need depends on the average daily, weekly and monthly consumption and the lead ordering time.

Stock management considerations

  • Record keeping: Reliable record keeping is very important, because errors caused by poor record keeping will affect the overall service delivery in the kitchen.
    • Stock control includes keeping accurate and reliable records of food commodities received and issued, stock taking (checking stocks on a regular basis) and carrying out an inventory of stock.
    • A stock control system uses tools such as stock record cards and a stock control spreadsheet. Effective stock control is important to help you order the right quantities of supplies. Ideally, you should carry out a stock take before placing an order for more food commodities, but this will be decided by the size of your establishment and stock size.

Stock management considerations

  • Seasonal demand: In some areas of food supply with certain availability during the different seasons, prices are at their lowest when large quantities of stock are available.
    • Food commodity supplies are low at the start of the harvest season, so prices are high. Prices may drop as supply increases. At the end of the season, prices normally increase again as supply diminishes.
    • Prices are generally highest during the off-season, when only a small percentage of farmers are able to grow the crop.
    • Certain food commodities are seasonal or favourite items at certain times of the year, such as turkey (Christmas) and game birds (hunting season). As a kitchen you will need to correctly forecast your supply and demand during this period to ensure you have sufficient stock.

Stock management considerations

  • Security: To ensure security, take reasonable precautions.
    • Keep the food commodity store locked.
    • Make one person responsible for looking after the supplies, keeping the keys and locking the store. Appoint someone else to be in charge when the person normally responsible is away.
    • Avoid having more than two sets of keys lying around the kitchen, and make sure there is always one set securely available in the kitchen.

Stock management considerations

  • Stock rotation: Rotate stock according to the use by and best before dates using the SLFO (Shortest Life First Out) and FIFO (First In First Out) rules. Put items that have the latest use by and best before dates at the back, and items with the earliest expiry dates at the front.
  • Storage capacity: The kitchen storeroom should have enough space to put all your food commodities on shelves or pallets, ensuring everything is off the ground. Organise the kitchen store in a simple and logical way so that items can be found quickly and easily.
