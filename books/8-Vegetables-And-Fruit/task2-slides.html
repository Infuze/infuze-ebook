Precision cuts

Precision cuts are used to add colour and structure to menu items. These cuts display the chef’s skill and attention to detail. Precision cuts are used for a variety of vegetables such as celery, leek, carrots, turnips and swedes.

For example, julienne of vegetables is often used for soups, as garnish on meat dishes or with seafood dishes.

Scallops with lemon oil and vegetable brunoise is a typical example in which the vegetables add texture and colour to a rich, fragrant soup.

Macédoine or jardinière can also be used for fruit e.g. fruit salad.

Cut sizes vary to some extent, depending whether the vegetables are used as a soup garnish, as a base for poaching fish, in a salad or on top of a main course.

Precision cuts

  • Julienne – 40 x 2 x 2mm
  • Macédoine – 5 x 5 x 5mm
  • Jardinière – 15 x 5mm
  • Brunoise – 2 x 2 x 2mm
  • Paysanne – 10 x 10 x 2mm
  • Mirepoix – no specific size

Preparation methods of fruit and vegetables

  • Health, safety and hygiene requirements must always be considered during the preparation of vegetables, fruit and pulses. Cutting and cooking food always involves some risks. However, these can be minimised by supervision, training, reinforcement and following correct procedures.
  • Washing vegetables and fruit prior to use is essential for removing dust, bacteria, pesticides, wax and other contaminants.
  • When peeling vegetables try to minimise waste by using a peeler (rather than a knife) and ensure that all of the outside skin has been removed unless a pattern is desired for presentation.

Preparation methods for fruit and vegetables

  • Following the wash, peel, re-wash process will help you maintain high standards of hygiene – people have died as a result of improper hygiene practices relating to vegetable and fruit items.
  • The most common preparation processes are slicing, dicing, turning and cutting items into evenly sized pieces.
  • Onions are usually diced and sliced for a multitude of dishes and are often pre-fried in oil/butter without colour to speed up the cooking process during service and improve storage.
  • Garlic can be sliced or chopped and is often stored in oil to prevent oxidisation.
  • Fresh herbs are sliced or chopped and used as a flavouring agent and as garnish.
  • Flavourings are used to enhance the taste of vegetables and fruit. Common types are herbs, soy sauce, and curry pastes, and stock syrup, cinnamon or vanilla for fruit.

Preparation methods of fruit and vegetables

  • Other preparation skills used are peeling or turning, for example potatoes or carrots. Grating or shredding is also used for rösti or hash browns.
  • Stuffing vegetables such as courgettes or tomatoes means they need to be de-seeded or hollowed out first. Flat mushrooms can also be stuffed and then grilled.
  • Peeled potatoes may be stored in water to prevent oxidisation. Turning, grating or using parisienne cutters or a mandolin help create a wide variety of shapes and final products such as sliced boulangère, piped duchess or cut parmentier potatoes. Drain potatoes well when making duchess mixture otherwise the mixture will be too soft.

Preparation methods – blanching

  • Blanching vegetables will speed up service due to the reduced cooking time. The vegetables may be tied into individual portions, for example bean bundles using chives or bacon.
  • Blanching potatoes in fat will prevent oxidisation and shorten cooking time during service.

Preparation methods for mushrooms

Mushrooms should not be wrapped in plastic as they will sweat. Instead they should be stored in paper bags or cloth to allow them to breathe and away from light to prevent oxidation.

Brush quickly or rinse mushrooms before cooking.

Do not let them sit in water as they will absorb it and their flavour will be diluted.

The whole mushroom is edible, but the stem can be hard so may be removed. If you remove stems or skin you can use the trimmings in farces or sauces.

Preparation methods for pulses and beans

Pulses and beans need to be checked for stones and soaked prior to cooking.

Either a rapid or long soak method can be used. In both cases, always drain off the soaking liquid, rinse and then cook in fresh water without any salt, as salt toughens the skins and extends cooking times.

Cooking times will vary depending upon the type and age of the pulse.

Storage

Storage of prepared ingredients for fruit and vegetable dishes is vital for the safety of your customer. Food safety and hygiene regulations must be complied with at all times.

  • Temperature
  • Date labelling
  • Coverings
  • Positioning
  • Stock rotation

Storage of vegetables, fruit and nuts

  • Vegetables and fruit should be stored in clean containers in the fridge or coolroom, with the exception of bananas - their skin turns brown when chilled, so they are better kept in dry storage.
  • Prevent unnecessary handling of vegetables and fruit and do not wash them until they are needed for service.
  • Nuts are high in fat content so are perishable and can become rancid quickly in hot weather. They should be stored in sealed containers in the dry store.

Storage of vegetables, fruit and nuts

  • Vegetables, fruit and nuts can be quite expensive out of season and therefore it is important to be aware of when to use fresh commodities and when to utilise dried, canned or frozen vegetables and fruit.
  • Vegetables and fruit can be purchased in a variety of ways, all of which have advantages and disadvantages.
  • The available kitchen, storage and fridge/coolroom space, as well as the size of the menu and the number of kitchen staff, will also influence how you purchase vegetables and fruit.
  • If you have only a small kitchen and one small fridge/coolroom it may be advisable to get your fresh vegetables delivered pre-cut.
  • Consider the advantages and disadvantages of the various products when you plan your menus. Cleaned/washed vegetables such as potatoes might be more expensive but once you consider the time involved in preparing unwashed vegetables, they often work out cheaper overall.

Storage considerations

  • In general, fresh vegetables and fruit can be stored for a week to a fortnight in the fridge/coolroom and root vegetables 1–3 months in a cool and dark dry store or coolroom.
  • Dried vegetables and fruit need to be stored in their original containers or in sealed bins. Most frozen vegetables and fruit are fine for 3–6 months at –18°C.
  • Canned vegetables and fruit need to be in the dry store on shelves off the floor.
  • To minimise handling, store the produce in the containers in which they have been delivered. Rotate all stock (first in first out) to comply with HACCP regulations. Consider contamination issues such as wooden crates.

Storage considerations

  • Cover vegetables using a damp, clean cloth or clingfilm to prevent evaporation.
  • Store potatoes in a dark room to prevent them from going green, which is evidence of a toxin (solanine) that develops under the skin of potatoes with exposure to light. This can cause bitterness and food poisoning if consumed in large amounts.
  • Do not store potatoes in the fridge, as they are affected by moisture and develop mould. Bagged potatoes should be kept well ventilated and are often stored in the cellar.
  • Onions should never be stored in the fridge/coolroom, as they go soft and their smell will influence other foods.

Storage considerations

  • Handle all produce with care. Avocados, lettuce, asparagus and berries are good examples of produce that discolours or bruises quickly if handled incorrectly.
  • Temperature control is also important with these delicate items – if the back of your fridge is too cold, your lettuce could freeze and go limp when thawed.
  • When receiving vegetables and fruit, ensure the delivery reflects the delivery paperwork/document. Take random checks of the quality of the product (including from the bottom of the box). Sign the paperwork if it is correct and then store the stock as quickly as possible to minimise spoilage and bacterial growth.
  • Chef’s hint: Cold air sinks, so the coldest part of your refrigerator is on the bottom shelf. In a coolroom, the shelf furthest from the door and nearest the floor will be the coldest spot.

Temperature

The following needs to be considered for all fruit and vegetable ingredients and dishes:

  • Ideally used fresh daily. Otherwise:
    • must be stored chilled between 5°C and 8°C
    • must be stored labelled in a fridge
    • if freezing must be –18°C or below.
  • If cooked fruit and vegetable ingredients and dishes are stored in a freezer:
    • they must be reheated to the legal required temperature for two minutes before use.
    • they must not be reheated more than once, especially when delicate vegetables
  • Hot holding must be above 63°C.

Positioning and stock rotation

When storing any fruit and vegetable ingredients and dishes you must always consider the possibility of cross-contamination. All fruit and vegetable ingredients and dishes not for immediate use should be:

  • covered to prevent cross-contamination
  • stored in a fridge or freezer
  • labelled to ensure stock rotation – ‘first in first out’.

Date labelling and coverings

When labelling any fruit and vegetable ingredients and dishes, the following guidelines should be followed:

  • Name of product
  • Date of production
