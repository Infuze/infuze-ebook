Introduction to producing biscuit, cakes and sponges

Various techniques are used in the production of biscuits, cakes and sponges. Some examples can be seen below:

  • Sugar batter method
  • Melting method
  • Separated egg method
  • Sabayon method
  • Rubbing-in method
  • Chemical method.

Sugar batter method

The sugar batter method is used in the making of Victoria sponge and Viennese biscuits:

  • The fat and sugar are mixed to produce a light and aerated cream
  • Eggs are beaten and added to the mix slowly
  • The remaining ingredients are then folded into the mix – folding prevents the aeration from collapsing.

Melting method

The melting method is used to enrich and prolong the shelf life of cakes such as parkin and ginger cake:

  • Eggs and sugar are whisked until ribbons are formed (when the mixture is aerated, thicker and lighter)
  • The other ingredients are folded into the mix
  • The final stage is for the butter, margarine or oil to be poured into the mix.

Separated egg method

This method is used for products such as sponge fingers:

  • Yolks, sugar and vanilla are whisked over warm water
  • Whisk the whites and sugar to form a soft meringue
  • Fold the yolk mix into the meringue
  • Finally fold in the flour carefully.

Sabayon method

A sabayon is a batter that is used in making a Genoise or Swiss rolls:

  • Whisk the sugar and eggs over a bowl of hot water until doubled in volume
  • Remove from heat and continue to whisk until cold
  • Other ingredients are then added to this batter.

Rubbing-in method

The rubbing-in method is utilised when making shortbreads and rock cakes:

  • Sieve the flour and combine it with the salt and butter
  • Using fingertips, rub the ingredients together until the texture of breadcrumbs is achieved
  • Add the sugar and combine with the egg.

Chemical method

Scones use the chemical method to achieve the rise.

  • Sieve the flour and combine it with the baking powder and salt
  • Combine the butter with the flour mix to a sandy texture
  • Dissolve the sugar in the cream and combine it with the dry ingredients
  • Do NOT overwork the mix.

Fillings

There are many different fillings available to the chef when producing biscuits, cakes and sponges. These include the following:

  • Buttercream
  • Chantilly cream
  • Crème pâtissière
  • Fruits
  • Pastes and spreads
  • Ganache.

Buttercream

Buttercream is made using the following method:

  • Sieve the icing sugar
  • Cream the icing sugar and butter together.

Buttercream can be coloured and flavoured as required.

Chantilly cream

Chantilly cream is used to fill and decorate cakes and sponges.

  • Whisk caster sugar, whipping cream and vanilla essence over ice until soft peaks are formed.

Crème pâtissière

Also known as pastry cream, this cream is made as follows:

  • Heat the milk with the vanilla pod and leave to infuse the flavour
  • Beat the yolks and sugar until creamy, then add the flour and custard powder
  • Strain the milk and blend with the sugar mix
  • Place on heat and bring to the boil, constantly stirring the mix
  • Chill immediately.

Fruits

Fruits can be used in several forms to both fill and decorate:

  • Fresh fruit
  • Jams
  • Fruit purée
  • Fruit mousses.

Pastes and spreads

Pastes and spreads are both convenient and flavoursome. They are used as a filling and to decorate and enhance the final product.

  • Chocolate spread
  • Praline
  • Nutella
  • Lemon curd.

Ganache

Ganache, used as a filling, contains the following ingredients:

  • Chocolate
  • Butter
  • Vanilla
  • Double cream.

When using ganache as a covering the vanilla is replaced with a spirit or liqueur.

Finishing

Finishing biscuits, cakes and sponges is achieved in many ways, such as:

  • dredging
  • piping
  • coating
  • soaking
  • decorations
  • feathering
  • portioning.

Dredging

Dredging is similar to dusting with icing sugar, the difference being dredging leaves a heavier coating

This technique is used to decorate a cake or sponge.

Piping

Piping is a skill used to enhance the appearances of biscuits, cakes and sponges. The following are some preparations that may be used to pipe:

  • Water icing
  • Royal icing
  • Whipped cream
  • Chocolate
  • Fondant
  • Meringue.

Coating

Coating provides the chef with another opportunity to enhance a cake or gateau. Possible preparations include the following:

  • Chocolate
  • Royal icing
  • Buttercream
  • Meringue
  • Fondant
  • Whipped cream.

Soaking

Soaking allows the chef to introduce more moisture to a cake or torte.

A good stock syrup is required and the sponge or cake must be cool before applying the syrup.

An example of using the soaking method is a rum baba.

Decorations

There are many decorations available to help the chef to enhance the appearance of biscuits, cakes and sponges. For example:

  • Chocolate rolls/flakes/run-outs
  • Nuts – whole, flaked, chopped
  • Crystallised fruits
  • Crystallised angelica
  • Edible flowers.

Feathering

Feathering is the art of decorating a cake with either icing or chocolate and feathering through the layer with a contrasting colour. For example:

  • Bakewell tart
  • Chocolate gateau.

Portioning

Portioning allows the chef to divide the cake or sponge for the customers whilst keeping the visual appearance of the whole cake or sponge.

It can achieved through piping, using run-outs of chocolate or sugar work. It can be used, for example, on:

  • Black Forest gateau
  • praline torte/gateau.

Common faults

When chefs make any type of biscuit, cake or sponge, there are many things that can go wrong. This can affect the texture, flavour and the final appearance of any of these products. Some of the common faults are listed below.

Substituting ingredients

In today’s world of healthier eating and people trying to reduce their sugar and fat intake there is a drive to substitute certain ingredients, such as stevia for sugar, especially in preparation and cooking of biscuits, cakes and sponges. The substitution of ingredients can cause many problems from changing the flavour of the final product to overcooking due to too much liquid content.

The simple answer to this problem is not to substitute ingredients. Better to find a recipe that has been tried and tested by others. With the advent of the internet many many chefs and bakers now place their recipes online for others to follow.

Measuring the ingredients

Another common fault lies in the measuring of the ingredients before actually preparing the biscuits, cakes or sponges. Whilst biscuits are little more forgiving, cake and sponge recipes must be followed to the gram.

Again follow the recipe, make sure you understand the unit of measure that is being used in the recipe.

Uneven cooking

There are many reasons why a biscuit, cake or sponge may show signs of uneven cooking. One of the these is the lack of preheating the oven beforehand. Not only is the texture affected but also cooking times may be increased, resulting in a dry finished product. Uneven cooking may also occur when there is too much liquid in the recipe, if it hasn’t been followed, therefore causing a crust to be made before the centre is cooked. Uneven cooking may also occur when chefs continually open the door to check on their ‘masterpiece’  this will interfere with the rising of the biscuit, cake or sponge.

It is essential that an oven is preheated before the biscuit, cake or sponge mix is placed in it. This will help to ensure even cooking and the quality of the final product. Only open the oven door when necessary and only a little at a time to prevent heat loss.

Moist bases

The potential for biscuits, cakes or sponges to have a moist base is always there and this is due to the lack of escape routes for the steam that has been created.

To solve the issue of a moist base simply allow the biscuits, cakes or sponges to cool slightly, for about five minutes in the cooking tin/tray, and then remove and place them on a wire rack.

Icing issues

There are times when icing a cake turns into a nightmare, when the cake is still warm and you slowly attempt to ice the cake. If the cake is too warm, then there is a chance the butter in butter icing will melt. There is also a possibility of the cake disintegrating and mixing in with the icing, causing a crumble effect.

Allow the cake to stand before icing until absolutely cooled to prevent issue with icing cakes or sponges.

Fruit has sunk

There are times when fruit, raisins/currants/mixed peel/cherries etc are added to recipes, such as Christmas cake or Dundee cake. One of the issues when adding fruit is that it all sinks to the bottom. This may be due to several reasons such as the fruit being wet or too much liquid in the mix. It may also occur when the temperature is too low, allowing the fruit to sink to the bottom.

To overcome these problems, ensure the recipe is followed for both the ingredients and the temperatures required. You may also dust your fruit in flour before adding them to the mix.

Finally

There are many other pitfalls when making biscuits, cakes and sponges but experience and practice will help any chef meet the quality they are searching for.

Recipe – Genoise

Genoise is a cake named after the Italian city of Genoa and has been recognised as having a superior texture, flavour and finish if completed correctly.

Ingredients

  • 300g butter
  • 8 eggs
  • 300g caster sugar
  • 350g flour
  • Pinch of salt

Preparation method

  • Whisk the eggs and sugar over a bain-marie until the mixture has doubled in size and ribbons are formed
  • Sieve the flour and salt, fold this into the egg mixture very carefully
  • Melt the butter and fold this into the mix
  • Using a greased and lined cake tin place in an oven at 190°C for 30 minutes
  • Allow to cool in the tin and turn out when cold or required.

Overview

The secret to ensuing the the Genoise is of the best quality is to:

  • follow the recipe
  • carefully fold in the flour
  • carefully fold in the butter
  • make sure the oven is preheated and do not slam the cooker door shut.

A Genoise can be used for the making of all types of cakes. Due to its superior quality it also lends itself a longer storage life.

Some examples of the use of a Genoise mix can be seen below:

A strawberry cake of layered Genoise, mousse and finished with a strawberry jelly and fresh fruit A plain Genoise with a light dusting of icing sugar A strawberry shortcake of Genoise, whipped cream, strawberry coulis and fresh strawberries A layered fruit cake of cream and fruit Madeleines can also be made from a Genoise mix

Whilst the basic Genoise mixture has not really changed, there are now commercially available sponge mixes in the market. However, these do not provide the quality product of freshly made mixes. As with all convenience products the chefs must decide between savings in time and money versus the quality required.

Recipe – praline gateau

A praline gateau can be one of the most complex gateaux to make. Not only do you need to make a Genoise sponge but also praline and butter cream.

Genoise sponge – ingredients

  • 300g margarine
  • 8 eggs
  • 300g caster sugar
  • 350g flour
  • Pinch of salt

Praline – ingredients

  • 200g almonds
  • 200g hazelnuts
  • 800g sugar
  • 250ml water
  • 1 lemon
  • 150g glucose

Buttercream – ingredients

  • 450g icing sugar
  • 450g butter
  • 125ml evaporated milk
  • Essence and colouring as required

Decoration – ingredients

  • Toasted flaked almonds and
  • Physalis

Buttercream – method

  • Sieve the icing sugar.
  • Lightly cream the butter to soften it, then gradually add the sugar, beating vigorously.
  • Add the milk and also any essence or colouring.

Praline – method

  • Blanch and peel the almonds.
  • Roast and skin the hazelnuts.
  • Chop the nuts together.
  • Cook together the sugar, glucose, lemon juice and water until golden brown.
  • Add the chopped nuts and pour onto a marble slab or a stainless steel table top.
  • Allow to cool then remove from the slab and break the praline into smaller pieces using a rolling pin.

Genoise – method

  • Whisk the eggs and sugar over a bain-marie until the mixture has doubled in size and ribbons are formed.
  • Sieve the flour and salt, and fold this into the egg mixture very carefully.
  • Melt the margarine and fold this into the mix.
  • Using a greased and lined cake tin, place in an oven at 190°C for 30 minutes.
  • Allow to cool in the tin and turn out when cold or required.

To make the gateau – method

  • Crush the praline and mix it with the buttercream. Retain some of the praline for decoration.
  • Slice the cake into three even layers.
  • Spread the three layers of Genoise with the butter cream.
  • Coat the sides with the buttercream, then decorate with the flaked almonds.

The top of the cake can be finished with either the buttercream or ganache (as in the picture above). Decorations can include crushed praline, whirls of cream, triangle of praline or physalis.

Modern takes on this classical gateau include sugar work, praline discs and various other decorations to make the gateau more appealing to the eye.
