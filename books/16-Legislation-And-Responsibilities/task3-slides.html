Introduction to the types of hazards

All supervisors need to understand the types of hazards that can be found in a professional kitchen. These include:

  • Microbial
  • Chemical
  • Physical
  • Allergenic

Microbial hazards

Typical microbial hazards include:

  • Salmonella
    The main cause of food poisoning in the UK. Can be found in poultry, eggs and in the human intestine. Causes fever, vomiting and diarrhoea.
  • Campylobacter
    Found in meat and poultry and in untreated water. Also spread by pests, such as birds and insects. Causes headaches, diarrhoea and stomach pains.
  • E-coli 015 VTEC
    Found in untreated sewage, raw meat, untreated milk etc. Symptoms include stomach aches, nausea and blood in a person’s stools. This can lead to death.

Pathogenic bacteria

Pathogenic bacteria cause food poisoning in different ways:

  • Sheer numbers infected on the food product or equipment that are then ingested.
  • Some are spore-forming – these can withstand extreme heat and unfavourable conditions, only to return as the conditions become more favourable in the future.
  • The production of toxins that are either secreted from the cells (exotoxins) or released when the cells die (endotoxins).

Common symptoms of suffering from pathogenic bacteria include diarrhoea, vomiting, stomach ache etc.

Bacterial growth

Bacteria grow through either binary fission (the continuous splitting of the cells to produce more) or by vegetative reproduction, as in the cases of moulds.

The ideal conditions for the growth of bacteria include:

  • Supply of nutrients
  • Supply of water
  • The right temperature, known as the danger zone, which is 5-63°C.
  • Time – bacteria can reproduce very quickly in the right conditions (every 10–20 minutes)

Bacterial growth

The numbers of bacteria grow in a specific manner, this is known as the bacterial growth line:

  • Lag – the bacteria are getting used to their surroundings before multiplication takes place
  • Log – rapid growth of bacteria takes place during this phase
  • Stationary – the number of bacteria has reached an extent that there is now an equal number reproduced as those that die
  • Decline – lack of resources and nutrients or other factors, such as temperature fluctuations

Bacteria types

Some bacteria specifically support growth in extreme temperatures outside the danger Zone:

  • Psychotropic
    Can multiply in unfavourable heat conditions; they enjoy the cold -15 to +15°C
  • Mesophilic
    This type enjoys a temperature range of +20 to +45°C
  • Thermophilic
    This bacteria can withstand a temperature range form +45 to +121°C

Physical hazards

Typical physical hazards include:

  • Parts of broken machinery, such as screws, nuts and bolts etc
  • Packaging material that has not been removed correctly
  • String from trussing birds or tying meat
  • Insects
  • Blue plasters from food handlers

Common symptoms of a physical hazard include choking, broken teeth and vomiting.

Chemical hazards

Typical chemical hazards include:

  • Cleaning chemicals not being rinsed correctly from machinery and equipment
  • Over-spraying cleaning chemicals in the professional kitchen
  • Residue from drugs used by a vet
  • Residue from chemicals used in farming
  • Common symptoms of suffering a chemical hazard include eyes, nose, throat and skin irritation; sickness and vomiting

Allergens

In December 2014 the EU introduced new legislation for a food business to identify 14 allergens to consumers. This information must be provided, whether the food is cooked or raw.

In the case of food producers/suppliers, these can be found on the labels of food, normally with the allergens typed in bold – although this is not a requirement.

Some producers provide the information by stating that their product contains, or may contain, one of the allergens.

Common symptoms of these allergens include anaphylactic shock, asthma-like symptoms, difficulty in breathing and swollen lips and windpipes.

Allergens

The 14 identified allergens are:

  • Celery
  • Cereals containing gluten – including wheat, rye barley and oats
  • Crustaceans – lobster and crabs
  • Eggs
  • Fish
  • Lupin – found in flower
  • Milk

Allergens

The 14 identified allergens are:

  • Molluscs – mussels and oysters
  • Mustard – including seeds
  • Tree nuts – almonds, walnuts, cashews etc
  • Peanuts
  • Sesame seeds
  • Soya
  • Sulphur dioxide – also known as sulphates and found as a preservative in food and drink

Types of allergen

Celery

This includes celery stalks, leaves, seeds and the root called celeriac. You can find celery in:

  • Celery salt
  • Salads
  • Some meat products
  • Soups
  • Stocks
  • Stock cubes

Types of allergen

Cereals

Cereals such as wheat, rye, barley and oats contain gluten

  • Some foods that may contain a cereal include:
    • Flour
    • Batter
    • Breadcrumbs
    • Breads
    • Cakes
    • Couscous
    • Meat products
    • Pasta
    • Pastry
    • Sauces
    • Soups
    • Fried foods which are dusted in flour.

Types of allergen

Crustaceans are crabs, lobster, barnacles and shrimp and these are one of the 14 allergens. Ingredients to look out for:

Introduction to training and communication

All food businesses are required to comply with legislation, including requirements for the training of all food handlers. This includes:

  • Induction training
  • Ongoing training

It is also key that management have a clear policy on communication with their staff and the benefits that such communication can achieve.

Training

The 2006 Food Hygiene Regulations require a food business to conduct training for their staff.

Staff need to be trained to a level commensurate with the tasks they undertake. That is to say, a food assistant in a professional kitchen should be trained to a level that recognises the work they are undertaking. As such, they do not need to complete the in-depth training required of a supervisor.

The training can take many forms:

  • Formal training provided by the business
  • Training provided by a training provider
  • Training that results in a recognised qualification

Induction training

Induction training should be provided to all new employees.

  • This integrates employees into the policies and procedures of the food business
  • Provides a basic awareness of food hygiene/safety matters
  • It proves due diligence is being carried out by the employer

Records should be kept and stored securely.

All food businesses are also required to have at least one employee who is trained in the principles of HACCP. This allows the business to implement these principles in accordance with the law.

Ongoing training

On-going training is also a requirement under the 2006 Act.

  • Supervisors should continually observe employees to ensure compliance with the organisation’s policies and to meet the legal requirements placed on the business.
  • Any discrepancies should be recorded and corrective actions, including further training, provided if required.
  • Annual refresher training should be considered for all food handlers, including training to meet legislation requirements and in order to understand and abide by the business’ own policies.
  • Records must be kept.

Communication

All organisations – in the hospitality industry, as well as other industries – require an effective communication policy. The professional kitchen is no different and time spent on developing this policy will help a business and staff to:

  • Prove that due diligence has taken place:
    Providing written evidence that the policies and practices of the business are being adhered to, along with supporting records.
  • Understand the policies and procedures of the business:
    Good communications within the business will ensure all employees are fully aware of the regulatory requirements and also the policies and procedures to meet these requirements.

Communication

  • Raise the awareness of staff to potential problems or issues:
    Good communications will ensure employers can quickly pass advice to all employees.
  • Encourage good working relationships:
    Good working relationships are a must in any business. Communication skills are essential if these relationships are to be built and maintained.
  • Help reduce possible foodborne illnesses and food poisoning outbreaks:
    All the above points will allow an organisation to act quickly and effectively to prevent or reduce further outbreaks by informing staff of any issues or potential issues that effect food safety.

Communication

Communication takes many forms within the professional kitchen, these include:

  • Induction training
  • Ongoing (refresher) training
  • Posters
  • Leaflets
  • Diaries
  • Staff meetings

Supervisors must make sure the communication method is effective. This can be checked by checking employees’ practices and by questioning employees during meetings, while supervising and mentoring, and also through audits.
