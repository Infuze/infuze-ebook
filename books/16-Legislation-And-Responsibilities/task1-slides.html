Introduction to food safety management

There are many aspects in a food business that require detailed input from the management team and none is more important than food safety.

As part of the management of food safety, supervisors also have an important part to play.

This presentation will focus on the following areas:

  • The importance of food safety management procedures
  • Types of food safety management procedures used in a professional kitchen

What is a food safety management system?

VIDEO SCREEN

Food safety management procedures

The reasons a food business needs to implement food safety management procedures include:

  • Compliance with legislation, such as the Food Safety Act 1990
  • To avoid causing harm to customers
  • To avoid legal action from government agencies such as Environmental Health Departments
  • To avoid civil action – both personal and against the business
  • To ensure the safe delivery, storage, preparation, cooking and service of food, in order to demonstrate:
    • due diligence

Food safety management procedures

There are many procedures that need to be implemented as part of any food safety management system, including:

  • Monitoring and recording processes to support due diligence
  • Control of pests to prevent contamination
  • The use of swabbing as a technique to identify microbiological threats
  • Supervisors to ensure the workforce complies with the management plan
  • Food safety training for staff commensurate with their employment
  • A comprehensive cleaning schedule
  • The maintenance of premises and equipment

Food safety management procedures

Since January 2006, it is a legal requirement that all food businesses implement a food safety management system. The system used must be based on the principles of a Hazard Analysis Critical Control Points (HACCP) system.

HACCP is based on seven principles:

  • Conduct a hazard analysis
  • Decide on the critical control points (CCP)
  • Decide on the critical limits at each CCP
  • Monitor the CCPs
  • Corrective actions of CCPs if required
  • Verify the system is working
  • Document the system and review

HACCP

In order for an HACCP system to work, businesses must first ensure the following actions are taken:

  • Buildings and equipment are fit for use
  • Pest control polices are written
  • Staff are trained
  • Suppliers are vetted for their own food safety measures
  • Stock control measures are in place
  • An effective cleaning schedule has been developed

In all the above cases the procedures must be recorded by the food business. This will allow the business to prove, if necessary, compliance with legislation.

Safer Food Better Business

In order to comply with the law it is recognised that producing a management system based on HACCP principles can involve a lot of work for small food businesses.

The Food Standards Agency recognised this and developed a system called Safer Food Better Business. It is based on the HACCP principles for businesses in England and Wales.

Another system, called CookSafe, is employed in Scotland.

Both systems provide food businesses with support to meet HACCP principles in food safety.

Safer Food Better Business

Safer Food Better Business provides different types of food businesses with a support pack, pre-printed documentation and advice on complying with the HACCP principles.

Different support packs have been created by the Food Standards Agency (FSA) to ensure compliance with legislation, these include:

  • Caterers
  • Retailers
  • Childminders

The FSA have also developed packs specifically for:

  • Chinese cuisine
  • Indian cuisine

Introduction to legislation and responsibilities

Any business that intends to provide and sell food in the UK must comply with the relevant legislation. The principal legislation is:

  • Food Safety Act 1990
  • Food Safety (England, Scotland, Wales, Northern Ireland) Regulations 2006

This legislation provides the necessary framework of food safety in the UK. In each case it places responsibilities on:

  • Employer
  • Employees

Food safety essentials

VIDEO SCREEN

Food Safety Act 1990

The Food Safety Act 1990 makes it an offence to:

  • Sell food that may be injurious to health
  • Sell food that is not as advertised
  • Sell food that is not of the nature and quality expected

The Food Safety Act 1990 also provides the framework for other regulations, including:

  • Food Labelling Regulations 1996
  • Food Hygiene Regulations 2006

Food Safety (England, Scotland, Wales, Northern Ireland) Regulations 2006

The main provisions of the Food Safety Regulations 2006 is to provide a legal framework for enforcement of food safety legislation. This includes the ability to issue:

  • Hygiene improvement notices
  • Hygiene prohibition orders
  • Hygiene emergency improvement notices

This legislation also makes it a legal requirement to keep records and monitor food safety procedures.

Enforcement notices

Hygiene improvement notice

  • A hygiene improvement notice may be served on a food business if an Environmental Health Officer (EHO) does not believe the business is complying with the law
  • It will provide details of what the EHO believes is wrong and why they believe this
  • The notice will state a date by which it must be complied with
  • Failure to comply with the notice within the agreed timescale is an offence

Enforcement notices

Hygiene prohibition order

  • These orders are placed against a person or persons
  • Applied for in court when the owner/proprietor is a repeat offender
  • Prohibits those under the order to work in a food premises

Hygiene emergency prohibition notice

  • Served by an EHO and closes the business for three days
  • Immediate risk to health
  • The EHO must apply to a court for a Hygiene Emergency Prohibition Order to keep a business closed until a certificate of satisfaction is issued by the EHO

Records

Records that a food business must keep include:

  • Choice of supplier (audits) – allows a business to audit from ‘farm to fork’
  • HACCP documentation
  • Delivery and waste records
  • Temperature records
  • Maintenance and cleaning schedules
  • Training records
  • Pest control
  • Visitor records/policy

Keeping records will allow a business to prove that due diligence is practiced. Monitoring and reviews are conducted with the appropriate corrective actions.

Responsibilities

Food safety legislation places responsibilities on the employer and the employee.

In each case it is vital that these responsibilities are undertaken with the importance due to them.

It is these responsibilities that provide the backbone of food safety practices carried out by businesses to meet the legislative requirements of food law.

In both cases the employer and employee must ensure compliance with the necessary food safety legislation.

Employer responsibilities

The employer in any food business is responsible for ensuring the following actions are undertaken:

  • The business is registered – this includes vehicles, if it is a mobile business
  • All appropriate licences are in place
  • Implement a food safety management plan
  • Monitor and evaluate the plan. This can be achieved using:
    • internal/external audits
    • reviews by the management
    • advisory visits by regulatory bodies such as EHOs
    • staff consultations
    • supervisor’s handover book/notes

Employer responsibilities

Businesses must also ensure:

  • Policies and procedures are in place for training, recruitment and day-to-day staffing levels
  • Training is provided to employees, commensurate with their level of responsibility
  • Train at least one employee in HACCP
  • Records are kept and accident reporting procedures are in place
  • They comply with EHOs/EHPs

Businesses must also provide:

  • The necessary levels of supervision
  • Sufficient ventilation for staff

Employee responsibilities

Employees must:

  • Comply with the law
  • Follow good personal hygiene practices
  • Report any illness to their line managers
  • Follow instructions and comply with the business’ policies and procedures
  • Attend any instruction or training provided

Employees must also:

  • Report any errors/omissions in their employer’s policies, practices and procedures

Compliance

Compliance with relevant food safety legislation is achieved through a mixture of activities, including:

  • Correctly scoping and implementing the seven principles of HACCP
  • Record-keeping of temperature controls, delivery records, sickness records, supplier records etc
  • Written documentation, including the recruitment process, training, safe systems of work, reporting procedures etc
  • Only using reputable suppliers to source goods

In all cases where the food business and its employees comply with legislation, policies and procedures, this will allow them to provide evidence of due diligence in the criminal of civil courts.
