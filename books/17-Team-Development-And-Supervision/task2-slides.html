Introduction to leadership styles and behaviours

The way in which a supervisor develops their leadership style and behaviour will effect those that answer to them. There are many different types of leadership style, including:

  • Autocratic
  • Authoritarian
  • Democratic
  • Participative
  • Laissez-faire
  • Delegative

Each of these styles have different characteristics.

Leadership styles

  • Autocratic

This style of leadership is where all the power is kept by the supervisor and they make all the decisions. The authority to make these decisions remains with the supervisor. These types of leadership can sometimes be seen as overbearing or bullies.

  • Authoritarian

Leaders of the authoritarian style are similar to the autocratic – they tell the chefs what to do and do not expect any input from them. They do not like to be questioned and their focus is on achieving the business goal at all costs.

Both these styles affect the morale of the workplace and, with little input from chefs, they will have no ownership of their work output.

Leadership styles

  • Democratic

This leadership style lends itself to teamwork, allowing chefs to be involved in the decision-making and supporting the supervisor to achieve the business aims and objectives.

  • Participative

Participative leaders tend to involve chefs in decision-making and problem-solving in the workplace. However, they will have the final say, after listening to everyone’s views.

Both these styles need the involvement of the whole team to deliver the output. The supervisor is considered to be part of the team – both professionally and socially.

Leadership styles

  • Laissez-faire

Laissez-faire leaders allow chefs to get on with their work with minimum input. The leader is available if the staff require guidance or information. This style is considered to be the least effective for employees, as they feel they are not being directed.

  • Delegative

The supervisor provides staff with the authority to make decisions and deliver output but the responsibility remains with the supervisor.

These styles provide both positive and negative characteristics: the laissez-faire taking a hands off approach, while the delegative style gives authority to the team but the supervisor always remains responsible.

Leadership styles

Supervisors may need to develop different styles of leadership for different situations that may arise in the professional kitchen, such as:

  • Using an autocratic/authoritarian style when under pressure to deliver due to time or a crisis
  • Being a participative or democratic leader when delivering training, mentoring or coaching
  • Staff that are highly developed in skills and experience are better suited to receiving direction from a delegative/laissez-faire leader
  • Different personalities will answer differently to the styles of leadership that a supervisor adopts. Through experience and knowing the team members, supervisors can select the most appropriate style in a given situation

Leadership behaviours

Within the professional kitchen environment, chefs look to supervisors for guidance and motivation. Supervisors need to understand the types of behaviour they must demonstrate, including:

  • Influencing others to meet team goals – achieved by developing positive working relationships. These include relationships with staff, suppliers, management etc
  • Communicating effectively – any leader must be able to communicate effectively or their team will not know the direction the supervisor wishes them to take. Communication must also be effective in building a team and positive working relationships

Leadership behaviours

  • Building trust – trust within the team is gained by supervisors listening to staff, being approachable, providing confidential support and earning respect
  • Setting a good example – as with many things in life there is a tendency for humans to follow one another. Within the professional kitchen it is essential that a good example is set by the supervisor; if the supervisor has bad habits, it is likely the staff will follow these
  • Proactive/supportive leadership – leaders who are proactive (observe, plan and act before a situation gets out of hand) will be seen by the staff as forward-thinking. This type of behavior, along with being supportive to their own staff and management, will be seen as very professional
  • Within the professional kitchen, the output is delivering a first-class product to the customer. Supervisors must ensure they are customer-focused to meet needs and expectations

Introduction to effective teams

Effective teams are built and developed over long periods of time. It is crucial that supervisors understand the impact these teams can have on a business. Supervisors need to be able to identify the characteristics of an effective team, as well as the benefits these teams can have on the business.

Characteristics of an effective team

  • Clear and open communication

All team members need to know and understand the objectives and aims of their team. All members need to be able to communicate with each other, from top to bottom and vice versa.

  • Mix of appropriate skills

An effective team will have a good mix of appropriate skills to be able to meet the team’s aims and objectives. These skills include practical, supervisory and communication skills. The team should develop those skills within the whole team.

Characteristics of an effective team

  • Clear understanding of team/individual goals/responsibilities

Within an effective team everyone knows their own and each other’s responsibilities. This enables cross-pollination of their activities to support each other. Clearly understood responsibilities allow all team members to focus their work ethic to achieve their personal goals and support the team as a whole.

  • Mutual support

All effective teams build a culture of mutual support – everyone is focused on the team output and support each other to achieve this.

Characteristics of an effective team

  • Respect and trust

Over time, team members begin to respect and trust each other, through admiration of each other’s skillset or their contribution to the team effort. This develops a team spirit that includes mutual respect and trust of one another.

  • Consistently achieving targets

Effective teams consistently achieve their targets as a team. If one member is underperforming due to welfare issues, an effective team will rally around them to support the team effort.

  • Continuous improvement

All the above traits of an effective team help to develop and nurture a culture of continuous improvement. Beating previously set targets and striving for excellence in delivering the output of the professional kitchen for customers, in turn this ill enhance the reputation of the food business.

Benefits of an effective team

The benefits of having an effective team are:

  • Maximum effectiveness

If team members who work together strive to meet a target and look out for each other, you will achieve maximum effectiveness of individuals and the team.

  • Maximising productivity

Effective teams that communicate well, achieve targets, work together and focus on the team aims and objectives will maximise their performance and productivity.

Benefits of an effective team

  • Inspiring individuals and team

Building the culture of mutual respect and trust allows the free passage of information and skills to each team member. An effective supervisor ensures all team members are inspired to improve their own, and the team’s, overall delivery.

  • Satisfied customers

The major benefit of an effective team is customer satisfaction. Customers who are provided with an excellent product at the right place, right time, right quantities and the right price will have their expectations met and will therefore be satisfied. Due to the continuous improvements that effective teams look for, these expectations to be surpassed and customer satisfaction levels increased further.

Developing teams and individuals

A supervisor’s role encompasses many different strands, none more so than the development of their teams and the individuals in these teams. Training can contribute to businesses through:

  • Increased productivity
  • Reduced accident rates
  • Improved profit
  • Enhanced reputation
  • Staff morale
  • Staff retention

In each case, the provision of training and development will support the overall aim of the business, which is to provide safe food and to make a profit.

Types of development

There are many different approaches to develop teams and individuals – different situations will require different approaches. This can be achieved in many different ways:

  • Shadowing
  • Coaching/demonstrations
  • Mentoring
  • Continuous Professional Development (CPD)
  • Training courses
  • Qualifications

Types of development

Shadowing

Shadowing is all about an employee observing someone doing a job and then trying to understand how it is done. This is a good, cost-effective training tool in providing a standardised approach that a business undertakes.

Coaching/demonstrating

An employee who is tasked with coaching/ demonstrating tasks to another, probably new, employee has many roles to fill. This includes demonstrating new skills, assessing and providing feedback, facilitating the training and acting as a role model. A good use of this techniques is when carrying out induction training.

Types of development

Mentoring

Mentoring is all about the person who the activity is aims at: the mentee. It is built around a relationship between the two people involved and, in a practical sense, the mentor is there to guide the mentee without giving all the answers.

Qualifications

The provision of qualifications is an essential tool in the development of chefs in a professional kitchen. This can be seen UK-wide, with the focus on the delivery of apprenticeships as a mean to upgrade and upskill the national workforce. Ideally suited to the younger generations, in recent years it has been adapted to include the whole UK workforce.

Types of development

Continuous professional development

The practice of CPD is employed in most industries throughout the UK. Essentially, it is about upgrading knowledge and skills to be deployed in the workplace. It can be achieved through different ways as a professional chef:

  • Training course to gain or improve knowledge and skills
  • Attending seminars and conferences to keep up to date
  • Reading magazines and other publications
  • Networking with other professionals
  • Carrying out work experience in other establishments
  • Entering competitions

Why develop?

Why do we want to develop our teams and individuals? In simple terms it is about continuous improvement but there are side effects:

  • A workforce that is happy and content, with high morale and good retention figures. Less turnover means less costs associated with finding and training new staff
  • Productivity is increased and costs are reduced due to less wastage
  • A highly motivated and trained kitchen will meet, or even beat, their customers’ expectations, increasing the customer base
  • Increased customers base will increase turnover, which may then increase profits
  • Increased profits may provide more money to keep training, looking for the continuous improvement
