Knives and cutting equipment

  • Knives and cutting tools are the tools of the trade for any chef working in any section of the kitchen. The correct tools allow a chef to show his or her skills by means of the different ways they handle a knife.
  • Care should be taken when handling knives and cutting tools, as they pose a risk to safety. When handled correctly they can be used to undertake many different tasks, from peeling to shredding to slicing and trimming.

Types of knives and cutting equipment

  • Chef’s knife: This knife can be the chef’s best friend. It is an all-purpose kitchen knife used for most types of chopping, dicing and slicing. Chef's knives come in various lengths of 6, 8, 10 and 12 inches. The size, weight and balance of this knife allow it to be used for heavy-duty work with thicker cuts of vegetables, fruit and meats.
  • Paring knife: This small knife has a straight, sharp blade that is generally 3 to 5 inches long. Its thin, narrow blade tapers to a point at the tip. It is easy to handle and works well for peeling and coring foods, or mincing and cutting small items.

Types of knives and cutting equipment

  • Filleting knife: A knife consisting of a thin flexible blade, typically 6 to 11 inches long, used for filleting fish. The narrow blade enables the knife to cleanly move along the backbones of the fish, in and around areas adjacent to bones, and to evenly slice along skin, removing it easily from flesh.
  • Scissors: Kitchen scissors are often made with plastic- or rubber-coated textured handles, allowing for ease of cutting and gripping. The blades may have a notch for cutting through poultry joints. Parts of the handles may have teeth to be used for opening bottles or jars.

Types of knives and cutting equipment

  • Boning knife: A knife with a thin blade, typically 5 or 6 inches long, used to remove the main bone within a cut of meat such as a ham or a beef roast. A boning knife will typically have a long narrow blade for ease of manipulation around bones. The blade is rigid and proportionate to the size of the bones being removed.
  • Carving knife: A knife with a long slender blade, used for cutting thin slices of meat. The knife has a long blade so that it can easily cut across a large roast. The knife is available in different lengths: 10, 12 and 14 inch blades. They are designed so that the meat will cut smoothly and not be torn as it is being sliced.

Types of knives and cutting equipment

  • Turning knife: A shorter blade than a paring knife, with a blade that is typically 2 to 3 inches long. It is designed to curve upwards on both the cutting edge and the top edge. The turning knife is commonly used to turn vegetables.
  • Palette knife: A knife with a blunt-edged blade used to apply creams and spreads.

Types of knives and cutting equipment

  • Serrated knife: A knife with a sharp edge that has saw-like notches or teeth. The blade of a serrated knife is 5 to 10 inches long. Serrated knives are difficult to sharpen. A serrated knife with a long blade is useful for slicing through food that is hard on the outside and soft on the inside, such as crusty bread.
  • Sharpening tool: A tool generally made of steel, used to realign a knife's edge. Often the knife's edge will bend or roll slightly from normal use. Running it across the steel will straighten the edge, and the knife will be sharp again. A sharpening tool can also be glass, ceramic or diamond-impregnated metal.

Types of knives and cutting equipment

  • Peeler: A tool used to remove layers of skin/flesh from vegetables and firm fruits. Standard designs for peelers include a rigid blade that is formed into one position, so the hand is required to guide the peeling by following the shape of the item being peeled. Peelers are also available with a swivel blade to enable a more effective method of peeling.
  • Corer: A round-bladed speciality tool for removing the cores and seeds of apples, pears and cucumbers. Corer blades may be v-shaped, formed into a half circle, or full-circled with scooped sides. The small-toothed or sharpened blades allow it to be easily inserted 4 to 6 inches deep into the end of an apple and rotated.

Characteristics of knives and cutting equipment

Maintaining and storing knives and cutting equipment

  • Washing knives by hand will extend their lifetime. Knives that are washed in the dishwasher are exposed to harsh detergents, which will dull the cutting edge, and to intense and varied temperatures that can affect the temper of the steel. Damage is also likely to occur to the sharp edge of a knife if it is knocked around by other items in a dishwasher.
  • Knives should be wiped clean immediately after use to prevent discoloration of the blade.
  • When washing items in a sink of warm soapy water, do not place a knife in with other items. Doing so risks injury to the person washing the knife, and it also risks damage to the blade from the other items. Do not allow the knife to soak in the water; soaking will damage the blade and handle. Wash and dry it immediately.

Maintaining and storing knives and cutting equipment

  • Most knives will need sharpening after three to five months of normal usage. In between sharpening times, the knife may need to be steeled to maintain its sharp edge. There are several methods and techniques that can be used for sharpening knives.
  • Different sharpening tools found in a professional kitchen:
    • V-Shaped knife sharpener
    • Electric knife sharpener
    • Sharpening steel
    • Sharpening stone

Maintaining and storing knives and cutting equipment

  • Store knives in a knife block or on a magnetic strip. If one is not available, store knives in a knife cases or knife rolls or, at a minimum, store the knives away from other utensils to prevent damage to the sharp edges.
  • Knives and small tools should be secured at all times to prevent loss, theft or damage. They are expensive items and tools of the trade for any chef.
  • Knives and tools should be stored in bags, and not in view, when travelling between home, work and college.

Knife skills and cutting techniques

As a chef in a professional kitchen, it is important that you can recognise and use a variety of different cuts. Many of these cuts are derived from traditional French cookery, which is why many of the terms are in French.

Mastering the various cutting techniques takes practice and skill, but once mastered they will enable you to use your skills confidently in the kitchen.

It is important to cut properly. The main reason is uniformity. Uniformity in cutting helps to ensure even, predictable cooking times.

Knife skills and cutting techniques: definitions

  • Boning: A knife is used to remove the main bone within a cut of meat, such as a ham or leg of lamb. Boning allows for quicker cooking times, and the area from which the bone has been removed can be stuffed.
  • Carving: Carving is usually associated with roasted meats and bread products. A serrated or thin-bladed knife is used to slice through the body of the food. The size of the cut will determine what the product is used for.
  • Chopping (finely, coarsely): This technique is used in a variety of ways to reduce the size of item, ie finely chopping herbs to incorporate into stuffings or curries. The blade of the knife is usually handled in a downwards rocking motion to enable the blade to cut through the food.
  • Coring: A round-bladed speciality tool is used to remove the cores and seeds of apples, pears and cucumbers. The area from which the core has been removed can be stuffed. For instance, an apple can be stuffed with dried fruit.

Knife skills and cutting techniques: definitions

  • Crushing: Crushing is most commonly used when crushing garlic. Place the heel of the knife flat over the food with the blade facing away from you and use the heel of your free hand to give the side of the blade a good hit.
  • Cutting into batons (julienne, jardinière):
    • Julienne is a cutting technique where food is cut into long, thin matchstick-like strips approximately 3mm thick.
    • Jardinière refers to medium batons, 5mm x 5mm x 20mm thick.
  • Dicing (brunoise/macedoine):
    • Brunoise is a fine dice cutting technique producing small cubes, no bigger than 3mm on each side.
    • Macedoine is a large dice cutting technique producing medium to large cubes, 8mm x 8mm. Typically this cut would be used for vegetables in a soup or a stock base.

Knife skills and cutting techniques: definitions

  • Filleting: Filleting is usually used when removing fillets from a fish, but can be used when removing the fillet cut from meat. A knife is used to move along the backbones of the fish or meat, in and around areas adjacent to bones, and to evenly slice along the skin, removing it easily from the flesh.
  • Jointing: Often used when preparing meat into different cuts (for instance, on a chicken the legs and breast are different joints/cuts). The menu will determine the joint used and its cooking method and time.
  • Marking and scoring: Often used on meat and fish to make cuts into the flesh so that flavours can be absorbed, which in turn can help to tenderise the meat by allowing the juice to penetrate the proteins in the flesh. Marking and scoring is also used to make decorative patterns on the flesh which are appealing to the eye.

Knife skills and cutting techniques: definitions

  • Peeling: Used to remove the outer skin from fruit and vegetables such as carrots, apples or potatoes. When certain fresh fruits and vegetables are peeled, an enzyme called polyphenol oxidase contained in the cells is exposed to and reacts with the oxygen in the air, which can turn them brown.
  • Segmenting: Segmenting is commonly used on citrus fruits, which contain different segments separated by a semi-transparent membrane. By segmenting you are removing all of the bitter white pith, and getting individual segments.
  • Shredding (chiffonade): Chiffonade cutting technique is used on herbs or leafy vegetables. They are cut into long, thin strips and can vary in thickness from 1mm up to 10mm.

Knife skills and cutting techniques: definitions

  • Skinning: Skinning is removing the skin from fish, poultry or game for reasons of nutrition, taste and appearance. The skin is removed before or after cooking, depending on the reason for its removal and how it is going to be cooked.
  • Slicing: Slicing is the cutting of food into thin, relatively even slices.
  • Trimming: Trimming is used to remove inedible or undesirable parts from food.
  • Turning/shaping: Often referred to as tourné, barrel-shaped or château. An oblong-shaped cut for vegetables such as carrots or potatoes that provides a distinctive and consistent appearance. When turning and shaping, a turning or bird’s-beak knife is used.

Factors to consider

Factors to consider when using knife skills and cutting techniques:

  • Selection of appropriate knives and skills: Ask yourself, is the knife or tool suitable for the job? For instance, you would not use a 10ʺ cook’s knife to peel vegetables.
  • Minimising waste: Use your knife skills to reduce the waste of foodstuffs, ie not cutting too much of the flesh off vegetables when preparing them.
  • Following safe practices: Ask yourself, are you handling the knife correctly? Overall, the best way to handle a knife is the way that feels safest to you.
  • Following hygienic practices: Always try and work in a clean, tidy and methodical manner, using the ‘clean as you go’ approach.

Parts and characteristics of a knife

Parts and characteristics of a knife

Tip

The tip of the knife is at the opposite end to the handle, and is sharp and fairly thin. It is typically pointed, but there are some knives with ends that are cut off straight, rounded or at a slant. The tip is used for cutting small items, cutting food into thin strips and carving. It is also used for making incisions.

Cutting edge

The cutting edge is the bottom edge of the blade that runs from the heel to the tip of the blade. It is very sharp, and can be straight cut or serrated. The cutting edge is used to slice, cut or chop food items both large and small, with the middle of the blade being used most often. The blade edges are available with different grinds, which have different purposes.

Heel

The heel is approximately the last two inches of the blade's cutting edge at the opposite end from the tip. It is used for cutting thick or coarse items that require extra pressure or strength. It assists in making faster, more efficient cuts when the task calls for it.

Rivets

Metal rivets are used to secure the tang to the handle of the knife. To prevent irritation to the hand, the rivets should be completely smooth and lie flush with the surface of the handle. In addition, this will help prevent debris and microorganisms from collecting in the spaces between the handle and the rivets.

Butt

The butt end of the knife is at the opposite end of the tip. If the knife has a full tang, it is where the tang ends.

Handle

The handle is the part of the knife that holds the blade. The tang of the blade extends down in the handle to attach the blade to the handle. The tang is riveted into the handle, or is sometimes enclosed in a plastic or metal handle. It is important to get a good feel of the handle before purchasing a knife to be sure it fits your hand properly. If it is too big or small, it can result in inefficient use.

Spine

The spine is the edge opposite the cutting edge on the blade. It is thicker than the cutting edge and adds strength to the blade. It has a smooth, blunt edge to allow the user to grip it with thumb and forefinger, or to be able to apply pressure with fingers or the palm of a hand to add control to the task being performed.

Bolster

A bolster is a thick piece of metal (collar or shank) at the end of the blade, just before the handle. It generally runs the full length from the spine of the blade down to the cutting edge. The bolster, along with the tang, gives the knife balance, which provides for better control of the knife when cutting. It also provides a place for fingers to be placed for comfort, and provides protection from the blade. The bolster is an indication that the blade was forged rather than stamped.
