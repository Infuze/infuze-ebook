Introduction to tools and small equipment

Small tools and equipment play a variety of different roles within the professional kitchen. The equipment being used will depend on the size and nature of the establishment.

It is important to choose tools and small equipment that are of good quality and fit your needs. To do this, you will need a basic knowledge of how they are used, and of the various models available. The information below should be helpful in selecting and maintaining your tools and equipment.

It is important that when using tools and small equipment, you are fully trained in how to handle them safely.

Types of tools and small equipment

  • Bat: Meat can be tenderised with the use of meat mallets or bats. These are typically made of steel or sometimes wood. The tenderising tools resemble a hammer-like handled utensil and are made with a flat or point-protruding surface that can be pounded into the meat to break apart the meat fibres. This process helps to reduce toughness in a cut of meat.
  • Blender: A blender is used to emulsify, liquify or puree. Blenders typically consist of a tall glass or plastic food container with a removable lid made with a filler hole for adding ingredients during the process. At the base of the food container there are motor-driven blades that blend, chop, mix or liquefy foods, depending on the speed setting selected.

Types of tools and small equipment

  • Blow torch: Produced as a finishing tool, this tool is most often used to add the golden tan surface to crème brulée desserts by caramelising sugar sprinkled over the top. Small in size, the blow torch uses butane gas for the flame emitted from the torch. Blow torches are typically built with safety locks and holding stands so they don't need to be laid down on counters.

Types of tools and small equipment

  • Can opener: A can opener is used to open or remove the top from a metal can. There are a wide variety of different designs available, ranging from manually operated small tools to automated openers that quickly and easily remove the top of the can.
  • Carving fork: A common utensil for use when slicing meats, poultry, fish and other food items. Carving forks are used to hold foods securely in place as well as keeping hands away from the sharp blade of the knife as food is being prepared.

Types of tools and small equipment

  • Chopping board: Chopping boards come in a wide variety of thicknesses, colours, shapes and sizes to fit a variety of different preparation and storage needs. In modern-day professional kitchens it is good practice to have colour-coded chopping boards to reduce the risk of cross-contamination.
  • Cooling rack: A cooling rack is used for placing cooked foods onto a surface that will enable the food to be cooled on all sides after being cooked. Most cooling racks are made of stainless steel wires formed into mesh squares so that food to be cooled can easily be supported without falling through, yet allowing enough air to reach the bottom of the food.

Types of tools and small equipment

  • Food processor: A food processor is used to chop, grate, mince, slice, puree and blend a variety of food ingredients. Food processors have different blades to perform the various functions. A food processor can usually perform any of these tasks in minimal time, which makes it an indispensable tool for a chef in a busy kitchen.
  • Frying pan: A flat-bottomed pan with shallow sides that are slightly curved. They are made of various materials such as copper, cast iron and aluminium, and some have a non-stick coating applied. There are several sizes available and they are generally round in shape. Also known as a ‘skillet’.

Types of tools and small equipment

  • Grater: An all-purpose grater or box grater generally has several grating sides that form a triangle or square, with each side containing a different grating pattern. With different panels, the specific grating procedure can be easily selected as the food is moved across the flat grating surface.
  • Mandolin: A mandolin is used for cutting and slicing fruits and vegetables. The mandolin has a variety of adjustable blades that enable it to make precise cuts of differing thickness. When using a mandolin the foods being sliced are held in place with the specially designed mandolin holder or grip.

Types of tools and small equipment

  • Mixers: The mixer is an important utensil used for mixing ingredients or beating foods. The types of electrical mixers vary in size and features beginning with hand-held models and moving up to heavy-duty models mounted on stands. The mixer provides several speeds and mixing, kneading or whisking attachments. Additional functions of the mixer can include a food grinder, a juice extractor and a slicer or shredder for attachments.
  • Measuring jug: Measuring cups are available in a variety of styles and materials such as tempered glass, plastic, aluminium or stainless steel.

Types of tools and small equipment

  • Moulds and cases: Bakeware can be made from several different materials, in a variety of shapes and sizes. Each can have a different effect on the outcome of your baked goods. Silicone bakeware is a fairly new product used for baking moulds. It is made of a flexible and bendable silicone material that can be used in the oven, microwave and freezer.
  • Pastry brush: A 1" to 1½"-wide small brush made with nylon or sterilised natural bristles. The brush is used to apply glazing to breads, pastries or cookies before or after baking. It is also commonly used to brush on barbecue sauces, olive oil, garlic oil and egg washes.

Types of tools and small equipment

  • Piping bag and nozzles: A cone-shaped bag that has a small opening on one end and a large opening on the opposite end. The small opening is the decorating end and generally has different tips that can be interchanged to create a variety of decorative shapes. Some common tips are the round tip for line work and writing, an open star tip for borders and a closed star for flower shapes.
  • Ricer: A ricer is generally used to reduce whole soft foods, such as cooked potatoes, into smaller bits. It works much like a garlic press but is larger in size, with an open chamber 3 to 4 inches wide. A ram or plunger connected to the handle of the ricer is pressed down onto the food, forcing it through the small holes in the bottom.

Types of tools and small equipment

  • Rolling pin: A rolling pin is a long cylindrical kitchen tool, generally used to roll out various types of doughs. Other types include baker's pin, French pin, mini-pin, tapered pin, single-cut or double-cut pin and hardtack pin. There are also a variety of specialised pins used for making specific foods, such as pasta.
  • Saucepan: A round cooking vessel with high, straight sides and a handle. Equipped with a tight-fitting cover, the saucepan can range in size and is made of materials such as stainless steel, copper, anodized aluminium, glass, enamelled steel or cast iron.

Types of tools and small equipment

  • Scales: Scales are used to measure the weight of ingredients and other food commodities. Kitchen scales are available in electronic balance or spring models. A electronic balance scale will give a more accurate reading than a spring scale.
  • Sieve: A sieve is used to strain liquids or to sift dry ingredients, such as flour or powdered sugar. Also referred to as a strainer, a sieve has perforated holes or mesh and is available in many different shapes and sizes. Similar kitchen tools that may be used as a sieve are a chinois or a colander.

Types of tools and small equipment

  • Temperature probe: A probe is used for checking the internal temperature of meat or other food products to ensure that an appropriate and safe temperature has been achieved. When checking the temperature of the food, insert the thermometer probe into the thickest part of the meat and as close to the centre as possible.
  • Trays: Trays are commonly found in every professional kitchen and are used for a variety of cooking tasks. Cake pans can be round, square or rectangular and are available in several sizes. Aluminium is a good material for trays because it is a good conductor of heat, which results in even cooking.

Types of tools and small equipment

  • Trussing needle: This is a large, thick stainless steel needle used to tie loose parts of meat or poultry together with string. The needle is threaded with kitchen string and is inserted and woven through the bird parts in order to form a more compact shape around the body.
  • Whisk: There are a variety of different types, shapes and sizes of whisks available depending on the requirements for their use. Some of the most common whisks include: ball, round balloon, oval balloon, coil, flat end.

Characteristics of different tools and small equipment

Each tool or small piece of equipment will be made for its own specialist task. It is important that when selecting the items you consider the following factors:

  • Colour (colour-coded chopping boards, knives)
  • Design
  • Length
  • Material (metal, aluminium, plastic, silicone)
  • Shape
  • Size

Maintaining and storing tools and small equipment

  • Cleaning: It is important that all tools and small equipment are cleaned to the correct standard after use and before storage.
  • Safe and hygienic storage: All tools should be safety stored in the correct area within the kitchen. Any faults or damages to the tools or small equipment should be reported to the supervisor immediately. Never use equipment that is damaged, as this could lead to injury.
  • Security procedures: Small tools and equipment should be secured at all time to prevent loss, theft or damage. They are expensive items, and are part of the tools of the trade for any chef.

Introduction

As a chef in a professional kitchen, you will use a number of tools and items of small equipment to carry out your job role. It is important that you understand how to use them properly and some of the definitions of the skills used.

The following set of slides will go through some of these techniques.

Using tools and small equipment

  • Basting is the process of moistening food during the cooking process, especially meat, with pan drippings, butter, or sauces. This can be done by spooning the liquid over the food periodically as it cooks, or by brushing it with a basting brush.
  • Beating is the act of mixing or stirring ingredients rapidly until they are blended. For example, egg whites can be beaten with a whisk until they are light and airy and the volume has increased.
  • Blanching is the process of briefly cooking food in boiling water or steam and then immersing the food in ice-cold water or frozen storage to stop the cooking process. Blanching brings out the colour in vegetables and helps to maintain their nutritional value, which can be lost with overcooking. Blanching is also useful for loosening the skins of fruits and vegetables so that the skins can be removed easily.

Using tools and small equipment

  • Blending is the process of combining two or more ingredients together so that they lose their individual characteristics and become smooth and uniform. For example, cake batter is the result of dry and liquid ingredients that are blended together in a uniform mixture, and this can be done with electric blenders or with spoons.
  • Brushing is the process of applying usually a liquid to food, ie egg washing a savoury pie top. It may also apply to brushing off the excess ingredients from a product.

Using tools and small equipment

  • Coating is a process in which foods are pulled/put through dry ingredients, such as flour, in order to coat them before cooking. Flour can be used alone as a coating, but it is more often combined with other dry ingredients such as herbs, spices, salt or breadcrumbs to create a seasoned coating on the food that becomes brown and crispy after it is cooked. Fish fillets, boneless poultry, pork cutlets and veal cutlets are some foods that are often dredged in flour and other dry ingredients before cooking.
  • Cooling/chilling/refreshing are terms referring to the cooling of food, either to keep it safe or to stop the cooking process of a food item such as vegetables. Refreshing is similar to blanching, where food is briefly cooked and then immersed in ice-cold water or chilled or frozen storage to stop the cooking process.

Using tools and small equipment

  • Cutting/shaping is the process of taking a ingredient and shaping or cutting in to a desired shape, such as potatoes, carrots or courgettes into square, barrel or round shapes. You can also cut out shapes in pastry items using a pastry cutter.
  • Draining is the process of pouring off the excess liquid or fat from food, such as draining the water from boiled pasta or potatoes, or pouring off the fat after roasting a joint of meat. This can be done using a colander.

Using tools and small equipment

  • Filling is the process of filling a cavity in a food item with a suitable filling. This can be done pre- or post-cooking and the fillings can be sweet or savoury. Filling would be done using a piping bag and nozzles.
  • Folding is the process of blending a light ingredient, such as beaten egg whites, into a heavier ingredient by lifting from underneath with a spatula or spoon. In order to fold ingredients properly, the heavier ingredient is placed at the bottom of a bowl and the lighter above.
  • Glazing refers to a creating a flavourful coating that forms on food as it is cooked. The glaze may be the result of basting food with a liquid, such as a meat stock, during the cooking process. A glaze may also refer to something that gives food a more substantial coating, such as a covering of chocolate; or glazes can be created to make food items more aesthetically pleasing, such as adding an egg wash to some baked goods to produce a shiny golden brown glaze.

Using tools and small equipment

  • Grating is the process of transforming solid, firm food items into small pieces by rubbing them against a grating instrument. A grater is a hand-held metal device that contains numerous raised slots of varying sizes that cut food items into small pieces as it is being rubbed across them. A food processor can also be used to grate foods, and it may be preferred for food items that are difficult to grate on a manual grater.
  • Lining is used between metal surfaces and prepared foods so that the foods do not stick to baking sheets, pie tins, muffin tins, baking dishes and other surfaces after baking.
  • Mashing is the process of breaking down a large piece of food into smaller and smoother pieces; for example, cooked whole skinned potatoes can be mashed using a fork, a potato masher or in a food processor.

Using tools and small equipment

  • Measuring ingredients properly is important to successful cooking and baking. Some foods can be greatly affected by too much or too little of certain ingredients. There are various types of measuring utensils: measuring cups, spoons, jugs, scales.
  • Mixing is similar to combining: the mixing of ingredients together to form another product or dish. For example, mixing of dry and wet ingredients such as flour, sugar, butter, eggs and milk to produce a sweet paste.

Using tools and small equipment

  • Moulding and demoulding is the process of placing a raw or cooked food item into a shaped mould so that the food takes on that shape, and then turning it out. For instance, you could steam a golden syrup pudding in a pudding-basin-shaped mould; once cooked, you would then gently turn it out of the mould for presentation and the sponge pudding would have taken on the shape of the mould.

Using tools and small equipment

  • Passing is the preparation procedure of passing a ingredient or dish such as a sauce through a mesh-bottomed sieve. This process combines air and removes lumps within the ingredient being passed, making it lighter and more uniform in texture.
  • Piping is squeezing a pastry bag in order to force icing or other paste-like mixtures through the tip of the bag for the purpose of decorating or creating special shapes.

Using tools and small equipment

  • Portioning is the dividing up of a larger quantity into specific serving sizes or portions. It is essential that food is correctly portioned so that the correct amount of customers are served from the dish/products; this will also ensure that budgetary requirements are met and that you don’t over- or under-produce a dish. Portioning will also ensure that the correct size of the product is achieved.
  • Pureeing is to blend, grind or mash food until it is a thick, smooth, lump-free consistency. Pureeing can be done in a blender, food processor or food mill, or can be accomplished by pressing the food through a sieve. This process is used on many types of food and the pureed food is used in many ways, such as in soups, as a sauce, in desserts and in making beverages.
  • Reducing is to thicken and intensify the flavour of a liquid by rapidly boiling the contents in an uncovered pan in order to evaporate excess liquid. This process is used generally on soups and sauces. It is similar to deglazing, but is not the same. When reducing, the liquid contents are boiled in order to evaporate and reduce the volume of liquid so that the remaining contents are thicker and more flavourful. If a recipe suggests reducing a sauce by a half, simmer the liquid until half of the original volume is removed by boiling off the lighter liquid contents.

Using tools and small equipment

  • Rolling is the process of making a larger food item thinner though applied pressure on the food surface. A rolling pin is commonly used in a kitchen for this task. For example, a sweet paste after it has been made and rested could be rolled out to the required thickness to line a pastry mould for a pie or tartlet.
  • Saucing is a term for creating mixtures to be applied over various foods either as they finish cooking, or for use after they are served. For example, a finishing sauce may be mixed into spaghetti as a pasta sauce or served as a topping over sliced pieces of roasted meat such as roast beef. Other types of finishing sauce may include red wine sauce, pepper sauce, béarnaise sauce or mushroom sauce.

Using tools and small equipment

  • Searing is the process of quickly browning the surface of food by exposing it to extremely high heat. Food is most often seared in a small quantity of oil and is then cooked to the appropriate doneness using another cooking method such as roasting, grilling or braising. Searing can be done in a frying pan, BBQ, oven or on a grill. Meat is often seared to provide a flavourful brown crust on the surface.
  • Serving is the process of serving food to the customer using a variety of serving trays, plates and bowls, and can include serving spoons, tongs, forks and slices.
  • Skimming is the process of removing scum, fat or other impurities from the surface of a liquid, such as stock or jam, while it is cooking. A slotted spoon is generally used to remove solid impurities, while an unslotted spoon would be used to remove liquids such as fat.

Using tools and small equipment

  • Slicing is to cut into thin pieces that are consistent in thickness. This can be achieved using a knife or a slicing mandolin.
  • Straining is to remove undesirable particles from a liquid, to separate liquid from other solids or to separate various contents from other contents, such as removing smaller particles from larger particles. When a food item is strained the contents are poured through a sieve, a perforated utensil or a fine-meshed cheesecloth.
  • Trussing and tying is to tie meat or poultry with a string, which may be woven through the bird parts by using a needle in order to create a more compact shape for the food being prepared. Typically, the wings and legs of poultry are tied or trussed securely against the body to enable the bird to be evenly cooked. Trussing utensils may also include skewers or pins that are used instead of string to hold the bird parts together.

Using tools and small equipment

  • Turning is the process of turning a food item during the cooking process. It can be done using a slice or spoon.
  • Weighing ingredients is essential to achieve the correct balance of flavours and texture when preparing food items – for example, weighing out the ingredients for a bread dough.
  • Whisking is the process of beating an ingredient vigorously to incorporate air, which makes the ingredient frothy. For example, egg whites are whipped until they form stiff peaks and double cream is whipped into whipped cream.

Factors to consider

Factors to consider when using the above techniques:

  • Selection of appropriate tools and small equipment
  • Minimising waste
  • Following safe practices
  • Following hygienic practices
