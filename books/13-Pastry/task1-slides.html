Introduction to types of paste

Offering a wide range of pastry products makes sense in many ways.

Customers can choose from a wide selection of different products which often have various shelf lives – this assists with daily preparation and production as it can be staggered and used for enhanced productivity.

Types of paste

There are numerous paste products available allowing chefs to deliver a wide variety of products for their customer, such as:

  • short
  • sweet
  • choux
  • puff
  • suet
  • hot water
  • filo.

Short paste

Short paste is crisp and flaky in texture, crumbly but not hard. Some examples of the products that can be made with this include:

  • shortbread biscuits
  • petits fours
  • quiches
  • pies.

Short paste can be produced using different methods such as:

  • rub-in method
  • creaming method.

Sweet paste

Sweet paste is similar to a short paste but with the addition of eggs and sugar. Some of the items it can be used to make include:

  • flans
  • fruit tarts
  • tartlets.

Sweet paste can be produced using different methods such as:

  • rub-in method
  • creaming method.

Choux paste

Choux paste is the only paste that does not require resting. It is a mixture of boiling water or milk, fat and flour which forms a panada. Eggs are then added to it to finish the paste.

Choux paste can be used to produce:

  • profiteroles
  • gateau St Honoré
  • éclairs.

Choux paste can be filled with:

  • pastry cream
  • Chantilly cream
  • fresh cream.

Puff paste

Puff paste is known as pâte feuilletée in French which can be translated as flaky pastry, referring to its many layers. Puff paste is used to produce many products, such as:

  • Tarte tatin
  • Mille feuilles
  • Vol-au-vents
  • Sausage rolls.

Puff paste can be made using several methods including:

  • French method
  • English method
  • (For both the English and French method, the dough is made first and the butter/fat is added at the next stage.)
  • Scotch method or ‘rough puff’ – made by incorporating the fat when making the dough and then rolled.

Suet paste

Suet paste is made using a mix of flour, baking powder, salt and a suet, either beef or vegetarian. The products it is used for include:

  • fruit puddings
  • steamed jam rolls
  • steamed meat puddings
  • dumplings.

Hot water paste

This is the traditional paste used to make pork or game pies. The water and fat are boiled with the salt and flour then added to form the paste.

This paste should be used whilst hot.

It is firmer in texture than a traditional short paste.

Filo paste

Filo paste is produced using a combination of flour, salt, water and olive oil. It is mixed, rested and rolled out to a thickness of 0.5mm. A generous amount of butter or oil is required to keep the finished sheets separate. Pastries made from filo paste include:

  • baklava – Turkish pastries
  • bougatsa – Greek breakfast pastry
  • zelnik – savoury pie
  • pastizz – Maltese savoury
  • bundevara – Serbian pastries.

Introduction

There are a vast number of commodities used in the production of paste products but the principle ingredients are:

  • flour
  • sugar
  • fats
  • eggs.

Flour

Flour is produced in many forms, such as:

  • wholemeal
  • wheatmeal
  • self-raising
  • plain.

Below are some examples of the different properties of different flours.

Weak (soft) flour ~9% protein Used for sponges or short bread biscuits in which eggs provide additional structure
Medium flour ~11% protein Used for yeast goods, such as Danish pastries
Strong (hard) flour >12% protein Used for bread and puff pastry
High protein ~15% protein Used for pasta and high protein bread – durum wheat added to flour for this purpose

Fat

Pastes are made with many types of fats including:

  • butter – this provides a richness and strong flavour to pastries
  • olive oil – used in making filo paste
  • shortening – another name used is lard and this product is 100% fat.

Sugar

There are many types of sugars used in making and finishing paste products. These include:

  • granulated sugar
  • caster sugar
  • icing sugar.

Other forms of sugar used include:

  • glucose
  • honey.

Eggs

Eggs are used in paste products for a variety of reasons. These include:

  • binding properties
  • flavour
  • coating properties
  • emulsifying properties.
