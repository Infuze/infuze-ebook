Dry heat cookery methods

Roasting

Used extensively for meat. Whole primal cuts are cooked either bone-in or boned out and often stuffed and tied. Place on a trivet or bones and cook to the desired degree of doneness. 45–60 minutes per kg. Beef can be trussed and can be barded.

Pot roasting can be used similarly as an alternative. Place the primal piece of meat in a roasting dish or braisière, smear with butter, cover with a lid and cook in a medium oven. Take the lid off for the last 30 minutes to brown the food.

Dry heat cookery methods

Baking

Popular for primal or portion cuts of meat, offal and poultry. The item can be sealed, enveloped in pastry, for example puff, filo or brioche and then baked. Pies are a popular item that use this method.

Grilling

All first-class portion cuts are suitable and should be placed on the grill and turned once the meat surface starts to show small beads of blood. Then continue cooking until the beads appear again for a medium rare product.

Offal items such as kebabs, sliced liver, kidney are perfect for grilling.

Dry heat cookery methods

Shallow frying

All first-class portion cuts of meat and offal are suitable for shallow frying. The meat or offal may be drawn through flour first to seal the item quicker and provide a crustier exterior. Crumbed items are very popular. Typical items are Viennese schnitzel, crumbed lamb cutlets and sautéed veal liver strips in balsamic vinegar.

Deep frying

Deep frying can be used for marinated second-class cuts, for example sweet and sour pork, using either crumbs or batter coating. Farces can also be formed and coated, for example pork croquettes.

Moist heat cookery methods

Boiling

  • Second-class cuts are suitable and should be submerged in the desired liquid, such as a court bouillon. Typical examples are boiled silverside.
  • Stocks are made from bones and trimmings.
  • Offal items such as tongue and tripe are perfect for boiling to cook them until tender.

Moist heat cookery methods

Poaching

All first-class portion cuts of meat and offal are suitable for shallow poaching. The meat or offal may be placed on marrow bones and thus be raised above the poaching liquid. Alternatively it may be placed on a bed of finely diced French onions or brunoise of vegetables. Typical items are beef fillet in hoisin stock, poached lamb brains or chicken liver pâté.

Deep poaching can be used for second-class cuts or whole poultry, for example poached pork shoulder or poached boned ham hock.

Moist heat cookery methods

Stewing

Second-class cuts are suitable due to their higher collagen content. For stewing the item is usually diced or cut small. It is sealed or added after the onions have been sweated, then covered with liquid and then cooked with barely perceptible movement. Typical examples are Irish stew, navarin of lamb, goulash, veal fricassée or blanquette, and kidney and beef stew.

Moist heat cookery methods

Braising

All second-class primal cuts of meat are suitable for braising. The meat is sealed and placed in a braisière on mirepoix. Stock is added to come just halfway up the meat. Add a lid and cook until done. Typical items are lamb shanks, braised lamb topside, beef olives or braised silverside.

Offal is often braised especially older animal offal such as ox liver or tougher items such as the heart, oxtail or tongue.

Degree of cooking – lamb and beef

When cooking steak – either lamb or beef – customers may choose the degree to which they wish the steak to be cooked:

  • Blue rare
  • Rare
  • Medium rare
  • Medium
  • Medium well
  • Well done.

Planning and preparing menus

  • Consider the type of menu items that your clientele is likely to order, for example steak cooked to order with suitable accompaniments and garnishes.
  • Quality of the key ingredients will determine the final product
  • A lot of meat and offal now comes prepared ready for service. However, it is important to understand how to correctly prepare it.
  • Consider the portion size required by the establishment, the cuts needed, for example 10oz fillet steak and the amounts that you are likely to sell to maintain the quality and freshness.

Planning and preparing menus

  • Prepare stuffing and coatings following the correct processes. For example, draw through flour, egg and crumbs.
  • The flavourings used in the dish also have an impact and must be a part of the mise en place, for example seasoning, soy sauce, spice pastes, marinades or special oils to add during service.

Suitable sauces

The sauce or accompaniments used in the dish, are as key to final dish as the meat or offal. Suitable sauces or accompaniment:

Beef:

  • Horseradish sauce
  • Peppercorn sauce
  • Blue cheese sauce
  • Yorkshire pudding
  • Port jus lie
  • Mustard.

Lamb:

  • Minted jus
  • Mint sauce
  • Pea purée
  • Port jus lie.

Suitable sauces

Pork:

  • Apple sauce
  • Wholegrain mustard
  • Cider sauce
  • Wild mushrooms

Offal:

  • Orange chutney
  • Cranberry chutney
  • Red onion marmalade
  • Tomato-based sauce

(Avoid using cream-based sauces when serving offal)

Menus and cooking

  • Follow recipe guidelines and use picture examples as presentation suggestions.
  • Finish dishes and make adjustments to optimise quality, for example thicken a sauce that is too runny.
  • Check finished item before placing it on the pass, confirm standards with the supervisor or head chef.
  • Finish dishes according to recipe, for example use correct garnish such as microherb salad, correct sauce or flavour such as tomato confit.
  • Present dishes to maximise visual impact – for example, sauce can be spooned around or in the centre, meat/offal and vegetables can be arranged using colour or texture, height can be created and the overall impression made appealing.
  • Sauce can be added as part of the dish or separately, for example a dipping sauce.

Storage of cooked dishes

Once service is finished ensure that correct storage procedures are applied:

  • Check all items in regards to freshness, smell and appearance. Place any leftover items into containers and back in the fridge.
  • Put all accompaniments and sauces into containers, cover and label and add a date and place in the fridge or coolroom.
  • Keep raw and cooked items separate.
  • Store in the appropriate areas in the fridge or coolroom.
  • Apply FIFO for the next service.
  • Plan out the mise en place needs and orders for the next service. Consider the workload.
  • Share with your colleagues.

Handout 4: Meat recipe – complex and modern

Spring lamb with a herb crust, seasoned faggot, fondant potato, spring vegetables, carrot purée and a port and redcurrant jus

Spring herb crust – ingredients

  • 6 slices of white bread
  • 200g flat leaf parsley
  • 100g fresh rosemary
  • 50g cheese
  • 1 clove of garlic
  • 2 teaspoons of olive oil
  • Salt and pepper

Spring herb crust – method

Finely grate the bread and cheese; finely chop all the herbs and garlic. Combine ingredients with a little olive oil.

Lamb loin – ingredients

  • 2.5kg rack of lamb
  • 15ml of olive oil
  • Knob of butter
  • Salt and pepper

Lamb loin – method

Take the rack of lamb; remove the bark (thin skin) using either your fingers or a knife. With the rest of the rack of lamb, use a knife to carefully cut around the loin of the lamb and begin to release it. Using a sharp knife, trim the sinew and fat attached to the loin of lamb.

Pan fry the lamb loin in a little butter evenly colouring the loin and season with salt and pepper. Place into oven at 170°C for about 2 minutes, take the loin out and roll in the herb crust, ensuring the loin is equally covered. Place back in the oven for a further 2–3 minutes.

Rest for 4–5 minutes before slicing.

Fondant potato – ingredients

  • 4 large potatoes
  • Sprig of rosemary
  • 200g salted butter
  • 200ml chicken stock
  • 2 garlic cloves
  • Salt and pepper

Fondant potato – method

Put the butter and potatoes in a saucepan and onto the heat, and fry until deep golden-brown on one side, about 5–6 minutes.

Turn over the potatoes and cook for a further 5–6 minutes or until golden-brown on both sides. Carefully pour in the stock and then add the garlic cloves and rosemary sprigs.

Place the pan into the oven at 170°C until tender, then remove the potatoes from the pan using a palette knife and keep warm.

Carrot purée – ingredients

  • 300g carrots
  • 50g salted butter
  • Chicken stock

Carrot purée – method

Put the carrots in a saucepan and pour over just enough boiling water or stock to cover them, then cover with a lid. Simmer for 15–20 minutes until soft.

Drain the carrots and place in a blender, adding some of the stock and butter, then purée until very smooth and pass through a sieve. Taste and adjust seasoning.

Spring vegetables – ingredients

  • Baby carrots
  • Peas
  • Beetroot
  • Asparagus
  • Shallots
  • Vegetable stock

Spring vegetables – method

Wash, prepare the vegetables and cut into the required size, lightly colour in a little butter using a frying pan. Blanch baby carrots in the vegetable stock and repeat the colouring process. Adjust seasoning and keep warm.

Lamb stock – ingredients

  • 5lb lamb bones with attached meat
  • 3 tablespoons olive oil
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 6 whole black peppercorns
  • 5 fresh bay leaves
  • 3 garlic cloves
  • 1 onion, peeled and quartered
  • 2 celery stalks, cut in pieces
  • 1 carrot cut in pieces
  • 200g minced beef
  • 2 pig trotters
  • 4 egg whites
  • Red wine

Lamb stock – method

Sweat off the onion, carrot and celery in a pan in a little olive oil. Put the lamb bones and sweated vegetables into a large roasting dish.

Roast at 220°C until they are well browned. Turn them a few times and take care that they do not burn, as this will cause the stock to taste bitter. Transfer the bones and vegetables to a large heavy-based stockpot. Fill with enough cold water so the bones are covered by about 5cm of the water.

Pour off any fat in the roasting dish. Deglaze with a little red wine or water. Scrape up any sediment and cook to reduce pan juices by half. Add to the stockpot. Add pig trotters and minced beef, peppercorns, garlic and bay leaves.

Bring the stock to the boil very slowly. Greyish scum will rise to the surface as the stock comes to the boil; use a wide ladle or large spoon to skim it away. Add egg whites and then strain. Reduce stock until correct consistency and taste.

Faggot – ingredients

  • 300g lamb mince
  • 1/4 teaspoon of grated nutmeg
  • 2 teaspoons of chopped parsley
  • Pig’s caul
  • Salt and pepper

Faggot – method

If you only have roughly chopped lamb not pre-minced lamb, mince it in a mincer or, if you don’t have a mincer, chop it in a food processor. Place the minced meat into a large bowl and add herbs, nutmeg and a pinch of salt and pepper. Mix thoroughly; divide the mixture into four and shape into balls.

Wrap each ball in pig’s caul making sure it overlaps as it will seal as it cooks and hold the faggots together.

Place the faggots onto a shallow oven proof dish and bake in the hot oven for 10 minutes.

Port and redcurrant jus – ingredients

  • 200ml lamb stock
  • 50ml port
  • 2 tablespoons honey
  • 200g redcurrant jelly
  • Red food colouring

Port and redcurrant jus – method

Combine the port and stock over a medium-high heat. Stir and bring to the boil. Add the redcurrant jelly and honey to the mix, and stir well. Allow to continue boiling, again until mixture is thick and just about halved – about 15–20 minutes. Stir occasionally. Once nicely thickened and reduced add a little red food colouring, a drop at a time to achieve a deep red colour, then turn off heat. Strain if needed, making sure the consistency and seasoning is correct.
