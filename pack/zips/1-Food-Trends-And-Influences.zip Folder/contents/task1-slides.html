Introduction to influences

The modern dining experience in now regarded as a stimulation on the senses of people who choose to eat out. Today people think of the dining experience as part of the social society we live in.

Professional kitchens have taken this on board and now develop their offering to guide customers through the stimulation of senses, such as:

  • Taste
  • Sight
  • Smell

Influences on people selecting different food and drink include:

  • Historical perspective
  • Contemporary perspective
  • People’s choices

Historical influences

From a historical perspective many regions around the world have played their part in the advancement of cooking. This includes their influence on others due to their:

  • Cooking techniques
  • Commodities
  • Menu structures
  • Cultures

In each case, these influences have driven the world of gastronomy to develop and enrich the palates of modern customers.

Cooking techniques

Cooking techniques have developed and changed throughout history, from Romans to modern-day national cuisines.

Initially, Romans boiled or stewed their meats. However, with the advent of war and the conquests of other countries, their techniques began to develop. They tended to adopt local techniques to feed their troops and people. Eventually these techniques found their way back to Rome for all to enjoy.

This process continued throughout the middle ages and still continues to this day, as travel has become more prevalent.

Cooking techniques

Today, cooking techniques from far and wide are now influencing food preparation all over the world. These include:

  • Africa – wrapping food in banana leaves and steaming
  • Indian subcontinent – the use of numerous spices to impart flavours
  • Asia – quick frying techniques to retain the colour and taste

Following the voyages by Europeans, some of these techniques were then adopted in their homelands. In turn, the techniques used in Europe influenced the inhabitants of countries thousands of miles apart:

  • South America – influenced by the Spanish and Portuguese
  • Far east – influenced by the British
  • Pacific Islands – influenced by British, French and Dutch

Commodities

Menus and recipes have developed over many years and in different regions of the world. This is due in part to the availability of commodities. Transportation – initially ships – allowed the sharing of commodities throughout the ‘known’ world. These included:

  • Potatoes from South America
  • Spices and herbs from the Indian subcontinent
  • Fruit from the Caribbean
  • Rice from China
  • Melons from Africa

All of these, and many more, have influenced cooking, both historically and in modern times.

Menu structures

The structure of a menu is influenced by many factors such as culture, commodities and the occasions.

In early times the main focus of the menu was on simple foods that had been adopted from other regions, especially so in the Roman era.

During the middle ages the European menu was focused on meat, with less emphasis on vegetables.

During the 17th century, food menus developed with the onset of classical French cuisine. This was shared later in the 18th century as French chefs migrated overseas.

The 19th century saw the development of individual courses on a menu, substituting the list of food. It was at this time that signature dishes began to appear.

Cultures

The historical influence that had the largest impact on eating and drinking out was culture. Throughout history all parts of the world have adopted differently and the effect of their own cultures has spread.

  • In ancient times meals lasted for hours
  • Continental Europe saw mealtimes as a gathering of the family
  • In the Far East it was acceptable to eat with your fingers
  • French chefs were held in high regard in the 17th and 18th centuries
  • Different cultures have different meanings for the names of a meal. A snack in Europe may be considered a meal in the Far East

Current influences

Modern chefs and their customers are influenced by factors such as:

  • The impact of geography on commodities:
    • soil
    • climate
    • lakes
    • rivers
    • sea
    • terrain

Another current influence are people’s beliefs and religions, including:

  • Types of commodities used
  • Preparation methods

Geography

The effect of geography on the supply, type and quality of any food commodity cannot be understated. Professional chefs need to understand this difference.

Soil

Soil provides nutrients for vegetables, fruits and other organic foods. If there are insufficient amounts of nutrients, the growth of the plant will be restricted. The type of soil can also affect the overall flavours of a food. An example of this is the different soils that grapes are grown in for particular tastes.

Climate

The climate probably has the largest impact on food. This includes providing sunlight and warmth for growing organic food and rainfall: not enough rain and both plants and animals may not grow. Certain food only grows in certain areas due to the climate in that area.

Geography

Lakes, rivers and the sea

Communities close to a lake or river, or surrounded by the sea, tend to have an abundance of fish. Lakes and rivers also provide a source of water for agriculture.

Terrain

Understanding the terrain and the effect it has on producing commodities is essential for producers. The terrain can best be defined as the ‘natural features’ of an area of land. Some commodities need good drainage, others plenty of water, more shade than sunlight etc.

People’s choices

There are many factors that influence a person in their choice with regards to food and drink, including:

  • Family
  • Health
  • Costs
  • Lifestyle choices
  • Availability of commodities
  • The media
  • Equipment

People’s choices

Family

The choice to eat out is a social behaviour that is often influenced by the family circle. This includes dining out for a celebration or as a family event that has developed over time.

Health

A person’s health will directly effect their choice of food and drink. This may be due to allergic reactions to some types of food or a person’s choice to eat certain foods for nutritional reasons.

Cost

Choosing a place to eat food or drink may well be defined by the costs involved. While you may go to a restaurant for a celebration you might only go to a café for a Sunday roast.

People’s choices

Lifestyle choices

A key factor today is people making lifestyle choices. These have materialised due to the expanding fields of travel, income and availability of commodities, among others. These factors include:

  • Increased availability of ready meals
  • Pressure of time – demands of family and work
  • Supermarket shopping
  • Fashion/fads/trends
  • Availability and appreciation of fine foods and drink
  • Tourism
  • Takeaway/eating out

People’s choices

Lifestyle choices

Another key lifestyle choice is diet, including:

  • vegetarian
  • vegan
  • healthy eating

Availability of commodities

Commodities are now available almost all year around, due to growing seasons in different countries and the ability to move vast quantities of food via planes and ships. Previously, shops only stocked a small amount of different food products; today the largest supermarkets hold about 40,000 food products, with 1,200 being organic.

People’s choices

Media

The media has a major influence on what people think and buy when it comes to food and drink. This influence is seen on TV, in books, magazines and in newspapers. It is the media that provides information on:

  • Eating out – advertising
  • Food poisoning outbreaks
  • Diets/healthy eating
  • Nutrition information
  • Marketing of food products

People’s choices

Equipment

The advantages of modern equipment has allowed people to taste foods from around the world while still fresh.

Modern equipment has allowed chefs and home cooks to develop and tantalise taste buds the world over. The types of equipment include:

  • Refrigeration, including freezing for storing and transporting
  • Microwaves for fast cooking
  • Mixers to reduce time and effort
  • Gas BBQs to reduce waste
  • Mobile cooking facilities – street cooking
