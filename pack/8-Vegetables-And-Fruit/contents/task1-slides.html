Introduction to fruits and vegetables

Fruit and vegetables are used extensively in menus during all services.

They provide colour, texture and balanced flavours, and supply the essential nutritional balance to the meal.

Quality is an extremely important consideration as the colour, dietary value and customer appeal deteriorate incrementally with a lower quality product.

Vegetable protein is becoming popular, for example using soy beans or soy bean products such as tofu. Seaweed and edible kelp are common in Asian cuisine.

Cooking with root vegetables

  • Root vegetables are fibrous, and high in starch and protein content.
  • They harden and become tougher as they mature.
  • They often have a pronounced flavour, for example turnips and swedes.
  • Older root vegetables need a cold water start when boiling or blanching to leach out bitter flavours and to ensure even cooking.
  • Young root vegetables have a sweeter taste due to their higher sugar content so use a hot water start. As the vegetable ages, the sugar converts to starch. This explains why young carrots are quite sweet tasting and tender, whilst old carrots become woody and lose flavour.

Cooking with green vegetables

Green vegetables vary considerably in their appearance and taste as they may be the leaves, stems, flowers, fruits, seed pods or seeds of a huge variety of plants. This also means that they are not always green, for example yellow squash or pumpkin.

You should use a hot water start when boiling green vegetables, as this helps to retain their appearance, nutrient content and reduces reheating times during service.

Par cooking is a process that is often used to allow vegetables to retain optimal colour. They are blanched in boiling water for a short period of time, refreshed in cold or iced water and then drained and stored. They can then be quickly reheated in a pan with oil and/or butter and seasonings for service. Microwave ovens are increasingly used for this reheating purpose.

Types of fungi

Fungi are a group of organisms that are not animals, plants or bacteria. They have important culinary uses and include yeasts, moulds, truffles and mushrooms. In the kitchen the key fungi used are mould in cheese production, yeast in bread and pastry production and truffles and mushrooms in the production of sauces and soups and various other applications.

Cultivated mushrooms are sold as button, cup or flat mushrooms. These types are all the same variety agaricus bisporus) but at different stages of maturity. Speciality and exotic varieties have become popular due to their flavour and shapes:

Types of fungi

  • Common field mushrooms or brown field mushrooms are also referred to as Swiss brown or Italian brown. They are similar to the commonly used, standard white mushrooms but have a firmer texture, darker colour and more intense flavour.
  • Portobello mushrooms are the mature variety of the Swiss brown and can be utilised similarly to flat mushrooms.
  • Oyster mushrooms have a mild flavour and appealing shape and can be used for decoration.

Types of fungi

  • Enoki mushrooms are grown in clumps and have long slim stems.
  • Shiitake mushrooms can be purchased fresh or dried. They have a strong woody flavour and are often used in Asian cooking.
  • Wild varieties of ceps, chanterelles and morels mainly grow in Europe and America, they usually are found in dark and damp woods close to birch and oak tree. However, they can be cultivated and farmed in different parts of the world. which ensure freshness when not found in their natural habitat. They possess a very intense flavour.
  • Truffles are edible fungi that grow underground.

Fruit

Most markets have an abundance of fresh fruit ranging from cold climate fruit such as apples, to exotic tropical fruit like rambutans and lychees.

Most fruits contain a wide variety of vitamins and minerals and can be eaten in their natural state. Some exceptions include rhubarb, lemon, cranberries and kumquats, which may be too bitter, sour or woody for direct consumption. These fruits are often stewed with sugar to soften the fibres and to impart extra flavour. (Rhubarb is actually a vegetable but is used as a fruit in sweet dishes.)

Nuts

Nuts are found within the fruits of certain plants and contain the seeds. Nuts are very high in fat, which makes them an ideal source of oils, which are used in both savoury and sweet applications.

  • Peanut oil (along with sesame oil made from sesame seeds) is an essential ingredient for many Asian dishes.
  • Almond and hazelnut oil are often used in desserts.
  • Nuts also form the base of specialised sauces such as pesto, a paste made with a mixture of pine nuts, basil, garlic, cheese and olive oil.
  • Satay sauce is a very popular nut-based Asian sauce, used with different meats and vegetables.
  • Whole or ground nuts may be used as a meat substitute in a vegetarian diet, along with pulses.
  • The most common use of nuts is in dessert recipes.

Handout 1: Exotic fruit and vegetables of the world

Fruit

Asian pears are available July to late October. Those with a clear yellow-brown skin are from Japan, while those from China are a clear-skinned green-yellow. Select fruits with a fragrant aroma. Unlike most pears, Asian pears are ripe even when they do not yield to pressure. Asian pears contain the vitamins A and C and approximately 61calories per pear.

Carambolas or star fruit are available from June to March. The pale yellow, edible, ribbed skin covers a pulp whose flavour, aroma and texture suggest a blend of apples, grapes and citrus. Choose firm, partly or fully yellow carambola. Ripen at room temperature (the ribs will turn brown) and then refrigerate as needed. To serve, cut into star slices, keeping the skin intact. Briefly stir fry to complement poultry, seafood or meat. Use in jams, relishes, drinks, ices, glazes, sherbets and mousses.

Cherimoyas are also known as the custard apple and are available in late November to May. Cherimoya has a creamy white flesh and large, black seeds. The custard-like texture has the combined flavours of pineapple, papaya and banana. Most cherimoyas are picked green. To ripen the fruit, allow to sit at room temperature for up to one week. Keep it out of direct sunlight and turn the fruit often. A ripe cherimoya will be dark brown-green in colour and yield to gentle pressure.

Guavas are available September to January. The skin is green or yellow and can be pear-shaped or round, measuring from 14 inches in diameter. Guava flesh is white, red or salmon-coloured and the flavour ranges from strawberry to lemon to tropical. Soften guavas at room temperature until they yield to gentle pressure but refrigerate ripe guavas. Guava is recommended for jellies, preserves, sherbets, fruit drinks or as a breakfast item.

Kumquats are available December to May. Two varieties, an oval or round, are entirely edible. The texture is juicy and they contain tiny white seeds. The skin is sweet but the flesh of the fruit is tart. Refrigerate kumquats, with the stems intact, for up to one month. Serve kumquats in fresh fruit drinks, gelatin salads and desserts or make into a preserve.

Lychees are an ancient Chinese fruit that are available from mid-June to mid-July. They are most popular in the United States as a canned product. Lychees have a sweet flavour similar to grapes with a touch of cherry. The creamy white fruit is small and round with a red, leathery, bumpy outer covering that can be peeled before consuming. Discard the smooth brown seed. Lychees can be stored in the refrigerator for up to three weeks without flavour loss. (Refrigeration quickly browns the peel.) Lychees can be eaten alone or combined with other fruits like pineapple, papayas, bananas or mangoes.

Mangoes are available year round in many varieties, shapes and sizes. Peel the skin to expose the ripe, juicy yellow pulp. A ripe mango will give slightly to the touch and have a light mango aroma at the stem end. Allow fruit to ripen at room temperature. Puréed mango can be added to drinks, desserts, sauces and salad dressings. Cubed mango may be sprinkled with lime juice or sugar and frozen for later use.

Papaya is available year round. Except for its yellow-green skin, it resembles an avocado in shape, with a solid, bright reddish-orange pulp and soft, black, non-edible seeds. Allow papaya to ripen at room temperature until the colour changes from green to yellow gold. A ripe papaya will have a soft fruity aroma. Puréed papaya is good in dressings, tropical drinks and yoghurt. Papaya can be used in marinades as it contains an enzyme (along with pineapple) which breaks down meat tissues so is useful for making tough meat more tender and permitting the use of a quick, dry cooking method. Papaya chunks may be cooked in meat, fish and poultry dishes. Shredded, unripe papaya is used frequently in the spicy green papaya salad throughout South-East Asia.

Passion fruit, native to Brazil, is the edible fruit of the passion flower. Early Spanish missionaries named the fruit in honour of the passion of Christ. The fruit is available year round from either New Zealand, Florida or California. It is a small round fruit the size of a large egg with a smooth (unripe) or wrinkled (ripe), red, yellow or purple-brown skin. The yellow flesh and small black edible seeds have an intense aromatic flavour while the texture is jelly-like and watery. Passion fruit is ripe when the skin is wrinkled and looks old.

Plantains are available year round from Ecuador and Mexico. They are slightly acidic and squash-like in taste. The skin is thick with a salmon-coloured pulp which sweetens as it ripens. The fruit must be cooked. Green plantains are often used in stews while ripe plantains can be sliced and fried. Even when the skin is black, the plantain is ideal for dessert recipes. Store in a cool, dry place for up to three weeks.

Tomatillos are available year round from Mexico and California. The tomatillo has a papery covering and the fruit resembles a green cherry tomato. The solid, seedy flesh has a slight, sweet apple or plum flavour that is enhanced by cooking. Store in a cool, dry area. Select fruit that is hard and does not give like tomatoes. Traditional uses include steaming and adding to salsas, Mexican stews and casseroles. They are the base of salsa verde or green sauce.

Ugli fruit is a native of Jamaica and available December to April. It is thought to be a cross between a tangerine and a grapefruit so the thick, loose skin peels very easily to reveal sweet yellow-orange sections with few seeds. Uneven colouring, bronzing or scarred skin is normal and because of its thick skin, it may be stored in the refrigerator for up to two weeks. Choose and use ugli fruit like a grapefruit.

Vegetables

Belgian endives have a white head of yellow-tipped, closely wrapped leaves. They have a mild, bitter flavour. Belgian endives are packaged in opaque containers and should be refrigerated.

Bok choy is an Oriental cabbage that has thick, white stalks that support large, spoon-shaped dark green leaves. When cooked, bok choy is tender and sweet, yet crisp. Large-leaf bok choy can be used in soups. Refrigerate unwashed bok choy in perforated plastic bags for up to three days.

Calabaza is a native American hard-shelled squash available year round. Calabaza is round to pear-shaped and comes in several sizes with a mottled green to buff-coloured skin. The flesh is bright orange with a fine-grained, sweet, moist texture. Calabaza can be cut and then refrigerated for up to a month. Calabaza is a popular ingredient in Caribbean and West Indian cooking and it can be substituted for other squashes in recipes.

Flowering kale is often referred to as ornamental cabbage. It has large, wrinkled leaves of either a purple or cream colour laced with green trim. Flowering kale has a milder flavour than cabbage and can be used in salads, steamed, boiled or used in stir-fry.

Jerusalem artichoke or sunchoke is a root tuber. Tan to cream in colour, sunchokes can be smooth and pearly but often are lumpy and bulbous. The Jerusalem artichoke is similar to ginger root in size and colour. The flesh is crisp like water chestnuts. Refrigerate and thoroughly scrub the vegetable to remove any sand or soil. It can be used in vegetable salads or meat or fish dishes.

Daikon is also known as the Japanese or Oriental radish. It resembles a white carrot and is sweet, juicy and slightly hotter than the traditional radish. It weighs 12 pounds and averages a foot long. Daikon can be added to relishes and salads.

Jicama is also known as the Mexican potato. Jicama is brown-skinned with a white flesh of a delicate, slight sweet flavour. It is a root tuber that should be smooth and firm with relatively unblemished roots. If a jicama is large or becomes shrivelled, cut it into pieces. Refrigerate the vegetable, unwrapped for up to three weeks. Once cut, it can be covered by plastic and used within one week. Jicama can be served cold and raw or in soups, stews or salads. It can also be used as a substitute for water chestnuts.

Kohlrabi is also known as the cabbage turnip. Kohlrabi is characterized by a swollen stem and a delicate, turnip-like flavour that is either green or purple. Wrap and refrigerate kohlrabi for up to one week. Kohlrabi leaves can be eaten raw in salads or steamed. Cook the bulbs as you would turnips.

Apricot, gooseberry and redcurrant crème brûlée tart

Pastry ingredients

  • 200g x plain flour
  • 100g x butter
  • 25ml x cold water
  • Flour, for dusting

Pastry method

Tip flour into a mixing bowl. Cut cold butter into small pieces and add. Toss to coat with flour. Using your fingertips, rub the butter into the flour, lifting it in the bowl as you do so to keep it light and cool. Continue until it looks like breadcrumbs.

Sprinkle in the cold water and mix until the mixture starts to come together. Gather the dough with your hands, knead lightly to form a smooth ball.

Heat oven to 150°C. Roll out the pastry on a lightly floured surface to the thickness of 4mm, then lift into a large tart tin. Press down gently on the bottom and sides, leaving a slight overhang. Line the tart with foil and fill with baking beans. Bake for 10 mins, then discard the foil and beans, and bake for another 20 minutes. Remove from the oven and leave to cool.

Filling ingredients

  • 200g x apricots
  • 200g x gooseberries
  • 50g x redcurrants
  • 200g x white caster sugar
  • 100ml water
  • 4 x eggs
  • 100ml x double cream

Filling method

Tip the ¾ of the apricots, gooseberries and redcurrants into a saucepan with 100g of the sugar and 100ml water. Simmer for 8–10 minutes until the fruit is soft and the juices are syrupy. Tip the fruit into a sieve set over a jug and leave to strain – you will need about 150ml of the syrupy juices. Tip the pulp into a bowl and leave to cool.

In a separate bowl, beat the eggs with 50g of the sugar, then beat in the cream and gooseberry syrup. Strain through a sieve into another jug and set aside.

Spread the pulp evenly over the base of the tart, then carefully pour the cream mixture over it to create two layers add the last ¼ of the apricots, gooseberries and redcurrants on top of the mix. Bake for 35–40 minutes at 130°C until the cream layer has the slightest wobble to it. Remove from the oven and trim the pastry edges. Leave to cool completely, then scatter over the remaining sugar and caramelise with a blowtorch.

NB: Variations of this recipe can be created using different seasonal fruits.

Handout 4: Vegetable recipe – complex and modern

Beetroot, parsnip, fennel, apple and chive burgers

Ingredients

  • 3 x medium beetroot, peeled and coarsely grated
  • 2 x eating apples, coarsely grated
  • 1 x fennel bulb, finely chopped
  • 200g parsnip, coarsely grated
  • Bunch fresh chive
  • 1 tbsp fennel and cumin seeds, toasted in a dry pan and lightly crushed using pestle and mortar
  • 600g vegetarian sausage mix
  • 100g fresh breadcrumbs
  • 1 x egg
  • 50ml olive oil

To serve

  • Brioche roll or wholegrain roll, toasted
  • Rocket leaves
  • Tomato and red onion slices
  • Red chard leaves
  • Wholegrain mustard mayonnaise
  • Fresh beer-battered onion rings

Method

To make the burgers, combine all the ingredients (except the oil) with 200ml water in a large mixing bowl and knead with your hands for around five minutes to form a dense but pliable burger mixture. Add a splash more water if needed. Cover the bowl with clingfilm, then put in the fridge for 15 minutes.

Divide the burger mixture evenly into eight pieces, then shape each one into a round patty, around 2.5cm thick.

Heat oil in a large frying pan. When hot, add the patties and fry for 10 minutes on each side.

Serve the burgers in toasted brioche/wholegrain rolls with rocket, tomato and red onion slices, and mustard mayonnaise. Serve with fresh beer-battered onion rings on the side.

NB: Variations of this dish can be created using different seasonal vegetables, for example using pumpkin or butternut squash.
