Equipment needed for vegetable and fruit preparation

  • Equipment use has to be considered in the context of the establishment. Menus should be designed with the available equipment in mind. Plan the menu to utilise different equipment, to spread out the load and use different staff skill levels – deep-fried tempura is more difficult to cook than marinated vegetables.
  • Large equipment includes fonts and bratt pans for the production of vegetable stocks and sauces, for example tomato sauce.
  • Pots are used for boiling and blanching, whilst pans are perfect for all sauté items.
  • Braising pans are used for fibrous vegetables and fruit.
  • The deep fryer is a key part of potato dishes such as chipped potatoes and other fried items.
  • The bain-marie is used to keep sauces and batches of vegetables warm during service.
  • Grills and salamanders are common to finish items such as moussaka whilst the stove, solid top or gas burner are used for all pan-based dishes.

Equipment needed for vegetable and fruit preparation

  • Tools used in the preparation are cutting boards (coloured for vegetables and fruit), knives and special cutting tools like a parisienne cutter for specific shapes.
  • Bowls and containers are used for storage and batters. These also require whisks and spiders, firstly in the production, secondly when removing fried items or spare batter.
  • Palette knives and spatulas are good for lifting delicate items, whilst ladles are needed for sauces and soups.
  • Tools vary based on the finishes of the food items. Nuts and fruit, and preparations like duxelles are often chopped using knives or can also be blended or minced.
  • Rolling pins may be needed for dishes that are encased in pastry.
  • Trays are used for crumbing or drawing items through a parmesan crust.

Benefits of different cookery and preparation methods

The restaurant industry is very competitive and often the little touches provided on a plate make all the difference. Cooking vegetables differently can provide an inexpensive option for differentiating your establishment.

  • Nutrition – steaming retains the most vitamins.
  • Eye appeal – glazed vegetables are glossy and look appetising.
  • Colour retention – blanching vegetables maintains and even enhances their colour.
  • Flavour – a combination of vegetables, for example in ratatouille, provides satisfying taste
  • Storage – pickling vegetables allows for extended storage.

Benefits of different cookery and preparation methods

  • Improved digestion – cooked cabbage is easier to digest than raw.
  • Enhanced texture and softened fibres – boiling corn softens the texture.
  • Contrast – utilising different cookery methods enhances the final menu item.
  • Always select the most suitable equipment, such as smaller pots for smaller amounts, and only cover the items with water – if you use too much water it takes longer to heat and wastes time and a precious resource! Do not cook too long as the vegetables discolour and look grey.
  • Always apply personal and food hygiene throughout preparation and finishing of dishes.

Potato cookery

Potatoes form the major starch component of dishes in Western cookery and a wide range of potato varieties is now available. The varieties have different properties: some are waxier meaning they have less starch and retain their shape when cooked; others have higher starch content, thus being more floury and are ideally used for purée or mashed potato dishes.

  • Washed potatoes have the advantage of less time in preparation, whilst brushed potatoes store better.
  • Always store potatoes in a dark place to prevent greening of the surface, which indicates the presence of the toxin solanine.
  • Potatoes provide starch to the diet and provide bulk to the meal. Whilst potatoes are mainly used as an accompaniment they are also used for dishes in their own right – potatoes such as hash browns and fried potatoes are popular for hot breakfast.

Potato accompaniments

  • Potato dishes provide a wide range of shapes, flavours and contrasts. Always consider your final product when making a decision in regards to the cookery method used for your potato dish.
  • The general rule is to use a softer potato accompaniment if you have sauce with the menu item or a recipe using a moist heat cookery method. For grilled dishes, fried potatoes are a more suitable accompaniment
  • Using varying methods creates an increased menu selection for your customers and displays the skill level of the chef. Quite often a combination of cookery methods is used to have the potatoes ready for service. Rissoler-style and duchess potatoes are commonly used accompaniments.

Potato finishes

The five main methods of potato finishes are as follows:

  • Baked – whole or peeled potatoes are sliced or pre-cut and placed onto a tray. Sometimes a liquid and other flavouring is added, then they are baked in the oven until soft in the centre
  • Water-finished – the potato is peeled, cut and placed into cold water. Bring to the boil, skim and cook through until soft in the centre. Other ingredients like butter may be added.
  • Water-blanched and fat-finished – the potato is peeled, cut into the desired shape and cooked slightly underdone. During service the potato is finished in a pan with hot fat.
  • Fat-blanched and fat-finished – the peeled potato is cut into specific sizes. If the shape is larger, it is fat-blanched at ~140°C to prevent it browning too much. During service, finish at 180°C to cook through and crisp it up.
  • Directly fat-finished – the peeled potato is cut into small sizes and finished at 180°C.

Cookery methods used for fruit

Cookery methods are chosen for a wide variety of reasons:

  • Eye appeal – glazed fruits are glossy and look appetising
  • Flavour – certain cookery methods enhance natural flavours, for example apple compote
  • Storage – cooking fruit with sugar allows for extended storage
  • Improved digestion – cooked rhubarb is easier to digest than raw
  • Enhanced texture and softened fibres – stewing quince softens the texture.

Cookery methods used for fruit

  • Contrast – utilising different cookery methods enhances the final menu item such as a complex dessert.
  • When cooking fruit to extend storage or change its properties it is important to consider the texture. Poaching is often used as it is a gentle method and the fruit retains its shape, whilst the flavours are extracted into the cooking liquid.
  • Compote describes the process of stewing fruit such as rhubarb with sugar and then serving it in its own juice.
  • Always store cooked fruit covered with clingfilm or a lid to prevent contamination, and preferably in its own juice. Never remove fruit with your hands – rather use implements such as tongs or slotted spoons, remembering to clean them in between uses to avoid contamination.

Finishing fruit and vegetables to dish requirements

  • Consider the type of menu items that your clientele is likely to order, for example fresh vegetables, fresh fruit, compotes, vegetarian dishes, baked beans for breakfast.
  • Quality of the key ingredients will determine the final product.
  • Many vegetables, fruit and pulses are prepared ready for service.
  • Consider the portion size required by the establishment, the cuts needed (for example brunoise) and the amounts that you are likely to sell to maintain the quality and freshness.

Finishing fruit and vegetables to dish requirements

  • Prepare stuffing and coatings following the correct processes, for example draw through flour, egg and crumbs.
  • The flavourings used in the dish also have an impact and must be a part of the mise en place, for example seasoning, soy sauce, spice pastes, marinades (hoisin for tofu, vinegar or special oils) to add during service. Fruit is usually placed in a stock syrup that has been infused with spices such as vanilla, cinnamon or star anise but can also be cooked using juices.
  • Also consider the type of sauce served with the dish. Creamy sauces such as béchamel are more filling hence a smaller serving size of vegetable bake is sufficient. With stir-fry vegetables, tofu can be added for extra nutrition whilst rice will add bulk.

Vegetable menus and cooking

  • Cook the dishes applying the correct method of cookery, for example roasting, stir frying or pan frying, grilling or baking for dry heat cookery methods and braising, steaming, poaching for moist heat cookery methods.
  • Ensure that you use the correct amount of ingredients. The items must be cooked to the correct degree and should be checked regularly, for example is the pear cooked through?
  • Check with colleagues or supervisors that you are applying the correct process – for example tempura is cooked long enough with correct colour and thin batter. Follow their instructions such as using vegetable or olive oil for potato dishes to crisp them on the outside, then finish with butter to enhance the flavour and eating quality.

Vegetable menus and cooking

  • Follow recipe guidelines and use picture examples as presentation suggestions.
  • Finish dishes and make adjustments to optimise quality – for example thicken a sauce that is too runny.
  • Check finished item before placing it on the pass and confirm standards with the supervisor or head chef.
  • Finish dishes according to recipe – use correct garnish such as fried glass noodles, correct sauce or flavour: tomato confit for stuffed courgettes, coconut milk for vegetarian curry, gratinate potato under the salamander.
  • Present dishes to maximise visual impact – sauce is spooned around dish or in the centre, vegetables are arranged using colour or texture, height is created and the overall impression is appealing.
  • Sauce can either be added as part of the dish or separately in a container, for example dipping sauce.

Storage of cooked dishes

Once service is finished ensure that correct storage procedures are applied:

  • Check all items in regards to freshness, smell and appearance. Place any leftover items into containers and back in the fridge.
  • Put all accompaniments and sauces into containers. Cover, label, add a date and place in the fridge or coolroom.
  • Keep raw and cooked items separate.
  • Store in the appropriate areas in the fridge or coolroom.
  • Apply ‘first in first out’ (FIFO) for the next service.
  • Plan out the mise en place needs and orders for the next service. Consider the workload.
  • Share with your colleagues.
