Seafood preparation

Seafood preparation requires a high skill level to avoid excessive waste. Whilst more and more pre-cut products are used, the chef must know the basic preparation steps and portion cuts.

Seafood is very susceptible to contamination through poor handling during preparation so you need to establish standard operating procedures (SOPs) and systems to prevent any food poisoning.

Preventing seafood spoilage

It is important to remember that spoilage is a health hazard. It can cost you money and possibly your kitchen’s reputation but it can be prevented by correct preparation, cleaning and storage. The following basic rules will help you to minimise contamination.

  • Establish overall SOPs and HACCP systems including cleaning schedules.
  • Minimise handling of seafood.
  • Wash your hands prior to any handling of seafood.
  • Handle seafood carefully and separate according to species to prevent cross-contamination.
  • Never mix cooked and uncooked fish and shellfish.
  • You should rinse fresh fish, then store it on ice. Keep covered and moist at 0–2°C.

Seafood preparation – work area

  • Use designated cutting boards (blue for raw fish) to prevent cross-contamination.
  • Process only small amounts at a time and put it straight back into the fridge or cold room.
  • Clean and then sanitise your work area regularly to prevent any build-up of bacteria – dry wipe and scrub, rinse with warm water to soften remnants, then use a brush and detergent to clean, rinse with clean water, dry and sanitise.

Buying seafood

  • Seafood can be purchased live, fresh whole, fresh gutted, as fillets and frozen.
  • They can be purchased individually or in bulk. Portion-controlled cuts and prepared items such as marinated skewers, pre-cleaned mussels, shucked oysters and sliced smoked salmon are also available.
  • Fish is often delivered gutted to extend its lifespan and ice is placed on top to keep it at a temperature of 0–2°C to maximise storage life.

Preparing fresh fish

  • Fresh fish can spoil fairly easily and this is exacerbated by poor handling practices such as unwashed hands or equipment leading to cross-contamination.
  • Fresh whole fish is best stored on ice until ready for filleting.
  • After preparation, the contact with water and ice should be minimised to prevent gaping and loss of flavour.
  • Keep covered at 0–2°C.

Preparing whole fresh fish

  • To prepare whole fresh fish, scale and gut the fish, then use coloured or seafood-only cutting boards for processing the fish.
  • Remove the fins and fillets (meat from bone) and if desired, skin the fish.
  • Place the fillets onto a perforated tray to allow the melted ice and fluids to drip off.
  • Cover or vacuum pack and top with ice, then refrigerate.

Preparing shellfish

  • To prepare shellfish wash, clean and remove shell, barnacles or byssus thread on mussels, clean cephalopod and remove beak, intestines and skin. Place on ice.
  • Only process a small amount of seafood at a time and do not leave seafood at room temperature for extended time periods. Seafood can be processed further into farces, stuffed or filled, wrapped or coated with crumbs.

Seafood cuts

Fish can be prepared and served in a wide variety of ways, which are determined by the cut of fish and the portion size. As a general rule of thumb:

  • Round fish have a waste percentage of 50%
  • Flat fish have a waste percentage of 60%.

Preparing seafood to dish requirements

  • Consider the type of seafood that your customer is likely to order, for example freshwater fish, whole fish or fillets, crustaceans such as lobster, molluscs such as oysters, or calamari rings.
  • As seafood is prepared ready for service, consider the portion size required, the cuts needed (such as goujons or fillets) and the amounts that you are likely to sell. Remember, seafood will go off quicker once cut and handled.

Seafood preservation methods

Sometimes you may get a special offer on excellent seafood due to a glut or surplus supply. Knowing classical preservation methods will allow you to take advantage of this opportunity. (Equally it may simply enable you to offer something special on the menu or to extend the skills of staff by developing new recipes and applications.)

  • Salting – dry versus wet (brine) (see photos)
  • Drying – salted and exposed to air
  • Smoking – cold versus hot
  • Marinades/pickling – use of vinegar or soy sauce, coconut milk and lime
  • Canning – seafood cooked in a jar to seal it hermetically
  • Chilling – use of ice to extend lifespan
  • Vacuum packaging – minimises oxidisation.

Seafood storage

  • Due to the susceptibility to spoilage and the relatively high cost of seafood it is important to establish a detailed analysis of the establishment’s needs and actual sales. Having a mixture of canned, frozen, fresh and live product will provide menu choice and assist in stock management.
  • Canned seafood such as tuna in oil, brine or spring water, canned salmon and pickled fish have extensive storage time and can be used for many menu applications.
  • Fresh seafood should be delivered in temperature-controlled vehicles or at least in sealed boxes and stored on ice. Ensure that you use reputable suppliers which are preferably HACCP-accredited.

Seafood storage

  • Develop standard operating procedures for the delivery of goods such as: delivery and order specifications; temperature and quality control checks; and storage standards (for example moving items into appropriate storage immediately). Cover and label.
  • Frozen seafood is also an effective way to always have stock on hand which can be cooked to order or thawed out on a needs basis. With frozen seafood it is important to have a good inventory system and use ‘first in, first out’ (FIFO) to ensure that stock is used appropriately. Many products are now individually frozen and can individually be thawed out easily or even cooked from frozen, cutting down waste.
  • Use FIFO and order according to your kitchens needs. It may be better to get three deliveries a week and use the entire product than get one large delivery and lose products due to spoilage.

Fresh seafood storage – points of care

  • Store at 0–2°C.
  • Use shaved ice or ice slurry to allow the ice to melt away from the fish or fillets without causing bruising.
  • Place the seafood on top of the ice. Make sure that the seafood does not sit in the ice for too long and that it does not sit in the liquid of the thawed ice.
  • Preferably use perforated trays to allow for drainage.
  • Place whole fish with belly cavity down so it can drain well.

Fresh seafood storage – points of care

  • Place fillets flat with the skin side up to prevent gaping. Use only two layers to prevent squashing.
  • Crustaceans must be stored belly side down on ice.
  • Prepared and opened oysters should sit flat on the shell, covered with clingfilm to prevent contamination. Before service the clingfilm should be removed.
  • Smoked and cured seafood should be placed on trays and stored in the fridge.
  • Modified atmosphere-packed (MAP) items require temperature controls to prevent spoilage.

Seafood storage

Temperature-controlled coolrooms and fridges will be a major factor to ensure correct storage of seafood. It may be necessary to add plastic sheeting on the inside of your coolroom door to prevent cold air from escaping. Research shows that vertical plastic sheeting in a cool room door will cut loss of cold air by 92%.

In the coolroom/fridge it is important to store seafood correctly to prevent cross-contamination:

  • Cooked seafood on the top shelf
  • Raw seafood for service, such as opened oysters and sashimi on the next shelf (covered)
  • Fish cuts on the next shelf
  • Raw whole fish on the lower shelf
  • Ink fish on the lowest shelf to prevent drips of ink contaminating other products.

Seafood storage – frozen seafood

Frozen seafood has to be stored below –18ºC and wrapped correctly to prevent any icing up and freezer burn. Shatter packs with plastic sheet layers to separate fish fillets are ideal as you can thaw out the estimated number of portions rather than the whole box.

Always thaw seafood out in the coolroom/ fridge overnight. Never float it in water to thaw out as you can contaminate the seafood and it also affects the structure of the fish flesh. Thawing it under cold running water is just as bad and uses up a precious resource.

Seafood frozen storage – points of care

  • Store at – 18°C to – 24°C.
  • Check temperature of frozen seafood at delivery. If the product is warmer than – 18°C reject it! Place straight into the freezer.
  • Use FIFO stock rotation principles.
  • Frozen seafood should be stored in its original packaging – the types available are:
    • whole blocks, for example cooked or raw prawns, scallop meat
    • individually quick frozen (IQF), for example prawns, whole fish
    • shatter packs, for example fish fillets or prawns interleaved with plastic for easy separation
    • individually wrapped and shrink-wrapped items, for example lobster pieces.
  • Frozen seafood always needs to be covered to prevent freezer burn.
  • Observe the date of purchase and ‘use by’ date.
