Introduction to cooking seafood

There are many methods for cooking seafood. It is important to cook the seafood according to the recipe and apply the correct method of cookery, for example roasting, stir or pan frying, grilling or baking, for dry heat cookery methods and braising, steaming, poaching for moist heat cookery methods.

Ensure you cook the seafood, so it is just cooked through not overcooked and dry or undercooked and raw. Check with colleagues or supervisors that you apply the correct process, for example seafood is cooked long enough on one side with correct colour before bei turned. Spoon fat over the seafood during shallow frying to enhance moisture.

Cooking seafood

Most seafood has very little connective tissue and is relatively easy to cook but to get it perfectly moist on the inside with a crisp outside texture requires practice. This is one of the reasons why so many people do not want to cook seafood at home as it often ends up dry and overcooked.

Retaining moisture – seafood can be cooked whole which retains the juices, making it moister than fillets. Fish fillets are very popular as customers do not want to have to remove or even swallow bones.

Removing bones – the chef must consider this in the preparation and remove all the bones either by cutting out the pin bones or using tweezers to remove them.

Cooking seafood

Traditionally the presentation side of a fillet of fish is the bone side, since it looks cleaner than the skin side and does not have the bloodline running through the middle.

For some presentation styles the skin side is used, for example in Asian dishes or Creole-style cooking. The scales need to be removed and the fish is grilled or cooked on the bone side briefly to prevent curling and then finished skin side down to make it crisp and to enhance the appearance.

Most seafood is cooked through but there are some expectations where the fish or shellfish should be only just cooked:

  • Oily fish such as tuna and salmon tend to become dry once cooked through, so are often seared to a little underdone.
  • Scallops are also just seared, to keep them moist.
  • Shellfish such as prawns and lobster should only just be cooked to the point of doneness.

However, you must consider your clientele and their preferences.

Moist heat cookery methods

Fish and shellfish are very versatile and provide an excellent opportunity to display the culinary skills of the chef. Cooking times vary of course depending on the size of the seafood and the type.

Most seafood is cooked through within minutes unless it is a whole large fish. Additionally the seafood can be checked by using a thermometer for internal temperature or by making a small incision and inspecting the degree of doneness. Most fish are well done at an internal temperature of 65°C.

It is important to understand the product and which cookery methods will be best suited for it and to then apply them correctly.

Moist heat cookery methods

Boiling: submerging food in a liquid, other than fat, at 100°C.

  • Boiling is not suitable for fish as the rapid movement of the water will break up the delicate flesh.
  • Crustaceans are submerged in boiling water which is reduced to poaching temperature once it has come back to the boil.
  • Smaller crustaceans such as prawns are cooked in small batches in salted water for 1–2 minutes, drained and refreshed in ice slurry. Then drain and sprinkle with rock salt.

Menu examples:

  • Prawns
  • Lobster
  • Crabs and scampi can all be boiled for use in seafood cocktails and platters.

Moist heat cookery methods

Poaching: submerging food in a liquid, normally other than fat, between 80°C and 95°C.This is a gentle form of cooking.

  • Both deep poaching and shallow poaching are suitable for seafood.

Menu examples:

  • Whole lobster for a buffet
  • Whole dressed salmon for buffet display
  • Poached crayfish tails with asparagus tips
  • Sole paupiettes in beurre blanc.

Moist heat cookery methods

Stewing: diced food is cooked slowly, covered in a liquid, with barely perceptible movement, on top of the stove at below 80°C.

  • This method is suitable for cheaper fish species such as black bream, mackerel or mullet, especially when they are cooked in an aromatic liquid.

Menu examples:

  • Seafood curry
  • Bouillabaisse
  • Thai fish curry with green beans
  • Seafood stew romagna
  • Clam chowder
  • Gumbo

Moist heat cookery methods

Braising: seafood is cooked in the oven partially suspended in liquid, covered with a close fitting lid, at 150°C.

  • Braising is suitable for whole fish and seafood. Sauté a selection of vegetables and spices then add some liquid such as wine. Add the seafood and cover with a lid. The liquid should cover up to about ¼ of the seafood.

Menu examples:

  • Sea bream in pernod sauce
  • Chilli crab
  • Whole salmon with Asian spices.

Moist heat cookery methods

Steaming: food is cooked using the steam of a boiling liquid without being suspended in the item in the liquid.

  • This gentle cookery method is ideal for seafood as it retains the food’s texture and flavour. Do not use a pressure steamer except for octopus.

Menu examples:

  • Whole fish, for example teamed seabass on pak choy with a soy glaze
  • Steamed Chinese fish dumplings.

Dry heat cookery methods

Roasting: seafood is exposed to direct heat in the oven or turned over an open fire. Fat is used to aid in the cooking process. Temperatures vary between 80°C to 240°C.

  • Roasting of seafood is rarely used in restaurants but may be done on a barbecue.

Menu examples:

  • Roasted whiting with rosemary and chilli
  • Roast sea bream with salsa verdi.

Dry heat cookery methods

Baking: seafood is cooked using convection of hot air in an oven. The air in the oven can be modified by using steam. Temperatures vary between 160°C and 240°C

  • Baking is suitable for whole seafood or smaller items in pastry. Whole fish can be de-boned and stuffed with a farce, coated with oil and baked. Fish fillets or shellfish can be wrapped in pastry for baking.

Menu examples:

  • Baked salmon fillet in brioche
  • Mini seafood strudel.

Dry heat cookery methods

En papillote: cooking seafood by enveloping it in paper or other sealed bag, using baking as the cookery process at ~180°C.

  • Smaller seafood items are suitable for cooking en papillote. The fish is often sealed prior to enveloping. Place aromatics into a paper bag, top with the seafood, envelop and bake in the oven.

Menu examples:

  • Red snapper fillet with exotic mushrooms
  • Lobster medallion with a herb butter.

Dry heat cookery methods

Grilling: seafood is cooked by exposing it to radiated heat. It can be cooked above or below the heat source, or between two heat sources.

  • Grilling is suitable for small whole items, fillets and cuts. You can grill on the barbecue or under the salamander.

Menu examples:

  • Grilled scallops with lemon oil and Thai basil salad
  • Grilled salmon with saffron potato and carrot foam
  • Grilled prawns with herb butter.

Dry heat cookery methods

Shallow frying: seafood is cooked in a pan using a small amount of fat.

  • This is suitable for whole fish, fish fillets and crustaceans. The seafood is often coated in flour prior to cooking to keep it dry and crisp the skin.

Menu examples:

  • Sole meunière
  • Sea trout in almond butter
  • Dublin Bay prawn tails in garlic butter and herbs.

Dry heat cookery methods

Deep frying: seafood is cooked by submerging it fully in hot fat. Temperatures vary from 140°C to 190°C.

  • Deep frying can be used for whole small fish such as whitebait and sardines, fish fillets and crustaceans. Whole fish are cooked at a lower temperature, depending on thickness, to ensure even cooking all the way through. The food is usually coated with flour, crumbs, batter or other suitable coating. Ensure that the coating covers all of the food.

Menu examples:

  • Whitebait
  • Tempura prawns
  • Chilli squid
  • Deep fried cod in beer batter.

Finishing seafood to dish requirements

  • Finish seafood according to recipe, for example use correct garnish such as fried parsley and lemon, and correct sauce such as brown butter for meunière-style or cold aioli for goujons.
  • Use images of the finished dishes for presentation suggestions.
  • Present seafood to maximise visual impact, for example: with the sauce spooned around or in the centre; seafood arranged appropriately; accompaniments such as rice, noodles or vegetables adding colour or texture; height created and the overall impression is appealing.
  • Sauce can be added as part of the dish or separately in a container, for example tartare sauce.
  • Be sure to check the finished dish before service and confirm standards with the supervisor or head chef.

Seafood accompaniments

  • The most common garnish for seafood is lemon. It can be served as wedges, slices, peeled slices for meunière or lemon halves wrapped in muslin cloth.
  • Cooked or smoked seafood is often served with fresh herbs, micro-leaves, cherry tomatoes and avocado.
  • For cooked seafood, fried parsley or other herbs are very popular. Grilled lime halves or slices can add subtle flavour.

Seafood accompaniments

Classical seafood dishes often have accompaniments and garnishes specifically suited to the dish. Here are some examples:

  • Fried fish can be served with fresh tomato sauce.
  • Fish fillets doria are served with cucumber and nut butter.
  • Deep-fried seafood work with tartare sauce or remoulade.
  • Aioli and rouille are popular French accompaniments for seafood and bouillabaisse.
  • Fried potatoes are standard for fish and chips. Many fried potato types such as parmentier or parisienne are used with pan-fried seafood.

Storing finished seafood dishes

Once service is finished, ensure that the correct storage procedures are followed:

  • Check all items in regards to freshness, smell and appearance. Place any leftover uncooked seafood into containers or back on ice.
  • Put all accompaniments and sauces into containers, cover, label and date, and place in the fridge or coolroom.
  • Store in the appropriate areas in the fridge or coolroom.
  • Always keep raw and cooked items separate.
  • Apply FIFO for the next service.

Handout 3: Fish recipe – complex and modern

Braised fillet of cod, pan-fried scallops, beurre blanc, cucumber, spring herbs and greens

Cod and pan-fried scallops – ingredients

  • 4 cod fillets (150g each)
  • 4 large scallops (80g)
  • 100g unsalted butter
  • Lemon juice
  • Sea salt
  • Black pepper
  • Fish stock

Sauce – ingredients

  • 20g butter
  • 50g shallots, peeled and diced
  • 120g girolles and chanterelles mushrooms
  • 100ml white wine
  • 80ml fish stock
  • 1 tbsp double cream
  • 20g cold unsalted butter, diced

Cod and pan-fried scallops, and sauce – method

Lay the cod fillets on a board. Mix the softened butter with the lemon juice and seasoning, and brush over the fillets.

Preheat the oven to 190°C/gas mark 5. In an ovenproof sauté pan over a medium heat, melt the butter and sweat the shallot for 2 minutes. Add the mushrooms and sweat for a further minute.

Meanwhile, bring the wine to the boil in another pan and boil for 10 seconds. Add the boiled wine, water and some seasoning to the sauté pan and bring to the boil. Sit the turbot fillets on top of the mushrooms and cover with a lid. Cook in the oven for five minutes

Pan fry the scallops in a little butter until golden brown on the outside and still a little raw on the inside.

Transfer the fish and scallops to a small baking tray and set aside to rest for three minutes, then put back in the oven for one minute to keep warm while you finish the sauce and cook the vegetables.

While the fish is resting, add the cream to the pan, bring to the boil and whisk in the butter. It is important to use cold butter, adding little pieces and whisking to create an emulsion

Spring herbs and greens – ingredients

  • 100g baby spinach
  • 1 tsp lemon juice
  • ½ tbsp chopped chives
  • ½ tbsp chopped chervil
  • 20g plum tomato flesh, diced and skinned

Spring herbs and greens – method

Cook the spinach for one minute, then add the cucumber and take off the heat. Check the seasoning and add a little lemon juice. Finish with the chopped herbs and diced tomato.

Place the vegetables in the middle of each warmed large serving bowl.

Cucumber ribbons – ingredients

  • ½ cucumber
  • Sea salt

Cucumber ribbons – method

Peel the cucumber, then cut 12 long thin ribbons, using a mandoline or swivel vegetable peeler, turning as you go, until you are left with the seeds, which can be discarded.

Season lightly with salt and freeze on a tray for 1 hour. Rinse under cold water to defrost and remove the salt.

Garnish – ingredients

  • Microherbs
  • Herb oil

Plating up

Warm a large serving bowl. Place the vegetables in the middle of each bowl, keeping them centred as much as possible. Place three or four ribbons of cucumber, on top of the vegetables. Place the rested cod on top of the vegetables, place the scallops around the dish. Sauce the dish around the fish, garnish with microherbs and a green herb oil. Ensure that when presenting that the colours of the dish are displayed.

Handout 4: Shellfish recipe – complex and modern

Lobster thermidor with pommes purées, green asparagus, samphire and black truffle

Lobster thermidor - ingredients

  • 1 cooked lobster (600g)
  • 100ml Mornay sauce (cheese sauce, with the addition of eggs yolks and cayenne pepper)
  • 50g shallots
  • 50ml white wine
  • 50ml fish stock
  • 5g English mustard
  • 50g parmesan cheese
  • 1 egg
  • 50ml cream
  • 25g butter
  • Parsley (chopped)
  • Salt (pinch)
  • Pepper (pinch)

Lobster thermidor sauce - method

Peel the shallots, cut them into fine dice and cook them in a little butter. Add the white wine, fish stock and the parsley, and reduce by two thirds.

Add the reduction to the Mornay sauce and stir in the mustard.

Lobster thermidor - method

Remove the claws from the lobsters, crack and remove the meat, leaving it whole. Cut the lobsters lengthways into two. Remove and dispense with the gravel bag and intestines. Remove the flesh from the tails and cut it into neat scallops. Clean the shells.

Cook the lobster meat in butter, mixed with a little of the sauce. Reheat the sauce, make a liaison with the egg yolk and add it to the sauce and keep hot but do not reboil.

Pour a little of the sauce into the lobster shells. Add the meat, placing the claw meat into the head cavity. Coat with the remaining sauce, liberally sprinkle with parmesan cheese and colour under a grill for service. Keep warm.

Pommes purées – ingredients

  • 500g potatoes
  • 50g butter
  • 50ml milk
  • Salt (pinch)
  • Pepper (pinch)
  • Nutmeg (pinch)

Pommes purées – method

Wash and peel the potatoes, then put them in a saucepan with salt, cover with cold water and a lid. Bring to the boil and simmer for 20 minutes until cooked.

Drain the potatoes, pass them through a sieve then mix in the butter, nutmeg and seasoning.

Bring the milk to the boil, mix thoroughly into the potatoes and serve.

Green asparagus – ingredients

  • 150g green asparagus
  • 30g salted butter

Green asparagus – method

Trim the woody part off the asparagus, place the stems in boiling salted water for two minutes, remove and drain. Melt the butter and toss the asparagus in butter for 30 seconds.

Samphire – ingredients

  • 100g samphire
  • 20g salted butter

Samphire – method

Melt the butter and toss the samphire in butter for two minutes.

Truffle garnish – ingredients

  • 1 black truffle
  • 1 punnet micro-herbs

Truffle garnish – method

Very thinly slice the black truffle and scatter on the lobster. Then garnish with some micro-herbs.

Plating up

Pipe pommes purées into the centre of a warm plate.

Place the cooked lobster thermidor on the top of the pommes purées - some restaurants serve the head as garnish but this is optional. Pour any remaining sauce around the plate.

Garnish with the warmed samphire and asparagus and place a thin slice of fresh black truffle on top of the dish. (Traditionally this is served with a sprig of parsley but modern styles have used such items as micro-seaherbs.)
