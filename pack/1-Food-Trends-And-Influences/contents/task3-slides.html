Introduction to types of dining experiences

The advent of travel and people’s interest in gastronomy has provided the hospitality industry with a plethora of opportunities to deliver very different dining experiences the world over.

Understanding their similarities and differences allows professional chefs to consider their careers and to focus their training.

The dining experiences also include the different types of meals available and the characteristics of these meals.

Types of dining experiences

There are many different types of dining experiences and a professional chef should clearly understand the similarities and differences between them.

Fine dining

Considered to be the jewel in the crown for gastronomy lovers, these are generally established restaurants with set menus and expensive surroundings. The cost of eating in these restaurants is normally £150+ per person. Chefs tend to be well known (celebrity), and bookings can be difficult to secure. Examples include:

  • Le Gavroche (London)
  • Noma (Copenhagen, moving to Mexico)
  • Le Jules Verne (Paris)
  • Spago (Beverley Hills)

Types of dining experiences

Pubs and gastro pubs

Pub cooking is generally confined to the UK but has established in other countries where there is a British influence. Food is now considered the driving force in this type of experience, whereas it used to be the drink. Within the UK there has been a explosion of this type of eatery, especially by large organisations and chain restaurants.

Gastro pubs are generally pubs providing a fine dining experience in a different surrounding. Many renowned chefs now also run gastro pubs in the UK:

  • The Coach – Tom Kerridge
  • The Flitch of Bacon – Daniel Clifford
  • The Kensington Arms – Josh Eggleton
  • The Scran & Scallie – Tom Kitchin

Types of dining experiences

Bistros/brasseries

In the UK, bistros and brasseries are often referred to as restaurants that provide a continental flavour on their menus. The French use the terms to describe cheap wholesome food. Within the UK this experience will provide a quality product that is not inexpensive.

This type of establishment is generally well decorated, airy and the staff are well trained, both front of house and chefs. Some examples include:

  • The Oak Bistro in Cambridge
  • Oxford Brasserie in Southampton
  • High Road Brasserie in London
  • Langlands Brasserie in Swansea

Types of dining experiences

Chains/branded

Chains and branded dining experiences can crossover into many areas of the hospitality industry. This type of establishment provides clientele with a standardised food product wherever they are served in the country.

In some cases the product is made centrally and the sites used as finishing kitchens. Included in this category are:

  • Hungry Horse
  • Beefeater
  • Table Table
  • Brewers Fayre

Types of dining experiences

Themed experiences

Themed restaurants aim to deliver customers the whole experience. The idea is to provide customers with an establishment where the theme covers everything including the décor, food, music, staff etc.

There are chains of themed restaurants but also individual sites (such as in London). Some examples include:

  • Applebee’s – American Grill Bar
  • Planet Hollywood – Burger bar
  • Turtle Bay – Caribbean
  • Trader Vic’s – South Sea dining
  • Bal Canto – opera themed

Types of dining experiences

Ethnic dining

Ethnic dining covers a vast array of different experiences – from fast food to high-end dining. Restaurants tend to specialise in their own cultural and ethnic dishes. The decor in these establishments can be either complementary or not, unlike a themed restaurant.

Examples of this type of eatery can be found in most towns in the UK and include:

  • Greek
  • Italian
  • Chinese
  • Indian

Types of dining experiences

Fast food

Fast food can best be described as a quick bite to eat. The food is ordered at a computer terminal or counter, collected by the customer, eaten at the table and then their trays are cleared by the customer themselves.

Characteristically the staff are generally young, move on quickly and are trained to do specific tasks. Turnover is high and food is cheap. There are a number of big players in the UK:

  • McDonalds
  • Burger King
  • KFC
  • Subway

Types of dining experiences

Takeaways

Takeaway establishments can be found in all towns and cities and are best described as outlets that provide food to be eaten off the premises. Their overheads are small so the food products are generally cheap.

The experience provided by a takeaway varies greatly as the food is diverse and not generally high quality. Examples include:

  • Fish and chip shops
  • Pizza shops
  • Kebab shops
  • Burger vans
  • Fast food outlets could also be classified as takeaways

Types of dining experiences

Events

Events catering experiences can range from picnics to fine dining. All styles of catering can be used at an event, depending on the event type.

Wedding catering has evolved from a wedding breakfast in a hotel or restaurant to eating BBQ in teepee tents surrounded by guests and family. Characteristically, food at events tend to be expensive, no matter if it is a celebration or a business meeting. Event catering includes:

  • Weddings
  • Sporting events
  • Concerts
  • Other celebrations

Types of dining experiences

In-house catering

In-house catering is generally the term used for organisations that provide a dining experience for their workforce. This provision has two benefits – providing nutrition to the workforce and keeping the workforce on site.

The type of dining experience is generally seen as cafeteria style – customers collect their own food and return their crockery and cutlery after finishing their meals. The food is sometimes subsidised and offers good value for money. This dining experience can be seen at:

  • Factories
  • Schools
  • Hospitals
  • HM Forces bases
  • Prisons

Types of dining experiences

Street food

Street food has become increasingly popular during the past 10 years, especially in UK cities. Abroad, street food can be found in many locations but this could be down to both cultural and climatic conditions.

Street food is meant to be eaten immediately, using fingers or supplied plastic cutlery. It is cheap and often of ethnic origin. Street food vendors might sell:

  • Stir-fry meals
  • Soups
  • Curries
  • Pancakes

Michelin stars

The importance of reviews, guides and awards cannot be underestimated. The general public will take notice of these reputational CVs to decide on the dining experience they want to have.

Michelin stars

The Michelin Red Guide is published annually and only covers certain regions throughout the world. Inspectors with a strict criterion inspect restaurants anonymously but the specific inspection regime is only fully understood by inspectors.

While nobody really knows how to win a Michelin star, people understand the actual meaning of the stars:

  • One star – Very good cooking in its category
  • Two stars – Excellent cooking, worth a detour
  • Three stars – Exceptional cuisine, worthy of a special journey

In the UK, three Michelin stars have been awarded to:

  • Alain Ducasse at the Dorchester
  • Gordon Ramsey – Chelsea
  • The Fat Duck – Bray
  • The Waterside Inn – Bray

Two stars have been awarded to 20 restaurants in the UK. The regions they represent include:

  • London
  • Cambridgeshire
  • Cornwall
  • Devon
  • Perthshire
  • Oxfordshire
  • Durham
  • Wiltshire

One star has been issued to 138 establishments across the UK with 18 new recipients. Restaurants also lose their stars and this can have a dramatic effect on their reputation and therefore turnover.

Michelin stars are not always wanted and the enfant terrible of British Cooking, Marco Pierre White, returned his three stars complaining he was doing more to please the inspectors than himself.
